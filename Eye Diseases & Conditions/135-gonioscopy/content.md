Gonioscopy
Gonioscopy is a specialized eye examination technique used to visualize the anterior chamber
angle—the area where the cornea and iris meet. This angle is critical for the drainage of aqueous
humor (the fluid in the eye). If the angle is too narrow or blocked, it can lead to glaucoma, a
serious eye condition that can result in vision loss.
Since this area cannot be seen with the naked eye or a standard slit-lamp exam, gonioscopy
provides essential insights for diagnosing and managing various forms of glaucoma and other
anterior segment disorders.
Symptoms and Causes Leading to Gonioscopy
Why Is Gonioscopy Performed?
Gonioscopy is not performed as a routine exam for all patients. It’s recommended when a patient
shows symptoms or has risk factors associated with angle-related eye conditions, especially
glaucoma.
Common Symptoms That Prompt Gonioscopy:
Elevated intraocular pressure (IOP)
Vision changes or loss of peripheral vision
Eye pain, especially in low light conditions
Seeing halos around lights
Redness and discomfort in the eyes
Underlying Conditions That May Require Gonioscopy:
Primary open-angle glaucoma
Angle-closure glaucoma (acute or chronic)
Neovascular glaucoma
Trauma-induced angle recession
Congenital angle abnormalities
Tumors or foreign bodies in the anterior chamber

What Happens During Gonioscopy?
Gonioscopy is performed using a goniolens or gonioscope, a special lens placed gently on the
surface of the eye after applying a numbing drop. The lens allows the ophthalmologist to see the
drainage angle and assess its structure and openness.
Steps in the Procedure:
1. Topical anesthetic is applied to prevent discomfort
2. A goniolens is placed directly on the cornea
3. The doctor uses a slit-lamp or microscope to examine the angle
4. The angle is graded based on visibility of angle structures (Schwalbe's line, trabecular
meshwork, scleral spur, and ciliary body)
Other Diagnostic Tests Often Done Alongside Gonioscopy:
Tonometry (measuring IOP)
Visual field testing
Optical coherence tomography (OCT)
Pachymetry (measuring corneal thickness)
Management and Treatment
Interpreting Gonioscopy Results
Open angles: Suggest lower risk for angle-closure glaucoma
Narrow angles: Indicate a risk for acute angle-closure attacks
Closed angles: Require immediate treatment to reduce intraocular pressure
Abnormal structures or pigmentation: May suggest trauma, pigment dispersion
syndrome, or neovascularization
Treatment Based on Findings:
Open-angle glaucoma: Managed with medications, laser trabeculoplasty, or surgery
Angle-closure: Often requires laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI) to relieve pressure
Abnormal angle anatomy: May require close monitoring or surgical correction
Gonioscopy is critical in determining the appropriate course of treatment for each individual.

Gonioscopy in Surgery
Gonioscopy is not a surgical procedure itself but is used during minimally invasive glaucoma
surgeries (MIGS). Surgeons use gonioscopic guidance to implant drainage devices or perform
angle-based procedures such as:
Goniosynechialysis
Trabectome surgery
Goniotomy or ab interno trabeculotomy
Prevention
Gonioscopy is a diagnostic tool, not a preventive measure. However, early use of gonioscopy
can help prevent vision loss by detecting angle abnormalities before symptoms occur.
Preventive Eye Care Tips:
Regular comprehensive eye exams, especially after age 40
Annual screening for individuals with a family history of glaucoma
Immediate evaluation after eye trauma
Routine IOP checks for high-risk individuals
Outlook / Prognosis
Gonioscopy plays a pivotal role in early glaucoma diagnosis and management, often
determining whether intervention is needed to prevent vision loss.
Early detection allows for effective treatment with eye drops, lasers, or surgery
Delayed diagnosis of angle-closure or neovascular changes can lead to irreversible
damage
Repeat gonioscopy may be required periodically to monitor disease progression

When integrated into regular ophthalmic care, gonioscopy significantly improves long-term
outcomes for glaucoma patients.
Living With Gonioscopy Findings
Living with angle-related eye issues means adapting to a management plan that may include:
Daily use of prescribed eye drops
Periodic monitoring with gonioscopy and IOP checks
Lifestyle changes to avoid triggers for angle-closure (e.g., dark environments, stress)
Understanding the signs of a glaucoma flare-up (sudden pain, nausea, vision loss)
Patient education and follow-up care are key to maintaining vision and quality of life.
Additional Common Questions
Is gonioscopy painful?
No. Anesthetic eye drops are used, and the procedure is painless. You might feel mild pressure
from the lens.
How long does it take?
The examination usually takes 5–10 minutes per eye.
Is it done for both eyes?
Yes, both eyes are typically examined, even if only one shows symptoms.
How often should I have a gonioscopy?
As recommended by your eye doctor—commonly once a year or more often if you have
glaucoma or narrow angles.
Are there risks?
Gonioscopy is very safe. Rarely, mild irritation or redness may occur after the test.
FAQs

Q: Can gonioscopy detect all types of glaucoma?
A: Gonioscopy is specifically useful for angle-related glaucoma’s, such as open-angle and angle-
closure glaucoma. It complements other tests like OCT and visual field testing.
Q: Who performs gonioscopy?
A: It is performed by trained ophthalmologists or optometrists specializing in glaucoma and
anterior segment evaluation.
Q: Can it be done after eye surgery?
A: Yes, though it may be deferred for a few weeks post-surgery to allow healing.
Q: What happens if abnormal angles are found?
A: Your doctor may recommend laser treatment (like iridotomy), medications, or surgery to
manage intraocular pressure and protect vision.
