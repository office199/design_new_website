Vitrectomy
Vitrectomy is a delicate eye surgery that involves removing the vitreous humor—the transparent
gel filling the inside of the eyeball between the lens and the retina. This procedure is performed
to treat a range of serious eye conditions affecting the retina or vitreous body, including retinal
detachments, vitreous hemorrhages, macular holes, and infections inside the eye. By removing
the vitreous gel, surgeons gain better access to the retina for repair or removal of scar tissue,
improving vision or preventing further vision loss.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms Suggesting Vitrectomy May Be Needed:
Sudden appearance of numerous floaters (dark spots or cobweb-like shapes in vision)
Flashes of light or shimmering in the visual field
Decreased or blurred vision
A shadow or curtain effect across part of the vision
Persistent vision disturbances following eye trauma or surgery
Noticeable reduction in the clarity of vision
Common Causes Leading to Vitrectomy:
Retinal detachment or tears causing separation of the retina from the eye wall
Vitreous hemorrhage, often from diabetic retinopathy or trauma, leading to blood in the
vitreous gel
Macular hole or epiretinal membrane causing distortion or loss of central vision
Proliferative diabetic retinopathy with traction on the retina
Intraocular infections (endophthalmitis) requiring removal of infected vitreous
Complications following cataract surgery or injury

Diagnosis and Tests
To determine the necessity of vitrectomy, ophthalmologists perform several diagnostic steps:
Comprehensive Eye Exam: Checking vision, pupil responses, and intraocular pressure.
Dilated Fundus Examination: To visualize the retina and vitreous in detail.
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): Non-invasive imaging to detect macular
holes, retinal thickness, or membranes.
Ultrasound B-Scan: Used when the vitreous is opaque, allowing visualization behind it.
Fluorescein Angiography: Imaging blood flow and identifying retinal vessel
abnormalities.
These tests help confirm diagnosis, assess damage extent, and guide surgical planning.
Management and Treatment
Treatment depends on the underlying eye condition but may include:
Observation and Medical Management: In mild cases without urgent complications.
Anti-VEGF or Steroid Injections: For retinal diseases involving abnormal blood
vessels.
Vitrectomy Surgery: Removal of vitreous gel to clear blood, repair retinal tears, or
remove scar tissue.
Laser Photocoagulation: Often used in combination with vitrectomy to seal retinal
breaks.
Vitrectomy is considered when conservative treatments are insufficient or vision is threatened.
Vitrectomy Types & Surgery
Vitrectomy techniques have advanced significantly, with several approaches depending on the
situation:
Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV): The most common method, involving small incisions
behind the iris to insert instruments and remove the vitreous.
Small Gauge Vitrectomy (23, 25, or 27-gauge): Utilizes very fine instruments resulting
in less trauma and quicker recovery.
Combined Surgeries: Vitrectomy may be combined with cataract removal or scleral
buckling for complex retinal detachments.

During surgery, the vitreous gel is replaced temporarily by saline, gas, or silicone oil to support
the retina while healing occurs.
Prevention
While some causes requiring vitrectomy cannot be fully prevented, steps can reduce risk or delay
progression:
Regular comprehensive eye exams, especially for diabetics and those with retinal
disorders.
Strict control of diabetes, blood pressure, and other systemic diseases.
Avoiding eye trauma by wearing protective eyewear during risky activities.
Prompt treatment of eye infections or inflammations.
Early intervention at signs of retinal tears or detachment.
Outlook / Prognosis
The visual outcome after vitrectomy depends heavily on the underlying disease and the timing of
surgery. Early intervention tends to yield better results. While many patients experience
significant vision improvement or stabilization, factors such as the severity of retinal damage and
overall eye health influence prognosis. Some patients may need additional surgeries or
treatments to manage complications.
Living With Vitrectomy
Recovery after vitrectomy varies by individual and procedure complexity:
Vision may be blurry initially but typically improves over weeks to months.
Patients may need to maintain specific head positions postoperatively if gas or oil
tamponades are used.
Eye drops and medications are usually prescribed to reduce inflammation and prevent
infection.
Follow-up visits are essential to monitor healing and detect complications early.
Most patients return to normal daily activities gradually but must protect their eyes and
report any new symptoms promptly.

Additional Common Questions
Q: How long does a vitrectomy procedure last?
A: Usually 1-2 hours, depending on the complexity.
Q: Is vitrectomy painful?
A: No pain during surgery; local or general anesthesia is used. Postoperative discomfort is
generally mild.
Q: What are common risks or complications?
A: Infection, bleeding, cataract progression, retinal detachment recurrence, and elevated
intraocular pressure.
Q: How soon will vision improve?
A: Vision may improve gradually over weeks to months.
Q: Can vitrectomy be repeated?
A: Yes, if necessary due to complications or recurrent problems.
FAQ’s
Q1: Will vitrectomy cure my eye condition?
A1: It treats the immediate cause (e.g., clearing hemorrhage, repairing retina) but ongoing
management of underlying disease is important.
Q2: Are there alternatives to vitrectomy?
A2: Alternatives may exist for milder cases but vitrectomy is often necessary for serious retinal
or vitreous problems.
Q3: Can I drive after vitrectomy?
A3: Usually not immediately; you must wait until vision stabilizes and your doctor clears you.
Q4: What lifestyle changes should I make post-surgery?
A4: Follow your doctor’s advice, protect your eyes, control systemic health issues, and avoid
strenuous activities until cleared.
Q5: Does vitrectomy affect cataracts?
A5: Vitrectomy can accelerate cataract formation, especially in older patients.
