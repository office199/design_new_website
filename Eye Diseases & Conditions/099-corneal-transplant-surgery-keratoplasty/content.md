Corneal Transplant Surgery (Keratoplasty)
Overview
Corneal transplant surgery, also known as keratoplasty, involves replacing a damaged or
diseased cornea with healthy donor tissue. The cornea is the transparent, dome-shaped surface at
the front of the eye responsible for focusing vision. This procedure is performed to:
Restore vision
Relieve eye pain
Improve the appearance of a damaged or scarred cornea
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms Indicating the Need for a Corneal Transplant
Blurred or cloudy vision
Eye pain
Redness or swelling
Sensitivity to light
Excessive tearing
Common Causes
Keratoconus – Progressive thinning and bulging of the cornea
Corneal scarring – From infections (e.g., herpes simplex) or injuries
Fuchs’ dystrophy – A degenerative disorder affecting the inner corneal layer
Previous eye surgeries causing corneal damage
Corneal ulcers or edema (swelling)
Diagnosis and Tests
A comprehensive eye exam by an ophthalmologist is essential. Diagnostic tests may include:
Visual acuity test – Measures the clarity of vision
Corneal topography – Maps the corneal surface curvature
Pachymetry – Measures corneal thickness
Slit-lamp examination – Detailed view of the eye structures
Specular microscopy – Evaluates endothelial cell density
Management and Treatment

<!-- Page 572 -->
Before Surgery
Initial management may include:
Antibiotic or corticosteroid eye drops
Treating underlying infections or inflammation
If these are ineffective, surgery may be indicated.
Post-Surgical Care
Steroid eye drops to prevent rejection
Regular follow-up appointments
Use of protective eyewear
Avoiding physical strain or eye rubbing
Types of Corneal Transplant Surgery
Each type addresses different layers of the cornea:
1. Penetrating Keratoplasty (PK):
o
Full-thickness transplant
o
Suitable for extensive corneal damage
2. Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty (DALK):
o
Partial-thickness transplant
o
Preserves healthy endothelium
3. Descemet's Stripping Endothelial Keratoplasty (DSEK):
o
Replaces posterior corneal layers
o
Used in endothelial dysfunction
4. Descemet Membrane Endothelial Keratoplasty (DMEK):
o
Transplants only the Descemet membrane and endothelium
o
Offers quicker recovery and fewer complications
Duration: Usually 1–2 hours under local or general anesthesia.
Complications and Risks
Potential complications include:
Graft rejection
Infection
Increased intraocular pressure (glaucoma)
Cataracts
Graft detachment or failure

Prompt medical attention is essential if symptoms occur.
Corneal Transplants in Adults vs. Children
Adults
Commonly due to Fuchs’ dystrophy or trauma
Recovery: several months to a year
Lifelong monitoring required
Children
Higher risk of graft rejection and amblyopia
Requires:
o
Early intervention
o
Pediatric-specific anesthesia
o
Intensive postoperative care
Prevention
While not all corneal issues can be avoided, you can reduce risk by:
Wearing protective eyewear
Promptly treating eye infections
Regular eye check-ups
Managing systemic diseases (e.g., diabetes)
Outlook / Prognosis
High success rates with modern techniques
Vision improvement may take time
Some patients may still need glasses or contact lenses
Long-term care ensures graft survival
Living with a Corneal Transplant
Patients should:
Follow medication schedules
Avoid rubbing or putting pressure on the eye
Attend all follow-ups
Report any sudden vision changes or discomfort
Frequently Asked Questions

<!-- Page 574 -->
Q1. Is the surgery painful?
A: No, anesthesia prevents pain during surgery. Mild discomfort is normal afterward.
Q2. How soon can I resume normal activities?
A: Light activities within days; avoid strenuous tasks for several weeks.
Q3. Are donor corneas safe?
A: Yes. Donor tissue is thoroughly screened for infections and stored safely.
Q4. What is graft rejection?
A: It’s an immune response against the donor tissue. Symptoms include redness, pain, and vision
decline.
Q5. Can I have another transplant if the first fails?
A: Yes. Repeat surgeries are possible, though success rates can decrease.
Q6. How do I know if my transplant is successful?
A: Signs include clearer vision and no discomfort. Regular check-ups confirm graft health.
Conclusion
Corneal transplant surgery is a vision-saving intervention that significantly improves quality of
life for individuals with corneal diseases or injuries. With careful follow-up and adherence to
postoperative care, most patients experience successful, lasting results.
