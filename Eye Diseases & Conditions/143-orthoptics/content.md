Orthoptics
Overview
Orthoptics is a specialized field of eye care focused on diagnosing and managing disorders of
eye movements and binocular vision. Orthoptists work closely with ophthalmologists to evaluate
problems like strabismus (eye misalignment), amblyopia (lazy eye), and binocular vision
anomalies. Through exercises, prisms, and sometimes collaboration with surgery, orthoptics aims
to improve eye coordination, depth perception, and visual comfort.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms Addressed by Orthoptics:
Double vision (diplopia)
Eye strain or headaches during reading or near work
Squinting or eye turning (crossed or wandering eyes)
Difficulty with depth perception or 3D vision
Poor eye tracking or focusing abilities
Difficulty reading or concentrating visually
Causes of Eye Movement and Coordination Problems:
Congenital strabismus (present from birth)
Refractive errors causing eye strain
Neurological conditions affecting eye muscles or nerves
Trauma or injury to the eyes or brain

Developmental delays impacting visual processing
Post-surgical eye alignment issues
Diagnosis and Tests
Orthoptists use a range of assessments to diagnose visual and ocular motor dysfunctions:
Visual Acuity Tests: Measuring clarity of vision.
Cover Tests: Identifying eye misalignment.
Hirschberg and Krimsky Tests: Estimating angle of strabismus.
Stereopsis Tests: Checking depth perception.
Eye Movement Recording: Tracking smooth pursuit and saccades.
Prism Cover Test: Quantifying eye deviation.
Refraction: To identify focusing errors.
These tests help determine the type, severity, and cause of binocular vision problems.
Management and Treatment
Orthoptic treatment aims to improve eye alignment, coordination, and binocular vision through:
Eye Exercises: Targeting muscle strength, focusing, and coordination.
Prism Glasses: Helping align images to reduce double vision.
Patching Therapy: For amblyopia to strengthen the weaker eye.
Vision Therapy: A structured program to retrain visual skills.
Referral for Surgery: In cases where muscle surgery is needed for eye alignment.
Treatment plans are tailored based on individual needs and response to therapy.
Orthoptics Types & Surgery
Orthoptic Treatments Include:
Convergence Exercises: For near vision and eye teaming.
Divergence Exercises: For distance vision alignment.
Saccadic and Pursuit Training: To improve eye tracking.
Accommodation Training: Enhancing focusing flexibility.
Surgery:
Surgical correction of eye muscles may be necessary for some types of strabismus not fully
corrected by orthoptic exercises. Orthoptists assist by evaluating pre- and post-operative function
to optimize outcomes.

Prevention
While not all binocular vision disorders can be prevented, early detection and intervention can
reduce complications:
Regular vision screenings in childhood.
Prompt treatment of refractive errors.
Early evaluation for eye turn or suspected amblyopia.
Protective eyewear to prevent trauma.
Monitoring after neurological injuries.
Early orthoptic intervention can improve prognosis and reduce the need for surgery.
Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis for patients undergoing orthoptic care varies by condition and treatment timing:
Many children with amblyopia or mild strabismus respond well to non-surgical therapy.
Adult patients with acquired eye movement disorders may experience symptomatic relief.
Surgical interventions complemented by orthoptic therapy often yield the best functional
results.
Long-term follow-up is important to maintain improvements and manage relapses.
Living With Orthoptic Conditions
Managing orthoptic disorders involves:
Commitment to prescribed therapy and exercises.
Regular follow-ups to monitor progress.
Using prescribed optical aids consistently.
Adaptations in school or work environments if needed.
Awareness that some conditions require lifelong management.
Support from orthoptists and eye care teams helps patients adapt and optimize their vision.
Additional Common Questions
Q: Can orthoptic exercises cure all eye movement problems?
A: Exercises are effective for many but not all cases; some require surgery.
Q: How long does orthoptic therapy take?
A: Duration varies, often weeks to months, depending on severity.

Q: Is orthoptics only for children?
A: No, it benefits all ages with binocular vision disorders.
Q: Are prism glasses permanent?
A: They can be temporary or permanent depending on condition.
Q: Is surgery always necessary for strabismus?
A: Not always; many cases improve with orthoptic treatment alone.
FAQ’s
Q1: What qualifications do orthoptists have?
A1: Orthoptists undergo specialized training and certification in binocular vision disorders.
Q2: Can orthoptics help with reading difficulties?
A2: Yes, by improving eye coordination and tracking skills.
Q3: Are there risks to orthoptic exercises?
A3: Generally, no; exercises are safe and non-invasive.
Q4: How often should I see an orthoptist?
A4: Follow your care plan; typically, weekly to monthly visits during therapy.
Q5: Can orthoptics improve double vision from nerve palsies?
A5: It can help manage symptoms and improve function but depends on nerve recovery.
