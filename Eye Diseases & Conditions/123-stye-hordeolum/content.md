Stye (Hordeolum)
A stye, medically known as a hordeolum, is a common and painful infection of the oil glands in
the eyelid. It appears as a red, swollen bump resembling a pimple or boil, typically near the edge
of the eyelid. Styes result from bacterial infection, often involving Staphylococcus aureus,
leading to inflammation and pus accumulation. Though usually harmless, styes can cause
discomfort and interfere with vision. Proper care and treatment help resolve styes quickly and
prevent complications.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms
Red, tender lump on the eyelid margin
Swelling and pain around the affected area
Increased tearing and eye irritation
Sensitivity to light
Crustiness along the eyelid edges
Feeling of a foreign body or scratchiness in the eye
Sometimes mild blurred vision due to swelling
Causes
Bacterial infection of the eyelid’s oil glands (usually Staphylococcus aureus)
Poor eyelid hygiene

Touching or rubbing eyes with unclean hands
Using expired or contaminated cosmetics
Chronic blepharitis (eyelid inflammation)
Stress or hormonal changes weakening immunity
Underlying conditions like diabetes
Diagnosis and Tests
Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on physical examination of the eyelid. Your doctor will:
Inspect the eyelid for a red, swollen, pus-filled bump
Assess for signs of conjunctivitis or other eye infections
Check for deeper infections or abscesses if symptoms worsen
Rarely, swab samples may be taken for bacterial culture if infection persists or recurs
Management and Treatment
Conservative Care
Apply warm compresses 3-4 times daily for 10-15 minutes to promote drainage
Maintain good eyelid hygiene; clean eyelids gently with mild soap or eyelid scrubs
Avoid squeezing or popping the stye
Use over-the-counter pain relievers for discomfort
Avoid eye makeup and contact lenses until healed
Medical Treatment
Prescription antibiotic ointments or drops if infection spreads or persists
Oral antibiotics in cases of severe infection
Steroid drops may be used to reduce inflammation under supervision
Stye (Hordeolum) Types & Surgery
Types
External Hordeolum: Infection of the glands at the eyelid margin, visible as a bump on
the outer eyelid
Internal Hordeolum: Infection of the Meibomian glands inside the eyelid, causing
deeper swelling

Surgery
Incision and drainage may be needed if the stye forms a persistent abscess that does not
resolve with conservative treatment
Performed under local anesthesia in a clinical setting
Rarely necessary but effective in complicated or recurrent cases
Prevention
Practice regular eyelid hygiene and remove makeup before sleeping
Avoid touching or rubbing eyes with dirty hands
Replace eye cosmetics regularly and avoid sharing them
Manage chronic conditions such as blepharitis or diabetes effectively
Maintain a healthy lifestyle to support immune function
Outlook / Prognosis
Most styes resolve within 7-10 days with conservative care
Prompt treatment reduces the risk of complications like chalazion or cellulitis
Recurrence is possible, especially if hygiene practices are poor or underlying conditions
exist
Long-term prognosis is excellent with proper care
Living With
Continue eyelid hygiene routines to minimize recurrence
Monitor for early signs of styes and treat promptly
Avoid cosmetic or contact lens use during flare-ups
Seek medical advice if swelling spreads, vision changes, or pain worsens
Additional Common Questions
Can a stye affect vision?
Usually no, but severe swelling can temporarily blur vision.
Is a stye contagious?
The infection itself is not highly contagious, but bacteria can spread with direct contact.
Can stress cause styes?
Stress may weaken the immune system, increasing susceptibility.
How long does a stye last?
Typically 1-2 weeks, shorter with treatment.
Can I wear contact lenses with a stye?
It’s best to avoid until fully healed to prevent irritation or spread.
FAQs

Q1: Should I pop a stye?
A: No. Popping can worsen infection and cause complications.
Q2: When should I see a doctor?
A: If the stye persists beyond 2 weeks, worsens, or causes vision problems.
Q3: Are there home remedies for styes?
A: Warm compresses and good hygiene are the safest home remedies.
Q4: Can a stye turn into a chalazion?
A: Yes, untreated styes can develop into chalazia, which are non-infectious cysts.
Q5: Is antibiotic treatment always necessary?
A: Not always. Many styes resolve without antibiotics unless infection is severe.
Styes are common but manageable eyelid infections. With proper care and hygiene, most
individuals recover quickly and can prevent future episodes effectively.
