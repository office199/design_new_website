Macular Hole
A macular hole is a small break or opening in the macula, the central area of the retina
responsible for sharp, detailed central vision. The macula enables you to read, recognize faces,
drive, and perform tasks that require fine visual detail. When a hole forms, it can significantly
impact central vision, causing blurring or distortion.
Macular holes are most often age-related but can also occur due to trauma, high myopia (severe
nearsightedness), or retinal diseases. Early detection and appropriate treatment offer a good
chance of visual improvement.
Symptoms and Causes

Common Symptoms of a Macular Hole
Blurred or distorted central vision (metamorphopsia)
A dark or empty spot in the center of vision
Difficulty reading or seeing fine details
Straight lines appearing wavy
Sudden worsening of vision in one eye
Symptoms typically progress over weeks or months and usually affect one eye at a time.
Causes and Risk Factors
Most macular holes develop due to age-related changes in the vitreous (gel-like substance in the
eye):
Vitreomacular traction: The shrinking vitreous tugs on the macula and forms a hole.
Aging: Most cases occur in people over age 60.
Eye trauma: Injury can damage the retina or macula.
High myopia: Increases the risk of retinal changes.
Macular edema or swelling
Retinal detachment: In some cases, a detachment may lead to macular holes.
Diagnosis and Tests
Clinical Evaluation
A comprehensive eye exam helps diagnose a macular hole. Tests may include:
1. Visual acuity test: Measures how well you can see central details.
2. Amsler grid: A checkered chart used to detect visual distortions.
3. Dilated eye exam: Allows the ophthalmologist to examine the retina and macula.
Key Diagnostic Tool: OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography)
OCT creates high-resolution, cross-sectional images of the retina.
It can show the presence, size, and stage of the macular hole.
OCT also helps monitor healing or response to treatment.
Management and Treatment

<!-- Page 736 -->
Observation (Early Stage Holes)
Small, early-stage macular holes (Stage 1) may close spontaneously.
Your eye doctor may choose to monitor the condition with regular OCT scans.
Surgical Treatment: Vitrectomy
For most full-thickness macular holes (Stage 2 and beyond), vitrectomy surgery is the standard
treatment:
1. Vitrectomy: The vitreous gel is removed to eliminate traction.
2. Internal limiting membrane (ILM) peeling: Helps the retina heal.
3. Gas bubble placement: A temporary gas bubble is inserted to gently press the macula
into place.
4. Face-down positioning: Required for a few days post-surgery to help the hole close.
Most patients experience significant improvement in central vision, especially if the hole is
treated early.
Macular Hole Types & Surgery
Types of Macular Holes
1. Stage 1 (Impending Hole): Vitreous traction; no full-thickness break yet.
2. Stage 2: Small full-thickness hole; typically 250 microns or less.
3. Stage 3: Larger full-thickness hole; no vitreous detachment.
4. Stage 4: Large full-thickness hole with complete vitreous detachment.
Surgical Considerations
Success rate: Over 90% of cases show anatomical closure with surgery.
Risks: Include retinal detachment, cataract formation, or infection, but these are rare with
experienced surgeons.
Reoperation: May be needed if the hole doesn’t close after the first procedure.
Prevention
While you can’t always prevent a macular hole, especially when age-related, certain strategies
may help reduce risk:
Regular eye exams, especially after age 50

Manage chronic conditions like diabetes and high blood pressure
Protect eyes from trauma (wear safety glasses when needed)
Monitor symptoms: Seek prompt attention for visual distortions or central blurring
Avoid excessive eye strain in high myopes (very nearsighted individuals)
Outlook / Prognosis
What to Expect After Diagnosis
Untreated: Macular holes often worsen and can lead to permanent central vision loss.
Treated: Most patients regain a significant portion of their lost vision.
Timing matters: Early surgery leads to better outcomes. Holes present for longer periods
may not recover as well.
Recurrence is rare, especially with proper post-operative care.
Visual recovery may take weeks to months, with gradual improvements in reading and detail
recognition.
Living with a Macular Hole
Before Treatment
Use an Amsler grid at home to monitor for worsening distortion.
Adjust lighting and magnification tools for reading or daily tasks.
Take precautions with the fellow eye to prevent bilateral issues.
After Surgery
Follow all post-op instructions, especially face-down positioning if advised.
Avoid air travel or changes in altitude while the gas bubble is still in the eye.
Use prescribed eye drops to prevent infection or inflammation.
Resume regular check-ups to monitor healing.
Most patients resume normal activities within a few weeks, though vision may continue
improving for months.
Additional Common Questions

Is a macular hole the same as a retinal detachment?
No. A macular hole is a central retinal break, while a retinal detachment involves separation of
the retina from the eye wall—both serious but different conditions.
Can a macular hole close on its own?
Yes, very small or early holes may close without surgery. However, full-thickness holes usually
require surgical repair.
Will my vision return to normal?
Many patients regain significant vision, but it may not return to pre-hole levels. The earlier the
treatment, the better the visual recovery.
Does the gas bubble hurt?
No. You won’t feel the bubble, but it may temporarily blur your vision until it dissolves
naturally.
How long do I need to stay face-down?
Face-down positioning is usually required for 3 to 7 days post-surgery, depending on the
surgeon’s advice and the size of the hole.
FAQs
Q: Can a macular hole come back after surgery?
A: Rarely. Once a macular hole is surgically closed, it usually stays closed.
Q: Are there alternatives to surgery?
A: For early-stage holes, observation may be an option. For most full-thickness holes, surgery is
the only effective treatment.
Q: How is a macular hole different from macular degeneration?
A: Macular holes are mechanical breaks, while macular degeneration involves damage from
aging and deposits. Both affect central vision but have different causes and treatments.
Q: Can I drive with a macular hole?
A: It depends on the severity and which eye is affected. Vision loss in one eye may be
compensated for by the other, but driving safety must be evaluated by an eye doctor.

Q: Will cataract surgery increase my risk of macular hole?
A: In rare cases, vitreous changes after cataract surgery can trigger a macular hole, particularly in
high-risk patients.
