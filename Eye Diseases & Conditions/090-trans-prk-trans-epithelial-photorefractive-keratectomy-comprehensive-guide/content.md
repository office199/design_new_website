Trans PRK (Trans-Epithelial Photorefractive Keratectomy) - Comprehensive Guide
Overview of Trans PRK (Trans-Epithelial Photorefractive Keratectomy)
Trans PRK (Trans-Epithelial Photorefractive Keratectomy) is a modern, advanced laser eye
surgery that offers an alternative to traditional LASIK for correcting refractive vision errors such
as nearsightedness (myopia), farsightedness (hyperopia), and astigmatism. This procedure falls
under the category of surface ablation, meaning that it reshapes the cornea's surface directly,
without creating a corneal flap, making it suitable for patients with thinner corneas or those at a
higher risk of flap complications.
Unlike traditional PRK, which involves manually removing the outer layer of the cornea
(epithelium) before laser treatment, Trans PRK uses a specialized laser to gently remove the
epithelium, leaving the rest of the corneal tissue untouched. This minimizes the risk of infection
and allows for a quicker healing process.
Trans PRK is particularly beneficial for people with active lifestyles, those who are at higher risk
of eye trauma (e.g., athletes), or those who have thin corneas. The procedure typically results in
reduced dependence on glasses or contact lenses and offers long-lasting improvements in vision.

Symptoms and Causes of Refractive Errors Treated by Trans PRK
Trans PRK is designed to address refractive vision errors that result from an imperfectly shaped
cornea, which prevents light from properly focusing on the retina. Common refractive errors
include:
1. Nearsightedness (Myopia):
o
Symptoms: Blurry vision when viewing distant objects, squinting, eyestrain,
headache.
o
Cause: The eye is too long, or the cornea is too curved, causing light to focus in
front of the retina.
2. Farsightedness (Hyperopia):
o
Symptoms: Blurred vision for close objects, eye fatigue, difficulty focusing on
books or mobile screens.
o
Cause: The eye is too short, or the cornea is too flat, resulting in light focusing
behind the retina.
3. Astigmatism:
o
Symptoms: Blurred or distorted vision at all distances, headaches, difficulty
seeing at night.
o
Cause: An irregularly shaped cornea causes light to focus on multiple points in
the eye, resulting in distorted vision.
The underlying causes of these refractive errors are typically genetic, but they can also be
influenced by environmental factors such as aging, injury, or diseases.
Diagnosis and Tests for Trans PRK Candidacy
To determine whether a patient is a suitable candidate for Trans PRK, an ophthalmologist will
conduct a comprehensive eye examination. This includes:
1. Eye Health Assessment: A general eye exam to ensure there are no underlying eye
diseases such as glaucoma or cataracts that could interfere with the procedure.
2. Corneal Thickness Measurement: Since Trans PRK does not involve creating a flap,
the thickness and health of the cornea are critical to ensure the treatment will be effective
and safe.
3. Pupil Dilation: To check the retina and optic nerve for any signs of eye diseases.
4. Refractive Error Test: To determine the degree of nearsightedness, farsightedness, or
astigmatism that needs to be corrected.
5. Corneal Topography: This test creates a map of the cornea's surface, which is essential
for assessing its shape and determining the best treatment plan for the patient.
6. Tear Film Evaluation: A dry eye test to check the quantity and quality of tears, as dry
eyes can affect the healing process after Trans PRK.

These diagnostic tests will help the ophthalmologist assess whether Trans PRK is a suitable
treatment option and ensure that the patient has stable vision and good eye health.
Management and Treatment for Trans PRK
After a successful Trans PRK procedure, patients typically experience an improvement in vision
over the course of several weeks to months. The management and treatment process includes:
1. Post-Surgery Care:
o
Medications: Patients are prescribed anti-inflammatory eye drops, antibiotics,
and lubricating drops to prevent infection, reduce inflammation, and alleviate
dryness.
o
Eye Protection: Protective contact lenses are often placed over the cornea
immediately following surgery to help with the healing process and to protect the
newly exposed corneal surface. These are usually worn for several days.
o
Follow-Up Visits: Patients are required to return for follow-up visits to monitor
healing, check for complications, and assess the progress of vision improvement.
2. Recovery Process:
o
The recovery period for Trans PRK can take several weeks. During the first few
days, patients may experience mild discomfort, blurriness, light sensitivity, and a
sensation of grittiness in the eyes, which should subside as healing progresses.
o
Vision clarity may fluctuate during the first few weeks after surgery as the cornea
heals. Most patients begin to see improvements within 2-4 days, with optimal
vision being achieved in 1-3 months.
Types of Trans PRK and Surgery Techniques
Trans PRK is a specific type of surface ablation surgery that differs from traditional PRK and
LASIK. While there are no distinct “types” of Trans PRK, the surgery can be tailored to the
patient's unique needs through different technological advancements:
1. Conventional Trans PRK: Uses standard laser treatment to reshape the cornea based on
the patient's refractive error.
2. Wavefront-Guided Trans PRK: Utilizes advanced wavefront technology to map the
individual imperfections in the eye, allowing for a more customized and precise
correction. This can lead to better visual outcomes, especially for patients with high-order
aberrations (such as glare or halos).
3. Topography-Guided Trans PRK: This technique uses corneal mapping to guide the
laser treatment, offering a more personalized approach, especially for patients with
irregular corneas or previous corneal surgeries.

Complicated Trans PRK (Trans-Epithelial Photorefractive Keratectomy)
Although Trans PRK is considered safe, there are some potential complications that may arise
during or after the procedure:
1. Dry Eyes: Temporary dryness and irritation are common after Trans PRK. In some cases,
dry eye symptoms may persist for several months and require long-term management
with lubricating eye drops or punctal plugs.
2. Under-Correction or Over-Correction: Although rare, some patients may not achieve
perfect vision right after the procedure. In cases of under-correction, a secondary
enhancement surgery may be required after the eye has fully healed.
3. Haze or Scarring: A small number of patients may experience corneal haze or scarring
during the healing process. This can cause slight vision blurring and may require
additional treatments to resolve.
4. Infection: Although rare, any surgical procedure carries the risk of infection. Patients
must adhere to all post-surgery care instructions to minimize the risk.
5. Fluctuating Vision: Some patients experience fluctuating or unstable vision in the weeks
following surgery, though this generally resolves as the eye heals.
Trans PRK in Adults
Trans PRK is most commonly performed on adults aged 18 and older, who have stable vision
and healthy eyes. It is suitable for people with:
Thin corneas (making LASIK unsuitable).
Active lifestyles or occupations where the risk of eye trauma is higher.
A need for long-term refractive correction.
Adults considering Trans PRK should have a stable refractive prescription for at least one year
prior to surgery to ensure the best outcomes. The procedure is effective for people who want to
reduce or eliminate their dependency on glasses or contact lenses.
Trans PRK in Children
Trans PRK is generally not recommended for children or teenagers because their eyes are still
growing, and their vision prescription may continue to change. Most surgeons will advise
waiting until the age of 18 or older before considering refractive surgery. Additionally, children
and adolescents often have a higher risk of complications from the procedure due to less
predictable eye growth.

Prevention of Vision Problems
While Trans PRK can correct refractive errors, taking steps to prevent or delay the development
of vision problems is always a better approach. These include:
1. Regular Eye Exams: Early detection of refractive errors can lead to corrective measures
like glasses or contact lenses before surgery becomes necessary.
2. Protective Eyewear: Wearing sunglasses with UV protection and using safety goggles
during sports or high-risk activities can prevent eye injuries that may lead to the need for
surgery.
3. Healthy Diet: Nutrients such as vitamin A, omega-3 fatty acids, and antioxidants can
support overall eye health and reduce the risk of age-related vision problems.
Outlook / Prognosis for Trans PRK Patients
The prognosis after Trans PRK is generally excellent, with most patients achieving 20/25 vision
or better, which is sufficient for daily activities like driving and reading. Here’s what to expect:
Vision Recovery: Vision improvement typically begins within a few days, with complete
stabilization of vision usually occurring within 1-3 months.
Long-Term Results: Once the eye has fully healed, the results are typically permanent,
although some individuals may experience slight regression of their vision over time,
especially in cases of high refractive error.
Enhanced Safety: Since Trans PRK does not involve creating a corneal flap, the risk of
flap complications is eliminated, making it a safer choice for some patients.
Living with Trans PRK Results
After undergoing Trans PR
K, most patients enjoy a significant reduction in their reliance on glasses or contact lenses.
However, there are some considerations for living with the results:
1. Follow-Up Care: Regular follow-up visits to ensure proper healing and to monitor the
health of the eyes are crucial.
2. Post-Surgery Adjustments: Patients should avoid rubbing their eyes, exposure to wind
or dust, and excessive screen time for the first few weeks after surgery.
3. Vision Changes Over Time: While the results are generally long-lasting, the natural
aging process may still affect vision, especially in terms of near-vision problems
(presbyopia) as patients age.

Additional Common Questions (FAQs)
1. Is Trans PRK a painful procedure?
o
The procedure itself is relatively painless, as a numbing eye drop is applied.
However, some discomfort may be experienced during the first few days of
recovery.
2. How long does the recovery take after Trans PRK?
o
Recovery usually takes longer than LASIK, with most patients seeing
improvements within 2-4 days, but full recovery and optimal vision may take 1-3
months.
3. Can Trans PRK correct astigmatism?
o
Yes, Trans PRK can effectively treat astigmatism, just as it does for myopia and
hyperopia.
4. What are the risks of Trans PRK?
o
Risks include dry eyes, infection, haze, or under/over-correction. However, these
risks are rare and can be managed with proper post-surgery care.
5. Can Trans PRK treat age-related vision issues?
o
Trans PRK is designed to correct refractive errors in younger patients with stable
vision. For age-related presbyopia, other treatments, like monovision, may be
considered.
Trans PRK offers a safe and effective alternative to LASIK for those looking to improve their
vision and reduce their dependence on corrective lenses. The procedure is suitable for a wide
range of patients, including those with thin corneas or active lifestyles. As with any surgical
procedure, it’s important to consult with an experienced ophthalmologist to determine if Trans
PRK is right for you.
