Floaters
Overview
Eye floaters are tiny shapes or specks that drift across your field of vision. They often appear as
dots, threads, or cobweb-like strands that move when your eyes move and seem to dart away
when you try to look at them directly. Floaters are most noticeable when looking at a bright,
plain background like a blue sky or a white wall. While usually harmless, floaters can sometimes
signal more serious eye conditions.
Symptoms and Causes
Common Symptoms:
Small, shadowy shapes that move with your eye
Spots that drift slowly across vision
Cobweb-like or thread-like structures
Objects that seem to float and then disappear when you try to focus on them
Underlying Causes:
Age-related changes: The vitreous, a gel-like substance in the eye, shrinks and forms
clumps as we age.
Retinal tears or detachment: When the vitreous pulls away too quickly, it may tear the
retina.
Eye injuries or trauma: Physical damage to the eye can cause bleeding or debris,
resulting in floaters.
Inflammation (uveitis): Inflammatory cells in the vitreous can appear as floaters.
Diabetic retinopathy or eye surgery complications may also lead to floaters.
Diagnosis and Tests
If you notice new or increasing floaters, especially with flashes of light, it’s essential to see an
eye doctor promptly. Diagnosis may include:
Dilated eye examination: To inspect the retina and vitreous for damage or detachment.
Optical coherence tomography (OCT): A scan to view the retina in detail.
Ultrasound imaging: If the view of the retina is obstructed due to bleeding.

Management and Treatment
Most floaters don’t require treatment and tend to become less noticeable over time.
Non-surgical Management:
Observation: Regular monitoring during eye exams.
Adapting to floaters: The brain often learns to ignore them over time.
Medical and Surgical Options:
Laser therapy (vitreolysis): Uses a laser to break up floaters and reduce their visual
impact.
Vitrectomy: A surgical procedure where the vitreous is removed and replaced with a
clear solution. This is only recommended in severe cases due to potential risks.
Types & Surgery
Types of Floaters:
Degenerative floaters: Common with aging.
Post-surgical floaters: Following eye surgeries like cataract removal.
Inflammatory floaters: Due to infections or autoimmune conditions.
Hemorrhagic floaters: Caused by bleeding within the eye.
Surgical Approach:
Surgery is considered when floaters significantly impair vision. Vitrectomy offers a permanent
solution but comes with risks such as cataracts or retinal detachment. Laser therapy is a less
invasive alternative but isn’t effective for all types.
Complicated Floaters
When floaters occur alongside sudden vision loss, flashes of light, or a dark curtain over part of
your vision, it may be a sign of:
Retinal detachment
Severe vitreous hemorrhage
Inflammatory eye disease
These require urgent evaluation by an eye specialist.
Floaters in Adults

Floaters are most common in adults over the age of 50. Myopia (nearsightedness), prior eye
surgeries, and systemic conditions like diabetes can increase the likelihood of developing floaters
at a younger age.
Floaters in Children
While rare, floaters in children can be caused by:
Congenital eye conditions
Inflammation (like juvenile idiopathic arthritis)
Trauma or infection
Any visual complaints in children should be assessed promptly to rule out serious issues.
Prevention
Although floaters often can’t be prevented entirely, some steps may reduce risk:
Regular eye exams for early detection
Managing chronic conditions like diabetes and hypertension
Wearing protective eyewear during high-risk activities
Avoiding smoking and maintaining a healthy lifestyle
Outlook / Prognosis
For most people, floaters are a mild inconvenience that lessens with time. However, new or
rapidly changing floaters should never be ignored, as they may signal a retinal problem. With
proper monitoring and care, most individuals maintain good vision.
Living with Floaters
Adapting to floaters involves patience and routine eye care. Strategies include:
Adjusting lighting to reduce contrast
Avoiding staring at bright, plain backgrounds
Seeking reassurance from an eye care professional
Focusing on other visual tasks to help the brain filter them out
Additional Common Questions (FAQs)
Q1: Are floaters dangerous?
A: Most floaters are harmless, but a sudden increase could indicate a serious problem.

Q2: Can floaters go away?
A: They don’t usually disappear, but they often become less noticeable over time.
Q3: When should I see a doctor for floaters?
A: If you experience new floaters, flashes of light, or vision loss, seek immediate medical care.
Q4: Can floaters affect both eyes?
A: Yes, though one eye may be more affected than the other.
Q5: Is surgery safe?
A: Surgery is effective but reserved for severe cases due to the risks involved.
