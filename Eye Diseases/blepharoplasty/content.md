Blepharoplasty
Blepharoplasty, commonly known as eyelid surgery, is a surgical procedure that corrects
drooping or sagging eyelids by removing excess skin, muscle, and sometimes fat. It can be
performed on the upper eyelids, lower eyelids, or both. While often sought for cosmetic reasons
to rejuvenate the appearance of the eyes, blepharoplasty can also be medically necessary when
sagging skin impairs vision.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms Indicating the Need for Blepharoplasty
Drooping upper eyelids that obstruct vision

<!-- Page 685 -->
Puffy appearance due to fat deposits
Loose or sagging skin causing folds or creases
Bags or dark circles under the eyes
Tired or aged appearance
Irritation from excess folds rubbing against the eye
Causes
Aging: Natural loss of skin elasticity and weakening of eyelid muscles
Genetics: Family history of droopy eyelids or under-eye bags
Medical conditions: Such as dermatochalasis (excess eyelid skin) or ptosis
Environmental factors: Sun exposure, smoking, and lifestyle habits that accelerate skin
aging
Diagnosis and Tests
A comprehensive consultation with an ophthalmologist or plastic surgeon typically includes:
Medical history review: Including vision issues and medications
Physical examination: Assessing eyelid structure, skin laxity, and fat deposits
Visual field test (perimetry): To determine if droopy eyelids interfere with vision
Photographs: Taken for planning and comparison purposes
Tear production tests: To ensure the eyes are adequately lubricated for safe surgery
Management and Treatment
Non-Surgical Management (limited role)
Skin-tightening creams and laser therapy for mild sagging
Dermal fillers or Botox® for under-eye puffiness and wrinkles
Lifestyle changes: Better sleep, hydration, UV protection
Surgical Treatment: Blepharoplasty
Blepharoplasty is the definitive treatment. It’s typically an outpatient procedure performed under
local anesthesia with sedation or general anesthesia, depending on the case.
Procedure Steps:
1. Incision made along the natural eyelid crease (upper) or just below the lashes (lower)
2. Excess skin, fat, and muscle are removed or repositioned

<!-- Page 686 -->
3. Incision is closed with fine sutures to minimize scarring
4. Recovery usually takes 1–2 weeks
Blepharoplasty Types & Surgery
Types of Blepharoplasty
Upper Blepharoplasty: Removes excess skin and fat from the upper eyelid
Lower Blepharoplasty: Targets under-eye bags and puffiness
Double Eyelid Surgery: Common in East Asian countries, creates a visible crease in the
upper eyelid
Functional Blepharoplasty: Performed to improve vision rather than appearance
Transconjunctival Blepharoplasty: Internal incision for fat removal, leaves no visible
scar
Prevention
While aging cannot be stopped, certain habits can slow the progression of eyelid changes:
Use sunscreen around the eyes daily
Quit smoking, as it accelerates skin aging
Stay hydrated and maintain a healthy diet
Avoid excessive rubbing of the eyes
Get regular eye exams to detect early functional changes
Outlook / Prognosis
Cosmetic blepharoplasty results in a refreshed, youthful appearance
Functional blepharoplasty can significantly improve peripheral vision and reduce eye
strain
Scarring is minimal and usually hidden in natural eyelid folds
Results are long-lasting, often for 5–10 years or more
Revisions are uncommon but possible if aging continues
Living With

After surgery, expect some bruising and swelling for 1–2 weeks
Use cold compresses, keep head elevated, and avoid strenuous activity during recovery
Protect eyes from sun and wind
Follow up with your surgeon to monitor healing and results
Use lubricating eye drops as recommended to prevent dryness
Additional Common Questions
Is blepharoplasty painful?
Most patients report minimal pain, with discomfort easily managed by medication.
How long does the surgery take?
The procedure usually lasts 1 to 3 hours depending on the complexity.
Will my insurance cover blepharoplasty?
Functional (vision-related) blepharoplasty may be covered; cosmetic procedures typically
are not.
When can I return to work?
Most people return to work in about 7–10 days.
Are the results permanent?
While long-lasting, natural aging may eventually cause new changes in the eyelids.
FAQs
Q1: What are the risks of blepharoplasty?
A: Risks are rare but can include infection, scarring, dry eyes, asymmetry, or blurred vision.
Q2: Who is a good candidate for blepharoplasty?
A: Adults with drooping eyelids that affect vision or appearance, and who are in good overall
health.
Q3: Can blepharoplasty be combined with other procedures?
A: Yes. It’s often done alongside facelifts, brow lifts, or Botox treatments for enhanced results.
Q4: Will I have visible scars?
A: Scars are usually minimal and well-concealed in the eyelid’s natural folds.
Q5: How do I prepare for blepharoplasty?
A: Avoid smoking, stop blood-thinning medications as advised, and arrange for post-surgery
care and transport.
