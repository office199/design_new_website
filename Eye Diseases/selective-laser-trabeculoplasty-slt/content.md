Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT)
Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT) is a modern, non-invasive laser procedure used to treat
open-angle glaucoma, a condition where increased intraocular pressure (IOP) damages the optic
nerve, leading to gradual vision loss. SLT works by using low-energy laser pulses to stimulate
the eye’s natural drainage system (the trabecular meshwork), improving fluid outflow and
lowering IOP.
Unlike traditional surgical interventions, SLT is painless, quick, and repeatable, making it an
increasingly preferred first-line or adjunct treatment for managing glaucoma.

Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms That May Lead to SLT:
Elevated intraocular pressure detected during routine eye exams
No symptoms at first (glaucoma is often called the "silent thief of sight")
Peripheral vision loss in later stages
Eye pain or pressure in advanced cases
Frequent prescription changes in glasses due to vision shifts
Causes of Increased Eye Pressure:
Decreased drainage of aqueous humor through the trabecular meshwork
Age-related changes in eye structure
Genetic predisposition
Long-term corticosteroid use
Other eye diseases or trauma
Ethnicity (more common in African and Hispanic populations)
SLT targets the drainage dysfunction at its root without damaging surrounding tissues.
Diagnosis and Tests
SLT is recommended after a comprehensive glaucoma evaluation by an ophthalmologist.
Diagnostic Tests Include:
Tonometry – Measures intraocular pressure
Gonioscopy – Evaluates the angle where the iris meets the cornea
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) – Visualizes the optic nerve and retinal nerve
fiber layer
Visual field test – Assesses peripheral vision loss
Pachymetry – Measures corneal thickness (can affect IOP readings)
Once open-angle glaucoma is diagnosed and medical management is insufficient or poorly
tolerated, SLT becomes a suitable option.
Management and Treatment

<!-- Page 632 -->
How SLT Works:
SLT uses a 532 nm frequency-doubled Q-switched Nd: YAG laser to target pigmented cells
within the trabecular meshwork. This selective targeting triggers a biological healing response
that improves fluid drainage—without scarring the tissue.
Treatment Procedure:
Performed in-office or outpatient clinic
Takes about 10–15 minutes
Numbing eye drops are used; no injections required
A special contact lens is placed on the eye to focus the laser
No incisions or sutures needed
Post-Treatment:
Anti-inflammatory drops may be prescribed for a few days
Patients typically resume normal activities the same day
Follow-up visit is required within a few weeks
SLT can reduce the need for daily eye drops or be combined with them for enhanced control.
Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT) Types & Surgery
SLT itself is a single type of procedure, distinct from other laser and surgical glaucoma
treatments.
Comparison with Other Glaucoma Interventions:
Argon Laser Trabeculoplasty (ALT): Older, more damaging to tissue; SLT is now
preferred
Micropulse Laser Trabeculoplasty: A newer variation similar to SLT, still under
research
Surgical options like trabeculectomy or shunt implants are reserved for advanced cases
or SLT failure
SLT is not considered surgical in the traditional sense—it’s non-invasive and repeatable if IOP
rises again over time.
Prevention

<!-- Page 633 -->
While SLT treats elevated eye pressure, it doesn't prevent glaucoma. However, early detection
and treatment can prevent vision loss.
Preventive Tips:
Schedule regular eye exams, especially after age 40
Inform your doctor if you have a family history of glaucoma
Avoid prolonged use of steroid eye drops unless prescribed
Monitor other health conditions like diabetes and hypertension
Wear eye protection to prevent trauma
For patients already diagnosed, SLT helps delay or reduce the need for long-term medication
or surgery.
Outlook / Prognosis
SLT has an excellent safety profile and success rate:
Reduces IOP in 70–85% of patients
Effects typically last 1 to 5 years
Can be repeated if the pressure rises again
Safe for both newly diagnosed and medication-resistant cases
Though not a cure, SLT is an effective tool in the long-term management of glaucoma and can
preserve vision when initiated early.
Living With SLT
After the Procedure:
Vision may be slightly blurry for a few hours but returns quickly
Mild discomfort or light sensitivity is possible for a day or two
Return to normal activities immediately unless advised otherwise
Regular IOP monitoring is necessary to track progress
SLT helps many patients reduce or even eliminate their dependence on eye drops, improving
quality of life and medication compliance.
Additional Common Questions About SLT

<!-- Page 634 -->
Is SLT painful?
No. The procedure is painless thanks to numbing drops. You may feel slight pressure or see
flashes of light.
How long does it take to see results?
IOP begins to lower over a few days to a few weeks, depending on individual response.
Can SLT be repeated?
Yes, SLT can safely be repeated if the pressure rises again—unlike older laser methods.
Does it replace eye drops?
For some patients, yes. Others may still need drops but in reduced doses or fewer medications.
Are there side effects?
Most are mild and temporary—like slight redness, eye ache, or light sensitivity. Serious
complications are rare.
FAQs for Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT)
Q1. Who is a good candidate for SLT?
A: Patients with open-angle glaucoma or ocular hypertension who have poor tolerance to
medications, inconsistent compliance, or need better pressure control.
Q2. How is SLT different from ALT?
A: SLT uses lower energy and targets specific pigmented cells, causing less damage and
making it safe to repeat, unlike ALT.
Q3. Will SLT work immediately?
A: Some patients experience pressure drops within a week; full effect is usually seen in 4–6
weeks.
Q4. How many times can SLT be repeated?
A: Typically up to 2–3 times, but effectiveness may reduce with each repeat.
Q5. Does SLT prevent blindness?
A: When used as part of a comprehensive glaucoma care plan, SLT helps preserve vision and
prevent progressive optic nerve damage.
