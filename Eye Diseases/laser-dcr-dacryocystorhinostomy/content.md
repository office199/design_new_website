Laser DCR (Dacryocystorhinostomy)
Laser DCR (Dacryocystorhinostomy) is a minimally invasive surgical procedure designed to
treat nasolacrimal duct obstruction (NLDO), a condition that blocks the normal flow of tears
from the eye into the nose, causing persistent tearing and recurrent eye infections. Unlike
conventional DCR, which requires external incisions, Laser DCR uses laser technology to
create a new tear drainage pathway between the lacrimal sac and nasal cavity through the
nasal passage.
This approach offers quicker recovery, minimal scarring, and reduced postoperative
discomfort, making it a preferred option for selected patients with tear duct obstruction.
Symptoms and Causes
Common Symptoms:
Chronic watery eyes (epiphora)
Frequent eye infections
Swelling near the inner corner of the eye
Mucus or pus discharge from the eye
Pain or pressure around the nose or eyes (in advanced cases)
Underlying Causes:
Age-related narrowing of the tear duct
Congenital blockage (present from birth)
Chronic nasal or sinus infections
Trauma or injury to the facial bones
Inflammatory diseases, such as sarcoidosis or Wegener’s granulomatosis
Tumors or growths obstructing the tear duct system
Scarring from previous surgeries or infections
Diagnosis and Tests
Diagnosis of tear duct obstruction leading to a Laser DCR recommendation typically includes:

<!-- Page 602 -->
Clinical eye exam – Evaluates excessive tearing and signs of infection
Dye disappearance test – Fluorescein dye is used to track tear drainage
Lacrimal irrigation – A saline solution is flushed through the tear ducts to check for
blockages
Dacryocystography (DCG) – A contrast X-ray to visualize the lacrimal drainage system
Nasal endoscopy – Examines the nasal passage to rule out anatomical abnormalities
CT scan or MRI – May be used in complex or recurrent cases to assess deeper
blockages or masses
Management and Treatment
Non-Surgical Options:
In early or mild cases, management may include:
Warm compresses
Topical antibiotics (for infection)
Massage therapy (especially in infants)
Tear duct stenting or balloon dilation in less severe blockages
However, if these approaches fail or symptoms persist, Laser DCR is a recommended surgical
solution.
Laser DCR Procedure:
Performed under local or general anesthesia
A laser probe is inserted through the nose or tear duct
The laser creates a new passageway between the lacrimal sac and nasal cavity
A temporary silicone stent may be placed to keep the new passage open
Procedure typically takes 30–60 minutes and is usually outpatient
Laser DCR Types & Surgery
Types of Laser DCR:
1. Endonasal Laser DCR – Laser is inserted through the nasal cavity with no external
incision
2. Transcanalicular Laser DCR – Laser is passed through the tear duct canal using a
microprobe
3. Hybrid Techniques – Combine endoscopic visualization with laser to enhance precision

Advantages Over Conventional DCR:
No visible scar (external DCR leaves a small facial scar)
Less bleeding during surgery
Shorter recovery time
Lower risk of infection
Faster return to normal activities
However, traditional external DCR may still be recommended in complex or revision cases
where laser access is limited.
Prevention
While not all tear duct blockages can be prevented, some measures can reduce the risk:
Treat sinus and nasal infections promptly
Avoid facial trauma (e.g., wear protective gear during sports)
Practice good eye hygiene
Avoid unnecessary or long-term use of topical eye medications that may cause irritation
Seek early treatment for chronic tearing or discharge
Outlook / Prognosis
Laser DCR has a high success rate, especially in properly selected patients. While the success
rate may be slightly lower than traditional DCR, the reduced recovery time and fewer
complications make it an appealing choice for many.
Most patients experience:
Significant relief from tearing
Fewer eye infections
Improved quality of life
Success depends on accurate diagnosis, surgical technique, and postoperative care.
Living with a Tear Duct Blockage or Post-Surgery
Living with a blocked tear duct can be frustrating and uncomfortable. After Laser DCR:

<!-- Page 604 -->
Follow all postoperative care instructions, including using prescribed nasal sprays or
antibiotics
Avoid blowing your nose forcefully for at least a week
Attend follow-up visits to ensure proper healing and stent removal
Report any signs of recurrence such as tearing, swelling, or infection
Most patients can resume normal activities within a few days, but full healing may take a few
weeks.
Additional Common Questions About Laser DCR
Is Laser DCR safe?
Yes, it is considered safe and effective, with fewer complications and a quicker recovery than
traditional surgery.
Will I have a scar?
No, Laser DCR is performed through the nasal cavity or tear duct, leaving no visible scar.
How soon will my symptoms improve?
Most patients see an improvement in tearing and infection within days to weeks after surgery.
Is the procedure painful?
The procedure is minimally painful, as it is performed under anesthesia. Postoperative
discomfort is usually mild and managed with medication.
Will I need the procedure again?
In rare cases, the passage may close again. Revision surgery or traditional DCR might be
needed.
FAQs for Laser DCR
Q1. How long does the laser DCR procedure take?
A: Typically, around 30 to 60 minutes, depending on the complexity.
Q2. Can Laser DCR be done on both eyes?
A: Yes, but often one eye at a time is treated to allow proper healing and monitoring.
Q3. Will I need glasses or drops after surgery?
A: Glasses are not typically affected. You may be prescribed eye drops or nasal sprays
postoperatively.

Q4. Is it suitable for children?
A: In children, probing or balloon dilation is often attempted first. Laser DCR may be
considered if these methods fail.
Q5. How long do the stents stay in place?
A: Silicone stents are usually removed after 4 to 12 weeks, depending on healing progress.
