OCT-ONH (Optical Coherence Tomography of the Optic Nerve
Head)
OCT-ONH, or Optical Coherence Tomography of the Optic Nerve Head, is a non-invasive
imaging test that provides high-resolution, cross-sectional images of the optic nerve, where
signals from the retina are transmitted to the brain. This advanced diagnostic tool is essential in
evaluating and managing diseases that affect the optic nerve, particularly glaucoma, optic
neuropathies, and neuro-ophthalmic disorders.
By measuring the retinal nerve fiber layer (RNFL) and neuroretinal rim, OCT-ONH enables
early detection of damage, even before visual field loss occurs.

Symptoms and Causes
Why Is OCT-ONH Needed?
OCT-ONH isn’t used to treat symptoms directly but is a diagnostic tool that helps uncover
structural changes in the optic nerve, especially when symptoms suggest optic nerve damage.
Symptoms That May Lead to OCT-ONH Testing:
Gradual or sudden vision loss
Tunnel vision or loss of peripheral sight
Distorted or blurred vision
Difficulty adjusting to dim lighting
Eye pain or pressure (especially in glaucoma)
Color vision changes
Conditions That Require OCT-ONH Imaging:
Glaucoma (open-angle, angle-closure, or secondary)
Optic neuritis (inflammation of the optic nerve)
Ischemic optic neuropathy
Papilledema (optic disc swelling due to intracranial pressure)
Optic atrophy
Inherited optic nerve diseases (e.g., Leber’s hereditary optic neuropathy)
Multiple sclerosis (MS) and other neurodegenerative diseases
Diagnosis and Tests
What Does OCT-ONH Measure?
Optic Disc Topography: Size, shape, and contour of the optic nerve head
Retinal Nerve Fiber Layer (RNFL) Thickness: Key metric for detecting glaucoma
Cup-to-Disc Ratio: Evaluates nerve tissue loss
Neuroretinal Rim: Health of the edge of the optic nerve
Ganglion Cell Complex (GCC): Optional parameter in advanced devices
OCT-ONH Procedure
1. The patient is seated and places their head on a chin rest.
2. The device uses light waves to scan the retina and optic nerve.
3. The scan is quick, painless, and takes just a few minutes.

<!-- Page 722 -->
4. The results are displayed as high-resolution images and graphs.
No dilation is typically needed, and the process is entirely non-contact.
Management and Treatment
How OCT-ONH Helps in Treatment Planning:
Glaucoma: Tracks progression and response to treatment by measuring RNFL thinning
Optic Neuritis: Detects inflammation-related damage and helps in MS monitoring
Papilledema: Assesses swelling and response to intracranial pressure treatments
Optic Atrophy: Monitors the stability or worsening of nerve damage
OCT-ONH as a Monitoring Tool
OCT is not a treatment but a guidance tool—doctors use it alongside visual field tests and
intraocular pressure measurements to adjust:
Medications (e.g., glaucoma drops)
Surgical planning (e.g., need for laser or drainage procedures)
Frequency of monitoring visits
Need for referral to neuro-ophthalmologists
OCT-ONH Types & Clinical Variants
Types of OCT-ONH Scans
1. RNFL Scan (Retinal Nerve Fiber Layer)
o
Most commonly used for glaucoma
o
Measures thickness around the optic nerve in four quadrants
2. ONH Scan (Optic Nerve Head)
o
Measures optic disc shape, cup size, and neuroretinal rim
3. GCC (Ganglion Cell Complex) Analysis
o
Provides early detection of glaucoma and neurodegenerative diseases
4. 3D Imaging and En Face Views
o
Advanced features in newer OCT machines (e.g., swept-source OCT)
Surgical Relevance
While OCT-ONH is not a surgical procedure, its findings directly influence decisions
regarding:

Glaucoma surgeries (e.g., trabeculectomy, MIGS)
Optic nerve decompression in certain neuro-ophthalmic cases
Laser procedures based on structural evaluation
Prevention
Can OCT-ONH Prevent Vision Loss?
Indirectly, yes. OCT-ONH plays a key role in the early detection and ongoing monitoring of
optic nerve diseases before symptoms arise, helping prevent irreversible vision loss.
Preventive Eye Care Tips:
Annual eye exams after age 40
Earlier screening for those with family history of glaucoma or MS
Managing risk factors like diabetes, high blood pressure, and high IOP
Prompt evaluation of any unexplained vision loss or eye pain
Outlook / Prognosis
When used effectively, OCT-ONH allows for:
Early diagnosis of glaucoma before noticeable vision loss
Stable management of optic nerve diseases with regular monitoring
Reduced risk of blindness in high-risk individuals through timely intervention
Improved disease tracking in systemic neurological conditions like MS
OCT-ONH’s ability to visualize structural changes makes it a powerful long-term monitoring
tool with excellent diagnostic accuracy.
Living With OCT-ONH Monitoring
Living with conditions requiring OCT-ONH monitoring (such as glaucoma) involves:
Regular OCT scans, often every 6–12 months depending on the disease
Consistent medication use (e.g., eye drops for glaucoma)
Lifestyle adjustments: Limiting screen time, quitting smoking, protecting eyes
Tracking changes and staying informed about your scan results
Open communication with your eye care provider

Additional Common Questions
Is OCT-ONH safe?
Yes. It’s completely non-invasive, uses no radiation, and has no side effects.
Does the test hurt?
No. It’s painless, quick, and requires no contact with the eye’s inner surface.
Can OCT-ONH detect glaucoma early?
Yes. It can identify RNFL thinning before visual field loss becomes noticeable.
How often should OCT-ONH be repeated?
Typically every 6–12 months, or more frequently if rapid progression is suspected.
Can OCT-ONH be done on children?
Yes, especially if optic nerve conditions or congenital anomalies are suspected.
FAQs
Q: What’s the difference between OCT and OCT-ONH?
A: OCT is a general term for imaging the retina. OCT-ONH focuses specifically on the optic
nerve head and nerve fiber layer, important for glaucoma and optic neuropathies.
Q: Is dilation required for OCT-ONH?
A: Often not needed, but may be used if combined with a broader retinal exam.
Q: Can I drive after an OCT-ONH scan?
A: Yes. Since the test doesn’t impair vision, driving afterward is safe.
Q: Is it covered by insurance?
A: In most cases, yes—especially when used for diagnosing or monitoring medical eye
conditions like glaucoma or optic nerve damage.

Q: Can OCT-ONH show brain tumors or stroke?
A: Not directly, but optic nerve swelling or atrophy seen on OCT may lead to further
neurological investigations.
