Epiretinal Membrane
An Epiretinal Membrane (ERM), also known as macular pucker or cellophane maculopathy, is
a thin, fibrous layer of scar tissue that forms on the surface of the retina, specifically over the
macula—the central area responsible for sharp, detailed vision. This membrane can contract,
causing the retina to wrinkle or distort, leading to visual disturbances. ERM commonly affects
adults over 50 and may develop spontaneously or secondary to other eye conditions.
Symptoms and Causes

<!-- Page 747 -->
Symptoms of Epiretinal Membrane:
Blurred or distorted central vision
Difficulty reading fine print or seeing details clearly
Straight lines appearing wavy or bent (metamorphopsia)
Double vision in one eye (monocular diplopia)
Mild loss of color intensity or contrast
Visual disturbances that often worsen gradually
Causes and Risk Factors:
Age-related changes causing vitreous shrinkage and retinal traction
Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)
Eye inflammation (uveitis)
Retinal tears or detachments
Eye trauma or surgery (e.g., cataract surgery)
Diabetic retinopathy or retinal vascular diseases
Previous retinal laser treatments
In many cases, ERM develops without a clear cause (idiopathic).
Diagnosis and Tests
Diagnosing epiretinal membrane involves a detailed eye examination by an ophthalmologist,
using:
Dilated Fundus Examination: Direct visualization of the retina to identify membrane
presence and retinal distortion.
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): The gold standard imaging technique
providing cross-sectional images of the retina, showing membrane thickness, retinal
folds, and any swelling.
Fluorescein Angiography: Occasionally used to assess retinal blood flow and rule out
other conditions.
Visual Acuity Testing: To assess the extent of vision loss.
OCT is critical in confirming diagnosis, monitoring progression, and planning treatment.
Management and Treatment
Not all epiretinal membranes require treatment. Management depends on symptom severity and
vision impairment:

<!-- Page 748 -->
Observation: If vision is minimally affected, regular monitoring is recommended.
Magnifying Aids and Vision Therapy: May help patients cope with mild visual
distortion.
Surgical Intervention: Considered when vision loss or distortion significantly impacts
daily activities.
Epiretinal Membrane Types & Surgery
Types of Epiretinal Membranes:
Idiopathic ERM: Most common, related to aging.
Secondary ERM: Develops following other eye diseases, inflammation, trauma, or
surgery.
Surgery:
The main treatment for symptomatic ERM is pars plana vitrectomy combined with membrane
peeling. The surgeon removes the vitreous gel and carefully peels the membrane off the retinal
surface, relieving traction and improving retinal contour.
Surgery is generally safe but carries risks such as cataract progression, retinal
detachment, or infection.
Visual improvement can be gradual and may not restore perfect vision.
Timing of surgery is critical; earlier intervention may yield better outcomes.
Prevention
Since many ERMs develop idiopathically or secondary to unavoidable conditions, prevention
focuses on minimizing risk factors:
Regular eye checkups, especially after eye surgery or trauma.
Prompt treatment of eye infections or inflammations.
Careful monitoring and management of systemic diseases like diabetes.
Protective eyewear to avoid eye injuries.
Early treatment of retinal tears or detachments.
Outlook / Prognosis

<!-- Page 749 -->
The prognosis depends on membrane thickness, duration, and retinal damage. Many patients
experience stabilization or mild improvement without surgery. After surgery, most see an
improvement in visual clarity and reduction in distortion, but full recovery may take several
months. Some visual symptoms may persist, especially if the macula is significantly damaged.
Living With
Living with an epiretinal membrane involves:
Adjusting to visual changes, possibly using magnifiers or adaptive devices.
Attending regular ophthalmology follow-ups to monitor progression.
Understanding that while vision may fluctuate, sudden worsening should prompt
immediate medical attention.
Maintaining good systemic health to reduce risk of secondary membrane formation.
Additional Common Questions
Q: Can an epiretinal membrane go away on its own?
A: No, the membrane does not spontaneously disappear but may remain stable without
worsening.
Q: How long does it take to recover after membrane peeling surgery?
A: Visual recovery typically takes weeks to months; some improvement can be slow.
Q: Will surgery restore perfect vision?
A: Surgery improves vision for many but may not completely restore it, especially if damage is
advanced.
Q: Are there non-surgical treatments?
A: Currently, no effective medical treatments exist; observation is standard for mild cases.
FAQ’s
Q1: Is epiretinal membrane common?
A1: It is relatively common in people over 50, with many cases remaining mild.
Q2: Does ERM affect peripheral vision?
A2: Usually, ERM affects central vision, leaving peripheral vision intact.

<!-- Page 750 -->
Q3: Can both eyes be affected?
A3: Yes, bilateral involvement occurs but not always simultaneously.
Q4: Does ERM cause pain?
A4: No, it is painless.
Q5: How often should I get checked if diagnosed with ERM?
A5: Follow your ophthalmologist’s advice, usually every 6 to 12 months if stable.
