Corneal Tattoo
Corneal tattooing is a specialized ophthalmic procedure where pigment is embedded into the
cornea—the transparent front surface of the eye—to improve cosmetic appearance or correct
visual disturbances. Though it is relatively rare, this procedure can be both medically necessary
and aesthetically beneficial for patients with corneal opacities, trauma, or congenital defects.
Historically performed for centuries, corneal tattooing has evolved into a safer and more
effective method, aided by advancements in medical-grade dyes and microsurgical techniques.
Symptoms and Causes

<!-- Page 708 -->
Why is Corneal Tattooing Needed?
Corneal tattoos are typically performed for either cosmetic or therapeutic reasons.
Common Reasons Include:
Disfiguring corneal scars due to trauma, infection, or surgery
Leukomas (white opacities) following corneal disease
Irregular pupil shape or size (e.g., traumatic mydriasis)
Photophobia (light sensitivity) due to iris defects
Congenital eye abnormalities
Blind eyes with an unsightly appearance
Symptoms that May Lead to Corneal Tattooing:
White or grayish discoloration of the eye
Disfigurement or asymmetry compared to the other eye
Cosmetic dissatisfaction
Sensitivity to light
Visual distortion due to corneal irregularities
Diagnosis and Tests
Before recommending corneal tattooing, an ophthalmologist performs a thorough eye
examination, including:
Slit-lamp biomicroscopy: To assess corneal thickness, health, and any underlying
pathology
Visual acuity testing: To determine if any vision remains in the affected eye
Corneal topography or tomography: To map the shape of the cornea
Assessment of ocular history: Previous injuries, infections, or surgeries must be
evaluated
Photography and color matching: To customize the pigment to the patient’s natural eye
color (if cosmetic purposes are intended)
Management and Treatment
Corneal tattooing is performed by a trained ophthalmic surgeon and typically involves:

<!-- Page 709 -->
Procedure Overview:
Done under local anesthesia (topical or injectable)
Pigment is introduced into or onto the corneal stroma (middle layer)
May be done using needles, lamellar pocket techniques, or superficial applications
The procedure can take 30 to 60 minutes depending on the complexity
Post-Procedure Care:
Antibiotic and anti-inflammatory eye drops
Eye shield for protection
Avoid rubbing or touching the eye
Follow-up exams to monitor healing and color retention
Corneal Tattoo Types & Surgery
Types of Corneal Tattooing:
1. Superficial Corneal Tattooing
o
Dye is applied to the outer layer (epithelium)
o
Best for minor discoloration or when vision is already lost
o
Less invasive, but may fade over time
2. Intrastromal Corneal Tattooing
o
Dye is injected or embedded within the stroma
o
Offers more durable and stable results
o
Preferred for deeper or more significant defects
3. Lamellar Pocket Tattooing
o
A thin pocket is created inside the cornea to place the dye
o
Provides controlled pigment placement and reduces risk of surface irregularities
Surgical Considerations:
The eye must be free from active infection or inflammation
Blind or non-seeing eyes are more commonly selected for cosmetic tattooing
Customized pigments may be used to match the iris color or cover defects precisely
Prevention
Corneal tattooing is typically performed after an eye condition has occurred, so preventing the
underlying cause is key:

<!-- Page 710 -->
Use protective eyewear to prevent trauma
Treat corneal infections promptly
Manage conditions like herpes simplex keratitis or corneal dystrophies
Avoid exposure to harmful chemicals or radiation
Regular eye check-ups and prompt treatment of any corneal abnormalities can prevent scarring
and opacities that may require tattooing later.
Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis after corneal tattooing depends on the purpose of the procedure:
Cosmetic outcomes are generally positive, with improved eye appearance and reduced
social or emotional distress
Therapeutic outcomes may reduce light sensitivity or glare
Vision is usually not improved, especially if the tattoo is performed on a blind or severely
damaged eye
Results may fade over time, and repeat procedures might be necessary
Living with a Corneal Tattoo
Most individuals adapt well to life after a corneal tattoo. Post-procedure adjustments may
include:
Regular use of lubricating eye drops
Awareness of possible fading or need for touch-ups
Annual eye exams to monitor eye health
Psychological comfort from cosmetic improvement, especially in visible injuries
For those with vision in the tattooed eye, ongoing assessment is critical to ensure no adverse
effects on sight.
Additional Common Questions
Is corneal tattooing painful?
No. The procedure is performed under local anesthesia, and most patients experience only mild
discomfort afterward.

<!-- Page 711 -->
How long does the tattoo last?
Intrastromal tattoos can last several years or longer. Superficial tattoos may fade faster and
require reapplication.
Can the tattoo be removed?
Not easily. Corneal tattoo removal is complex and may require surgical intervention such as
corneal transplantation.
Is it safe?
When performed by a skilled ophthalmic surgeon, corneal tattooing is generally safe. Risks
include infection, inflammation, or pigment dispersion.
FAQs
Q: Can I get a corneal tattoo for aesthetic purposes only?
A: Yes, some individuals choose corneal tattooing to correct asymmetry or disfigurement,
especially in a blind eye.
Q: Is it covered by insurance?
A: If medically necessary (e.g., to reduce light sensitivity), it might be covered. Cosmetic cases
often are not.
Q: Can both eyes be tattooed?
A: It's rare and typically unnecessary. Usually, only one eye is affected by trauma or scarring.
Q: What colors are available?
A: Pigments can be custom-mixed to match natural eye color or achieve specific cosmetic
results, such as black, blue-gray, green, or brown shades.
