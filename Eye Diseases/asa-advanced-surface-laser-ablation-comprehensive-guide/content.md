ASA (Advanced Surface Laser Ablation) - Comprehensive Guide
Overview of ASA (Advanced Surface Laser Ablation)
Advanced Surface Laser Ablation (ASA) is a modern laser eye surgery designed to treat
refractive vision problems, including nearsightedness (myopia), farsightedness (hyperopia), and
astigmatism. ASA is a surface-based procedure, meaning it involves reshaping the cornea
without the need for creating a corneal flap, as in LASIK.
The procedure is especially beneficial for patients with thin corneas, those at a higher risk for eye
trauma, or individuals who are not suitable candidates for LASIK. ASA is typically

<!-- Page 534 -->
recommended for patients who prefer a non-invasive approach to correct their vision and are
comfortable with a longer recovery time compared to LASIK.
ASA is based on the principles of Photorefractive Keratectomy (PRK) but utilizes advanced laser
technology to achieve more precise and effective results. The laser reshapes the corneal tissue,
improving its focus and helping the light entering the eye to be properly focused on the retina,
leading to clearer vision.
Symptoms and Causes of Refractive Errors Treated by ASA
ASA is most commonly used to treat common refractive vision errors that are caused by the
eye’s inability to focus light properly on the retina. These conditions include:
1. Nearsightedness (Myopia):
o
Symptoms: Blurred vision for distant objects, squinting, headaches, eye strain.
o
Cause: The eye is too long or the cornea is too curved, causing light to focus in
front of the retina.
2. Farsightedness (Hyperopia):
o
Symptoms: Difficulty focusing on nearby objects, eye fatigue, headaches, and
blurry vision.
o
Cause: The eye is too short or the cornea is too flat, causing light to focus behind
the retina.
3. Astigmatism:
o
Symptoms: Blurred or distorted vision at all distances, headaches, trouble seeing
clearly at night.
o
Cause: The cornea or lens is irregularly shaped, causing light to focus on multiple
points instead of one.
Refractive errors are often inherited, but environmental factors such as aging, eye injury, or
disease can also contribute to their development. ASA can help correct these issues, providing
clear and reliable vision without the need for glasses or contact lenses.
Diagnosis and Tests for ASA Candidacy
To determine whether a patient is a suitable candidate for ASA, an eye care specialist will
conduct a thorough eye exam and evaluate the health of the eyes. Some of the key diagnostic
tests include:
1. Refractive Error Test: Determines the degree of myopia, hyperopia, or astigmatism and
helps the doctor assess how much correction is needed.
2. Corneal Mapping: Creates a detailed map of the cornea's surface to check for
irregularities and ensure it’s healthy enough for laser treatment.

<!-- Page 535 -->
3. Pupil Dilation: Used to examine the retina and optic nerve, ensuring no underlying
conditions are present that could affect the procedure.
4. Corneal Thickness Measurement: Since ASA involves surface ablation, the thickness
of the cornea is critical for determining the amount of tissue that can be safely removed.
5. Tear Film Evaluation: Evaluates the quality and quantity of tear production, as dry eyes
can impact healing after surgery.
6. Eye Health Assessment: Ensures that there are no other eye diseases or conditions, such
as glaucoma or cataracts, that could affect the success of the procedure.
These tests will help the ophthalmologist determine if ASA is the best option based on the
patient’s unique needs and eye health.
Management and Treatment of ASA
Post-operative care is essential for ensuring the best outcome following ASA. The management
of ASA involves several key aspects:
1. Medications:
o
Anti-Inflammatory Drops: To reduce swelling and discomfort following
surgery.
o
Antibiotic Drops: To prevent infection and ensure proper healing of the cornea.
o
Lubricating Drops: To keep the eyes hydrated and manage dryness, which is a
common post-operative issue.
2. Protective Lenses: After ASA, patients typically wear a special contact lens for several
days to protect the cornea and aid in healing.
3. Follow-Up Visits: Regular follow-ups are crucial in the first few weeks to ensure the
cornea is healing properly and there are no signs of complications like infection or
scarring.
4. Vision Stabilization: ASA is a surface procedure, and it can take longer than LASIK for
vision to stabilize. Vision may fluctuate during the first few weeks as the eye heals.
Types of ASA and Surgery Techniques
ASA, as an advanced version of PRK, is typically customized to meet the needs of each patient.
The surgery may be classified based on the laser technology used:
1. Conventional ASA: The most common type, where the excimer laser is used to remove
corneal tissue to correct refractive errors. It is effective for treating mild to moderate
myopia, hyperopia, and astigmatism.
2. Wavefront-Guided ASA: Uses advanced wavefront technology to create a personalized
map of the eye, which is used to guide the laser for highly accurate and precise treatment.
This method can correct higher-order aberrations (such as halos or glare), leading to
sharper vision and reduced visual distortions.

3. Topography-Guided ASA: In this technique, corneal mapping is used to guide the laser
treatment. It is ideal for patients with irregular corneas, such as those with previous eye
surgeries or eye injuries, as it offers a more tailored approach to treatment.
Complicated ASA (Advanced Surface Laser Ablation)
Although ASA is generally considered safe, complications can arise in certain cases. Some
potential complications include:
1. Dry Eyes: A common post-operative issue. Some patients may experience persistent dry
eye symptoms that require ongoing treatment with lubricating drops or punctal plugs.
2. Infection: Although rare, infections can occur after ASA. Strict adherence to post-
operative care instructions can help reduce the risk.
3. Corneal Haze: Some patients may develop corneal haze, especially if the corneal
epithelium is not properly protected during healing. This can affect vision clarity but
usually resolves with time or additional treatments like steroids.
4. Under-Correction or Over-Correction: While ASA is a highly effective procedure,
some patients may need enhancement surgery if their vision does not improve to the
desired level. This typically happens in cases of under-correction.
5. Scarring: Though rare, scarring of the cornea can occur if the epithelium heals
improperly or if there is an infection. This can affect vision and may require additional
treatments.
ASA in Adults
ASA is most commonly performed on adults aged 18 and older, especially those who are not
candidates for LASIK due to thin corneas, eye conditions, or previous refractive surgeries. ASA
is suitable for adults who:
Have a stable refractive prescription for at least 12 months.
Have a healthy cornea with no signs of eye disease.
Do not engage in high-contact sports or activities that may put the eyes at risk of injury.
For adults, the recovery process typically takes a few weeks, with vision improving
progressively over time. The procedure is safe and effective, and the results are generally long-
lasting.
ASA in Children
ASA is generally not recommended for children or adolescents, as their eyes are still growing,
and their refractive error may change over time. For younger patients, refractive surgery is

typically postponed until their vision stabilizes. Most ophthalmologists suggest waiting until the
age of 18, or at least until the prescription has been stable for a year, before considering ASA.
In children, glasses or contact lenses are usually the first line of treatment for refractive errors.
Prevention of Vision Problems
While ASA can correct existing refractive errors, taking proactive steps can help prevent or
delay the need for corrective surgery. Some preventive measures include:
1. Regular Eye Exams: Early detection of refractive errors allows for early treatment,
which can help prevent vision deterioration.
2. Protecting Eyes from Injury: Wearing protective eyewear during sports, activities, or
jobs with potential hazards can prevent trauma that may lead to vision issues.
3. Maintaining Eye Health: A diet rich in vitamins, antioxidants, and omega-3 fatty acids
can support overall eye health and reduce the risk of eye diseases.
Outlook / Prognosis for ASA Patients
The prognosis for ASA is generally very good. Most patients experience significant
improvements in their vision, with many achieving 20/25 vision or better. Here’s what you can
expect:
Vision Recovery: Full visual recovery can take several weeks to months. However,
improvements in vision typically begin within a few days to a week.
Long-Term Results: ASA offers lasting results, with many patients achieving stable,
clear vision that eliminates the need for glasses or contact lenses. Some may experience
slight regression over time, especially as they age.
Reduced Risk of Flap Complications: Unlike LASIK, ASA does not involve creating a
flap, which eliminates flap-related complications.
Living with ASA Results
After ASA surgery, most patients enjoy a life free of glasses or contact lenses. However, there
are some factors to consider when living with the results:
1. Ongoing Eye Care: Regular eye exams are necessary to ensure continued eye health and
to monitor for any complications such as dry eye or changes in vision.
2. Vision Maintenance: While ASA corrects

refractive errors, patients may experience age-related changes in vision (such as presbyopia) later
in life, which may require reading glasses.
3. Post-Surgery Adjustments: The first few weeks after surgery may involve some
discomfort or visual fluctuations. Patients are advised to follow all post-operative
instructions to ensure proper healing.
Additional Common Questions (FAQs)
1. Is ASA painful?
o
The procedure itself is not painful as local anesthesia is used, but some discomfort
may be experienced during the recovery period.
2. How long does the recovery take after ASA?
o
The recovery process can take several weeks. Most patients experience significant
improvement within 3-5 days, but full stabilization of vision may take 1-3
months.
3. Can ASA treat astigmatism?
o
Yes, ASA can effectively treat astigmatism, along with myopia and hyperopia.
4. What is the risk of corneal haze after ASA?
o
Corneal haze is a potential but rare complication. It can usually be managed with
medication, and it typically resolves within a few months.
5. Can ASA correct age-related vision changes (presbyopia)?
o
ASA primarily treats refractive errors like myopia, hyperopia, and astigmatism.
For age-related near-vision issues, other treatments or reading glasses may be
required.
ASA is a safe and effective alternative to LASIK for patients seeking a reliable way to improve
their vision. By understanding the procedure, recovery process, and potential complications,
patients can make informed decisions about whether ASA is the right choice for them. Always
consult with an experienced ophthalmologist to ensure the best possible outcome.
