Macular Buckle
Macular buckle is a specialized surgical procedure used to treat complex retinal conditions
affecting the macula—the central part of the retina responsible for sharp, detailed vision.
Primarily, it is employed to manage macular holes and macular detachments often caused by
myopic traction maculopathy (a condition linked to severe nearsightedness). The procedure
involves placing a custom-shaped buckle on the outer wall of the eye to provide structural
support, relieve traction forces, and promote retinal reattachment and healing.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms Indicating Need for Macular Buckle
Sudden or gradual central vision loss or distortion

<!-- Page 643 -->
Blurred or wavy vision in the center of the visual field
Difficulty reading or recognizing faces
Central dark spots or scotomas
Floaters or flashes in the field of vision (in some cases)
Common Causes
High myopia causing elongation of the eyeball and mechanical stress on the retina
Macular holes due to vitreoretinal traction
Macular detachment or schisis (splitting of retinal layers)
Recurrent or persistent retinal detachments after other treatments
Tractional forces from posterior staphyloma (outpouching of the eyeball wall)
These conditions disrupt the delicate architecture of the macula, impairing central vision.
Diagnosis and Tests
Comprehensive diagnostic assessment is crucial before macular buckle surgery:
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): Provides detailed cross-sectional images of
the retina, identifying macular holes, schisis, or detachments.
Fundus Examination: Direct visualization of the retina and macula using slit-lamp
biomicroscopy.
Ultrasound B-scan: Helps evaluate retinal detachment extent and eyeball shape,
especially in highly myopic eyes.
Visual Acuity Test: Measures the sharpness of central vision.
Fluorescein Angiography: Sometimes used to assess retinal blood flow and leakage.
Accurate diagnosis guides surgical planning and prognosis.
Management and Treatment
Macular buckle surgery aims to counteract the abnormal forces pulling on the macula, promoting
retinal reattachment and hole closure.
Performed under local or general anesthesia.
Involves placing a silicone or other biocompatible buckle around the posterior pole of the
eye.
The buckle reshapes and supports the globe, relieving traction on the macula.
May be combined with vitrectomy (removal of vitreous gel) for optimal outcomes.
Postoperative care includes monitoring for complications and supporting healing.
This procedure is typically reserved for cases unresponsive to standard vitreoretinal surgery.

Types of Macular Buckles
Partial Buckle: Covers only the affected macular area.
Circumferential Buckle: Encircles a larger portion of the posterior globe for extensive
support.
Customized Shapes: Tailored to the patient’s eye anatomy for precise traction relief.
Surgical Considerations
Surgery duration varies between 1 to 3 hours depending on complexity.
Intraoperative imaging or indirect ophthalmoscopy assists precise placement.
Postoperative positioning and follow-up are essential for success.
Risks include infection, hemorrhage, increased intraocular pressure, or buckle extrusion.
Prevention
Preventing conditions that may lead to macular buckle surgery largely focuses on:
Regular eye exams, especially in high myopia patients.
Early detection and treatment of retinal tears or detachments.
Protecting eyes from trauma.
Managing systemic diseases such as diabetes and hypertension.
Avoiding activities that strain the eyes excessively in vulnerable individuals.
Early intervention minimizes progression to advanced macular complications.
Outlook / Prognosis
Success rates depend on the underlying condition’s severity and timing of intervention.
Many patients experience improved retinal reattachment and partial to significant vision
recovery.
Visual outcomes vary; some central vision loss may be permanent.
Close postoperative monitoring is essential to address complications early.
Long-term follow-up ensures stable retinal status and preserves vision.
Living With Macular Buckle
Patients may experience some discomfort or foreign body sensation initially.
Vision might take weeks to months to improve.
Adhering to medication regimens and follow-up appointments is critical.
Protective eyewear helps avoid injury to the treated eye.
Lifestyle adjustments may be needed temporarily during recovery.
With appropriate care, many regain functional vision and maintain daily activities.

Additional Common Questions
Is macular buckle surgery painful?
The procedure is done under anesthesia; postoperative discomfort is usually mild and
manageable.
How long is recovery?
Visual improvement may begin within weeks but can continue for several months.
Can the buckle cause complications?
Possible risks include infection, bleeding, increased eye pressure, or buckle displacement.
Is the surgery suitable for all macular detachments?
It is specifically for cases related to tractional myopic maculopathy or complex
detachments not responsive to other treatments.
Will I need glasses after surgery?
Corrective lenses may still be necessary depending on the eye’s refractive status.
FAQs
Q1. What makes macular buckle different from standard retinal surgery?
A: It provides external mechanical support to relieve macular traction, especially in highly
myopic eyes, unlike vitrectomy alone.
Q2. Can macular buckle surgery be repeated?
A: It’s rarely repeated; usually, one well-placed buckle suffices. Revision surgery may be needed
if complications arise.
Q3. What lifestyle changes are required after surgery?
A: Avoid heavy lifting, strenuous activity, and eye trauma during healing; follow doctor’s advice
for positioning.
Q4. How soon should I see my eye doctor after surgery?
A: Follow-ups typically start the day after surgery, then weekly, tapering to monthly as healing
progresses.
Q5. Does macular buckle cure macular holes?
A: It can close holes caused by traction and reattach the retina, improving vision, though some
damage may be irreversible.
Macular buckle surgery offers hope for patients with challenging macular conditions, especially
those linked to severe myopia. With skilled surgical care and proper follow-up, many achieve
anatomical success and improved vision quality.
