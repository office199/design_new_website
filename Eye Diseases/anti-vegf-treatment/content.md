Anti-VEGF Treatment
Anti-VEGF (anti–vascular endothelial growth factor) treatment is a groundbreaking therapy used
to manage and treat diseases that involve abnormal blood vessel growth in the eye. VEGF is
a protein that promotes the formation of new blood vessels. However, in certain eye conditions,
overproduction of VEGF leads to leaky or fragile vessels that can damage vision.
Anti-VEGF medications work by blocking this protein, preventing further damage, reducing
swelling, and, in some cases, improving vision. This therapy has revolutionized the treatment of

<!-- Page 597 -->
retinal diseases such as wet age-related macular degeneration (AMD), diabetic retinopathy,
and retinal vein occlusion.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms That May Indicate a Need for Anti-VEGF Therapy:
Blurred or distorted vision
Sudden vision loss in one eye
Dark spots or shadows in central vision
Difficulty recognizing faces or reading
Straight lines appearing wavy (metamorphopsia)
These symptoms are often related to fluid leakage, swelling, or bleeding in the retina due to
abnormal vessel growth.
Causes Requiring Anti-VEGF Treatment:
Wet Age-Related Macular Degeneration (Wet AMD)
Diabetic Macular Edema (DME)
Diabetic Retinopathy
Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)
Myopic Choroidal Neovascularization (mCNV)
Neovascular Glaucoma (in some cases)
Diagnosis and Tests
Diagnosis is usually made during a comprehensive eye exam, including specialized imaging.
Common tests include:
Dilated Fundus Examination – To inspect the retina and macula
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) – Visualizes cross-sectional images of the
retina, showing fluid, swelling, or abnormal vessels
Fluorescein Angiography (FA) – Uses dye to highlight blood vessel leakage or
neovascularization
Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICG) – For deeper choroidal imaging, particularly in
AMD
Accurate diagnosis is essential to determine the type and extent of vascular damage and the need
for Anti-VEGF treatment.

Management and Treatment
Anti-VEGF medications are administered via injections directly into the eye (intravitreal
injections). While this might sound alarming, the procedure is quick, done under local
anesthesia, and usually well-tolerated.
Common Anti-VEGF Medications:
Ranibizumab (Lucentis)
Bevacizumab (Avastin) – Used off-label
Aflibercept (Eylea)
Brolucizumab (Beovu)
Faricimab (Vabysmo) – Dual-action drug targeting both VEGF and Ang-2
Treatment Process:
Typically starts with monthly injections for 3–6 months
Frequency may reduce based on response (treat-and-extend or PRN approach)
Regular OCT scans track progress and determine next steps
Follow-up is crucial to maintain vision and prevent recurrence
Anti-VEGF Treatment Types & Surgery
Types of Anti-VEGF Treatment:
Standard Intravitreal Injections – Most common and effective method
Sustained-Release Implants – Newer devices that release anti-VEGF drugs over time
(e.g., Port Delivery System)
Combination Therapy – Anti-VEGF used with laser or corticosteroids in complex cases
Surgery:
Surgery is not typically part of anti-VEGF treatment but may be considered when:
There's vitreous hemorrhage
The retina detaches
Other treatments like laser photocoagulation or vitrectomy are needed in advanced
diabetic eye disease

Prevention
While Anti-VEGF therapy addresses existing conditions, preventing the underlying causes is
essential to reduce dependency on treatment:
Control blood pressure, blood sugar, and cholesterol
Avoid smoking – A known risk factor for AMD and vascular diseases
Eat a diet rich in leafy greens and omega-3s
Maintain a healthy weight and exercise regularly
Attend regular eye exams, especially if diabetic or over 50
Outlook / Prognosis
Anti-VEGF therapy has transformed the outlook for conditions that previously led to
irreversible blindness. Many patients:
Stabilize or improve vision within months
Experience reduced retinal swelling
Require fewer injections over time with proper maintenance
Avoid the need for invasive surgeries
However, continued monitoring and timely treatment are essential to preserve long-term vision.
Living with Anti-VEGF Treatment
While the idea of eye injections may seem intimidating, many patients adjust well to the routine.
Living with this treatment involves:
Attending all follow-up appointments
Reporting any sudden changes in vision
Following eye care instructions after each injection
Managing systemic conditions (e.g., diabetes, hypertension)
Understanding that ongoing treatment may be necessary to maintain vision
Additional Common Questions

How long will I need Anti-VEGF injections?
Duration varies by condition. Some need monthly injections for a few months, while others
require ongoing maintenance therapy for years.
Is the injection painful?
Most patients feel pressure, not pain. Numbing drops are used before the procedure.
Are there risks?
Yes, but rare. Risks include infection (endophthalmitis), bleeding, retinal detachment, or
increased eye pressure.
Can both eyes be treated?
Yes, often done on different days or at the same visit, depending on the doctor’s
recommendation.
Do Anti-VEGF drugs cure the condition?
No, but they control the condition and can preserve or improve vision significantly.
FAQs for Anti-VEGF Treatment
Q1. How quickly will I notice improvement in my vision?
A: Some patients see improvement within weeks, while others may take longer. Regular
injections and monitoring are key.
Q2. Are there alternatives to Anti-VEGF?
A: For some conditions, laser treatment or corticosteroid injections may be alternatives, but Anti-
VEGF is the most effective for many retinal diseases.
Q3. Can Anti-VEGF injections cause blindness?
A: Serious complications are extremely rare. When performed properly, Anti-VEGF treatments
help prevent blindness rather than cause it.
Q4. What should I do after the injection?
A: Rest your eyes, avoid rubbing them, and follow your doctor’s aftercare instructions. Report
any pain or vision changes immediately.
Q5. Is it safe during pregnancy?
A: Use during pregnancy is not routinely recommended and should only be considered if the
benefit outweighs the risk. Discuss with your specialist.
