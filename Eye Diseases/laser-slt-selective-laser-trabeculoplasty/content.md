Laser SLT (Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty
Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT) is a modern, non-invasive laser procedure used to treat
open-angle glaucoma by reducing intraocular pressure (IOP). It targets the trabecular
meshwork, the eye’s natural drainage system, enhancing fluid outflow to lower pressure.
SLT is called "selective" because it specifically treats pigmented cells in the trabecular
meshwork without damaging surrounding tissue. It’s a popular alternative or complement to eye
drops and is often used as a first-line or adjunctive treatment.
Symptoms and Causes Leading to SLT
Why Is SLT Performed?
SLT is not used to treat symptoms but to manage the root cause of certain types of glaucoma:
elevated intraocular pressure due to inadequate drainage of aqueous humor.
Common Symptoms That Lead to Diagnosis (and SLT Consideration):
Gradual loss of peripheral (side) vision

<!-- Page 717 -->
Elevated IOP found during routine eye exam
Halos around lights
Eye pain (rare in open-angle glaucoma)
Frequent changes in eyeglass prescription
Causes of Elevated Eye Pressure (Indications for SLT):
Primary open-angle glaucoma
Ocular hypertension (high pressure without optic nerve damage)
Pseudoexfoliative glaucoma
Pigmentary glaucoma
Diagnosis and Tests Before SLT
To determine if SLT is appropriate, a comprehensive glaucoma evaluation is done, including:
Tonometry: Measures eye pressure
Gonioscopy: Ensures the drainage angle is open
Visual field test: Detects any vision loss due to glaucoma
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): Assesses optic nerve health
Slit-lamp exam: General eye health check
Dilated fundus exam: Examines the retina and optic nerve
If medication alone isn't controlling pressure—or if the patient has poor tolerance or adherence—
SLT is considered.
Management and Treatment with SLT
How SLT Works
SLT uses a low-energy, 532 nm green laser
It selectively targets melanin-rich cells in the trabecular meshwork
This stimulates a biological response that improves fluid drainage over time
It does not cause scarring or structural damage
The SLT Procedure: Step-by-Step
1. Topical anesthetic drops are applied
2. A special lens is placed on the eye
3. The ophthalmologist delivers laser pulses to the trabecular meshwork (usually 50–100
spots)
4. The procedure takes about 5–10 minutes per eye
5. Post-procedure, anti-inflammatory drops are prescribed
Most patients can resume normal activities the same day.

Laser SLT Types & Surgical Considerations
While there's only one general form of SLT, its application may vary depending on the eye
condition:
Application Variants:
180-degree SLT: Treats half the drainage angle; may be used in early or mild glaucoma
360-degree SLT: Full treatment; more common and may yield stronger pressure-
lowering effects
Repeat SLT: Can be repeated if IOP rises again, unlike older laser procedures that cause
permanent damage

Prevention
SLT is not a preventive procedure but plays a preventive role in glaucoma progression by
controlling intraocular pressure early and effectively.
Preventive Strategies That Complement SLT:
Routine eye exams (especially after age 40)
Managing systemic conditions like diabetes and hypertension
Avoiding smoking and limiting alcohol
Using protective eyewear to avoid trauma
Adhering to prescribed medications if used alongside SLT
Outlook / Prognosis

<!-- Page 719 -->
SLT is highly effective, with success rates of 70–85% in lowering IOP by 20–30%. For many, it
delays or even eliminates the need for daily glaucoma drops.
Prognosis Details:
Effects usually last 1–5 years
The procedure can be safely repeated
Lower risk of side effects compared to medications or surgery
Best outcomes in patients with pigmented trabecular meshwork
SLT is not a cure for glaucoma but helps preserve vision when done early and monitored
regularly.
Living With SLT Treatment
Most patients resume normal life immediately after SLT. Long-term care still requires:
Periodic pressure checks (every 3–6 months)
Annual or semi-annual OCT and visual field tests
Possible retreatment if pressure rises again
Ongoing communication with your eye doctor to adjust treatment plan as needed
Lifestyle tips: Avoid strenuous activity for 24 hours post-procedure, and continue healthy eye
habits.
Additional Common Questions
Is SLT painful?
No. The procedure is painless due to numbing drops. You might feel a slight pressure or see
flashes of light.
How soon does SLT lower eye pressure?
Intraocular pressure typically drops within a few days to a few weeks post-treatment.
Can SLT be done in both eyes on the same day?
Yes, but some doctors prefer treating one eye at a time to assess the response.
What are the risks of SLT?
SLT is very safe. Minor risks include temporary eye redness, soreness, mild inflammation, or a
brief spike in IOP.

Does SLT replace eye drops?
For some, yes. Others may still need drops but at reduced dosages. Your doctor will decide based
on response.
FAQs
Q: How many times can SLT be repeated?
A: SLT can be safely repeated 1–2 times if the pressure rises again, though effectiveness may
reduce slightly with each session.
Q: Is SLT better than eye drops?
A: It depends on individual needs. SLT offers a drop-free option with fewer side effects and
better adherence, but some may still benefit from or prefer drops.
Q: Is SLT covered by insurance?
A: In most countries, yes—especially when medically necessary for glaucoma management.
Q: Who should not get SLT?
A: Those with closed or very narrow angles, uveitic glaucoma, or inflammatory eye conditions
may not be ideal candidates.
