Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP) - Overview
Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP) is a type of eye surgery that involves the complete
replacement of a damaged or diseased cornea with a donor cornea. The cornea, the clear, dome-
shaped tissue at the front of the eye, plays a crucial role in focusing light, and any damage to it
can lead to vision impairment. PKP is a common surgical treatment for corneal diseases that
cannot be corrected with glasses or contact lenses. The procedure is performed when corneal
transplants are required due to conditions such as corneal scarring, infection, or degeneration.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms of corneal disease that may necessitate PKP include:
Blurred or cloudy vision
Eye pain or discomfort
Light sensitivity
Redness or inflammation of the eye
Decreased vision despite corrective lenses
Causes of corneal damage requiring PKP include:
Corneal scarring: Often caused by injury, infection, or previous surgeries.
Keratoconus: A condition where the cornea thins and bulges outward, causing distorted
vision.
Fuchs' dystrophy: A degenerative disease that affects the inner layer of the cornea.

Corneal edema: Swelling of the cornea, usually due to endothelial dysfunction.
Infectious keratitis: Severe infections that leave the cornea scarred.
Trauma: Injuries that cause deep corneal damage that cannot heal naturally.
Diagnosis and Tests
To determine if Penetrating Keratoplasty is necessary, ophthalmologists perform several
diagnostic tests:
Visual acuity test: Measures how well the patient can see, often with the help of an eye
chart.
Slit-lamp examination: A detailed exam of the eye’s structures, especially the cornea, to
check for damage, scarring, or disease.
Corneal topography: Maps the surface of the cornea to identify abnormalities in shape
and thickness.
Pachymetry: Measures the thickness of the cornea to assess whether it is suitable for
transplant.
Intraocular pressure (IOP) test: Checks the pressure inside the eye to rule out
glaucoma, a common complication after surgery.
Management and Treatment
Treatment for corneal diseases typically begins with non-surgical options, such as:
Prescription glasses or contact lenses: To correct mild refractive errors.
Medications: Eye drops to reduce inflammation, prevent infection, or address other
underlying causes.
Corneal crosslinking: A procedure to strengthen the cornea, primarily used in cases of
keratoconus.
However, when these treatments fail to restore normal vision or the cornea is severely damaged,
Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP) may be recommended.
Types of Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP)
There are two types of corneal transplant surgeries:
1. Full-thickness PKP: This is the standard form where the entire cornea is replaced.
2. Lenticule transplant: A partial transplant that replaces only a portion of the cornea in
specific cases, though it is less common than full-thickness PKP.
Surgery Procedure
During PKP, a surgeon removes the central portion of the diseased cornea and replaces it with a
donor cornea. The donor tissue is sutured into place. The surgery is usually performed under
local anesthesia with sedation to ensure comfort.

The entire procedure may take between one to two hours. The patient may need to remain in the
hospital for observation for a short period post-surgery, and follow-up visits are essential to
monitor healing and detect complications early.
Complicated Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP)
While PKP is generally safe, complications can arise:
Graft rejection: The body’s immune system may attack the transplanted cornea, leading
to vision loss if untreated.
Infection: Any surgery carries a risk of infection, which can lead to graft failure or other
complications.
Increased intraocular pressure: Some patients may develop glaucoma after surgery.
Astigmatism: Changes in corneal shape following surgery can lead to refractive errors.
Graft failure: The transplanted cornea may not function as expected and may need to be
replaced.
Scarring: If there is any infection or inflammation, it can cause scarring in the new graft,
affecting vision.
Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP) in Adults
In adults, PKP is often used for treating conditions like Fuchs' dystrophy, keratoconus, and
corneal scarring. Recovery may take several months, and the risk of complications such as graft
rejection or astigmatism is higher. However, PKP in adults often results in significantly
improved vision and quality of life.
Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP) in Children
PKP is less common in children but is sometimes necessary in cases of congenital corneal
disease, trauma, or infection. Children may recover faster than adults, but they are also at greater
risk of developing complications such as graft rejection. Post-surgical care is particularly
important to ensure the long-term success of the transplant.
Prevention
Preventing corneal diseases that may require PKP involves:
Protecting the eyes from injury by wearing safety glasses during activities that pose a
risk.
Regular eye checkups, particularly for people with conditions like keratoconus or Fuchs'
dystrophy.
Prompt treatment of corneal infections to prevent scarring and permanent damage.
Managing underlying health conditions such as diabetes, which can affect corneal
health.

Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis after PKP surgery depends on several factors, including the patient's age, the
underlying cause of the corneal disease, the presence of other eye conditions, and how well the
transplant "takes." Many patients experience significant improvements in vision, but it can take
up to a year for full recovery. The success rate for PKP is generally high, though long-term
follow-up is necessary to ensure the transplant remains healthy.
Living With Penetrating Keratoplasty
After PKP, patients may need to:
Use prescribed eye drops to reduce inflammation and prevent infection.
Attend regular check-ups to monitor the graft and detect any early signs of rejection or
complications.
Follow the surgeon’s advice regarding physical activity and avoiding situations that
could stress the eye.
Wear glasses or contact lenses post-surgery to correct any remaining refractive errors.
With proper care and monitoring, many patients lead normal, active lives after PKP.
Additional Common Questions (FAQs)
Q1: How long does it take to recover after Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP)?
Recovery can take several months, with significant improvements in vision often occurring
between 6 to 12 months. However, full recovery and stabilization of vision can take up to 2
years.
Q2: Can I return to normal activities after PKP surgery?
Most patients can return to light activities after a few weeks. However, high-impact activities or
those that could put strain on the eyes should be avoided until the eye has fully healed.
Q3: Is there a risk of the corneal graft being rejected?
Yes, graft rejection is possible but can often be treated with medication if detected early. It is
essential to attend all follow-up appointments to monitor for signs of rejection.
Q4: Will I need glasses or contact lenses after PKP surgery?
While PKP can significantly improve vision, some patients may still need glasses or contact
lenses for optimal vision correction, especially if there is any astigmatism or refractive error after
surgery.
Q5: How successful is Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP)?
The success rate for PKP is generally high, with many patients experiencing substantial
improvements in vision. However, success depends on factors like the patient's age, underlying
eye condition, and how well the graft heals.
