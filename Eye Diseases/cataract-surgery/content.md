Cataract Surgery
Cataract surgery is a medical procedure to remove a clouded lens from the eye and replace it
with a clear artificial lens, known as an intraocular lens (IOL). This surgery is one of the most
common and effective procedures worldwide, restoring clear vision for millions of people every
year.
Cataracts develop slowly and may not initially affect vision, but over time, they can significantly
interfere with daily activities such as reading, driving, or recognizing faces.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms of Cataracts
Blurry or cloudy vision
Increased sensitivity to light and glare
Fading or yellowing of colors
Difficulty seeing at night
Double vision in one eye
Frequent changes in eyeglass or contact lens prescription
Causes of Cataracts
Aging – The most common cause
Genetic predisposition

Prolonged UV exposure
Diabetes and other health conditions
Eye injuries or surgeries
Long-term use of corticosteroids
Smoking and excessive alcohol use
Diagnosis and Tests
Diagnosing cataracts involves a comprehensive eye exam by an ophthalmologist or optometrist.
Common tests include:
Visual acuity test: Measures how clearly you see letters at a distance.
Slit-lamp examination: Uses a special microscope to detect abnormalities in the eye.
Retinal exam: After dilating your pupils, the doctor examines the retina for signs of
cataract or other eye problems.
Tonometry: Measures eye pressure, often included to rule out glaucoma.
Management and Treatment
Non-Surgical Management (Early Stage)
Stronger prescription glasses
Improved lighting for reading or tasks
Anti-glare sunglasses
Magnifying lenses
These are only temporary solutions. When cataracts begin to significantly affect vision, surgery
is typically the only effective treatment.
Surgical Treatment
Cataract surgery is performed on an outpatient basis and usually takes about 15–30 minutes per
eye. It involves:
1. Removing the cloudy lens
2. Inserting a clear intraocular lens (IOL)

Types of Cataract Surgery
1. Phacoemulsification (Phaco)
o
Most common technique
o
Uses ultrasound waves to break up the lens, which is then removed through a
small incision
2. Extracapsular Cataract Extraction (ECCE)
o
Involves removing the cloudy core of the lens in one piece
o
Used for very advanced cataracts
3. Femtosecond Laser-Assisted Cataract Surgery (FLACS)
o
Uses laser technology to make incisions and soften the lens
o
Offers more precision and potentially quicker healing
Intraocular Lens (IOL) Options
Monofocal IOLs – Focus at one distance (usually far)
Multifocal IOLs – Provide clear vision at multiple distances
Toric IOLs – Correct astigmatism
Accommodative IOLs – Mimic the eye’s natural focusing ability
Prevention
While cataracts can't always be prevented, some lifestyle changes can reduce the risk or slow
their progression:
Wear UV-protective sunglasses
Quit smoking
Manage diabetes and other chronic conditions
Eat a diet rich in antioxidants (leafy greens, fruits, omega-3s)
Regular eye exams, especially after age 40
Outlook / Prognosis
Cataract surgery has a high success rate, with over 95% of patients experiencing improved
vision. Recovery is generally quick, with most people resuming normal activities within a few
days.
Possible (but rare) complications include:
Eye infection or inflammation
Increased eye pressure

Dislocated lens

Living With Cataracts (Before and After Surgery)
Before Surgery
Monitor vision regularly
Adjust lighting and use aids like magnifiers
Avoid driving at night if glare is severe
After Surgery
Use prescribed eye drops to prevent infection
Avoid rubbing the eyes or heavy lifting
Wear sunglasses outdoors
Attend all follow-up appointments
Additional Common Questions
Is cataract surgery painful?
No. The surgery is performed under local anesthesia or eye drops, and patients usually feel only
mild pressure.
How long does recovery take?
Most people experience significant improvement within a few days. Full recovery can take up to
4–6 weeks.
Can cataracts come back?
The cataract itself doesn’t return, but a condition called “secondary cataract” can develop. This is
treatable with a quick laser procedure (YAG laser capsulotomy).
Will I still need glasses after surgery?
That depends on the type of IOL chosen. Monofocal lenses typically require reading glasses,
while multifocal or toric lenses reduce dependence on glasses.

FAQs
Q: Is cataract surgery safe for seniors?
A: Yes. Cataract surgery is commonly performed on older adults and is considered very safe
with proper care.
Q: Can both eyes be treated at the same time?
A: Usually, one eye is treated first. The second eye is operated on after the first has healed,
usually within a few weeks.
Q: How do I know when it’s time for surgery?
A: When cataracts interfere with daily activities or quality of life, surgery is typically
recommended.
Q: What happens if I delay cataract surgery?
A: Cataracts will continue to worsen, potentially leading to complete vision loss. Advanced
cataracts may also make surgery more complex.
