DESAK - Overview
Deep Anterior Sclerokeratoplasty (DESAK) is an advanced surgical technique designed to
treat severe corneal diseases that affect both the cornea and the sclera (the white part of the eye).
This procedure is often used for patients with sclerocorneal disorders, such as advanced
keratoconus, corneal scarring, and certain types of ocular trauma that involve both the cornea
and the sclera. Unlike traditional corneal transplants that involve only the cornea, DESAK is
more comprehensive, as it addresses both the outer and deeper layers of the cornea and its
surrounding tissue.

By replacing the damaged corneal and scleral tissue with healthy donor tissue, DESAK can
restore both the structure and function of the eye, significantly improving vision and eye health.
This procedure has gained recognition for its effectiveness in cases where corneal transplants
alone would not provide the required benefits due to the involvement of the sclera.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms that might indicate the need for DESAK surgery include:
Severe visual impairment: This can result from progressive corneal diseases like
keratoconus, where the cornea becomes irregular and causes blurry or distorted vision.
Eye pain or discomfort: In cases of ocular trauma or infection involving both the cornea
and sclera, patients may experience persistent pain and discomfort.
Increased sensitivity to light (photophobia): Damage to both the cornea and sclera can
result in significant sensitivity to light.
Corneal thinning or bulging: Progressive thinning or abnormal bulging of the cornea,
commonly seen in conditions like keratoconus, may require DESAK when accompanied
by scleral involvement.
Frequent changes in prescription: This is especially common in patients with
conditions like keratoconus, where refractive errors increase over time and cannot be
corrected with traditional eyewear or contact lenses.
Common causes for DESAK include:
Keratoconus: A progressive thinning of the cornea that causes it to bulge into a cone
shape, leading to vision impairment. In advanced stages, it can involve the sclera,
necessitating a combined corneal-scleral transplant.
Corneal scarring: This can result from trauma, infection, or previous surgeries, causing
both corneal and scleral damage.
Corneal dystrophies: Genetic conditions such as lattice dystrophy or granular
dystrophy, which affect the corneal layers and may involve the surrounding sclera in
severe cases.
Ocular trauma: Accidents that cause deep injury to both the cornea and sclera, leading
to combined tissue damage that requires surgical repair.
Post-surgical complications: After surgeries like cataract removal, scarring and changes
in the corneal and scleral structure can sometimes require a procedure like DESAK.
Diagnosis and Tests
To diagnose conditions requiring DESAK, an eye specialist will conduct a series of tests:
Slit-lamp examination: This is a comprehensive eye exam using a special microscope
that allows the doctor to inspect the cornea, sclera, and other eye structures in detail. The
presence of thinning, scarring, or irregularities is carefully assessed.

Corneal topography: This test provides a detailed map of the cornea’s surface, helping
the surgeon identify irregularities in curvature and thickness, which are critical for
planning DESAK.
Pachymetry: This test measures the thickness of the cornea to assess the degree of
corneal thinning and determine whether DESAK is a suitable treatment.
Ocular coherence tomography (OCT): OCT is an imaging technique that allows for a
detailed cross-sectional view of the cornea and sclera, enabling the surgeon to assess the
depth and extent of damage.
Specular microscopy: This test is used to examine the corneal endothelium and measure
the density and health of endothelial cells, which may also be impacted by the disease.
Management and Treatment
Before proceeding with DESAK, the eye specialist may attempt non-surgical treatments to
manage symptoms and slow disease progression:
Contact lenses: In cases like keratoconus, specialized lenses such as rigid gas-permeable
(RGP) lenses or scleral lenses can help manage vision until surgery is needed.
Hypertonic saline drops: These are used to draw fluid out of the cornea and reduce
swelling in cases of corneal edema.
Medications: Anti-inflammatory drops or antibiotics may be prescribed if there’s an
underlying infection or inflammation affecting the cornea and sclera.
Corneal crosslinking: This treatment can be used to strengthen the cornea in conditions
like keratoconus, though it may not be effective if the sclera is also affected.
If these measures do not adequately address the condition, DESAK becomes the treatment of
choice, as it addresses both the cornea and sclera for long-term vision restoration.
DESAK Types & Surgery
Types of DESAK surgery include:
1. Standard DESAK: This involves the transplantation of both the corneal layers and the
outer scleral tissue. It is the most common type used when both the cornea and sclera are
damaged, such as in advanced cases of keratoconus or trauma.
2. Selective DESAK: In this approach, only the corneal portion of the damaged tissue is
replaced, while leaving the scleral tissue intact if it is still healthy. This is suitable for
patients where scleral involvement is minimal.
3. Double-barrel DESAK: A more complex procedure in which both the cornea and sclera
are transplanted with two different donor tissues. This is done when there is severe
combined damage to both tissues.
The procedure itself involves the following steps:
Preparation of the recipient eye: The surgeon removes the diseased layers of the cornea
and the damaged portion of the sclera.

Donor tissue preparation: The donor tissue, including both corneal and scleral layers, is
prepared and fitted to the recipient eye.
Transplantation: The donor tissue is carefully placed into the recipient's eye, with the
surgeon ensuring that the edges of the graft align perfectly with the surrounding tissue.
Suturing: The donor tissue is securely sutured in place, and any excess tissue is trimmed.
Postoperative care: The patient will need to follow specific aftercare instructions,
including the use of eye drops, pain management, and avoiding physical strain on the eye
to facilitate healing.
Complicated DESAK
Like any surgery, DESAK carries certain risks and potential complications:
Graft rejection: Although less likely in the case of DESAK compared to full-thickness
transplants, rejection is still possible, particularly if the immune system recognizes the
donor tissue as foreign.
Infection: Postoperative infection can lead to graft failure or serious complications,
requiring immediate medical intervention.
Graft dislocation: The transplanted tissue may shift or become displaced, requiring a
secondary surgery to reposition the graft.
Increased intraocular pressure (IOP): After surgery, elevated eye pressure can occur,
potentially leading to glaucoma if not properly managed.
Vision irregularities: Astigmatism or other refractive errors may occur due to changes in
the shape of the cornea or sclera post-surgery.
Corneal edema: In rare cases, fluid accumulation in the cornea can interfere with
healing, causing blurry vision.
DESAK in Adults
DESAK is most commonly performed in adults who have advanced keratoconus, traumatic
corneal and scleral injuries, or corneal dystrophies that have affected both the cornea and sclera.
For these individuals, DESAK provides a highly effective way to restore vision, reduce pain, and
prevent further deterioration of the eye's structure. The success rates for DESAK in adults are
generally high, with most patients experiencing significant improvements in vision and quality of
life post-surgery.
DESAK in Children
While DESAK is less frequently performed in children, it may be indicated for young patients
with congenital corneal dystrophies, severe keratoconus, or other conditions that affect both the
cornea and sclera. Special care is needed when performing this surgery on children, as the eye is
still developing, and the growth of the cornea and sclera must be considered. Pediatric patients
typically require longer follow-up and monitoring for potential complications.

Prevention
Preventing the need for DESAK involves:
Early detection of corneal and scleral diseases, especially for conditions like
keratoconus, through routine eye exams. Early treatment can delay the need for surgery.
Protective eyewear: For those at risk of trauma to the eye (e.g., athletes), wearing
protective eyewear can prevent damage to the cornea and sclera.
Timely treatment of infections: Managing corneal infections or inflammations promptly
can reduce the risk of permanent damage to both the cornea and sclera.
Managing underlying health conditions: Treating conditions that might exacerbate
corneal disease (e.g., atopy, allergies) can help prevent worsening of symptoms.
Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis after DESAK surgery is generally excellent, with many patients experiencing
significant improvements in vision, especially when both the cornea and sclera are repaired. The
procedure's success depends on factors like the underlying condition being treated, the patient's
age, and adherence to postoperative care. In cases where DESAK is successful, patients often
regain clear vision and enjoy improved eye health for many years, with a relatively low risk of
graft rejection or other complications.
Living With DESAK
After undergoing DESAK surgery, patients will need to:
Follow prescribed aftercare: This may include using antibiotics, anti-inflammatory eye
drops, and monitoring the eye for any signs of complications.
Avoid eye strain or injury: Protective measures should be taken to prevent trauma to the
healing eye, especially in the early stages after surgery.
Regular follow-up visits: Ongoing monitoring is crucial to assess the success of the
transplant and detect any potential issues early.
Gradual resumption of activities: While most patients can return to light activities soon
after surgery, more strenuous activities, especially those involving risk to the eye, should
be avoided for several months.
Additional Common Questions (FAQs)
Q1: How long does it take to recover from DESAK surgery?
Recovery from DESAK typically takes several months, with full healing expected within 6-12
months. Visual improvement is usually noticed within 2-3 months, but it can take longer for
complete recovery.
Q2: Is DESAK suitable for everyone?
DESAK is best suited for patients with advanced corneal and scleral damage that cannot be

treated with standard corneal transplant procedures. It may not be appropriate for those with
damage to the deeper corneal layers or extensive endothelial dysfunction.
Q3: Will I need glasses or contact lenses after DESAK surgery?
Most patients will still need glasses or contact lenses after DESAK to correct for any refractive
errors that may remain. However, the improved structure of the eye often leads to better vision
than before surgery.
Q4: What are the risks of DESAK surgery?
The risks include graft rejection, infection, elevated intraocular pressure, and dislocation of the
graft. However, these risks are relatively low with proper postoperative care.
Q5: Can both eyes be treated with DESAK at the same time?
It is possible to treat both eyes with DESAK simultaneously, but it is typically done one eye at a
time to reduce the risk of complications and allow for better healing.
