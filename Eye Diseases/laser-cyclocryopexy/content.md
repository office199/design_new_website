Laser Cyclocryopexy
Laser Cyclocryopexy (or Cyclodestructive Procedure) is a treatment used to lower
intraocular pressure (IOP) in patients with refractory glaucoma—a type of glaucoma that
does not respond well to conventional treatments like eye drops, oral medications, or standard
laser procedures. This technique works by damaging part of the ciliary body, the structure that
produces aqueous humor (the eye’s fluid), thereby reducing fluid production and pressure inside
the eye.
Laser cyclocryopexy is generally reserved for advanced or painful glaucoma cases, especially
when vision has already been significantly compromised and other treatment options have failed
or are unsuitable.
Symptoms and Causes
When Is Laser Cyclocryopexy Considered?
This procedure is not for early-stage glaucoma but is used for advanced, uncontrolled, or
painful glaucoma.
Symptoms Indicating the Need for Cyclocryopexy:
Persistently high intraocular pressure (IOP) despite maximal medical therapy
Severe eye pain, especially in blind or non-seeing eyes
Redness and inflammation associated with uncontrolled glaucoma
Loss of vision or progressive optic nerve damage
Previous failed glaucoma surgeries (e.g., trabeculectomy or tube shunt)
Common Causes Leading to Refractory Glaucoma:
Neovascular glaucoma (due to diabetes or retinal vein occlusion)

Uveitic glaucoma (inflammatory)
Congenital or developmental glaucoma
Post-surgical or post-traumatic glaucoma
Aphakic/pseudophakic glaucoma (after cataract surgery)
Diagnosis and Tests
Pre-Treatment Evaluation
Before laser cyclocryopexy is performed, the ophthalmologist will conduct a thorough diagnostic
evaluation to determine if it’s appropriate:
Tonometry: To measure eye pressure
Slit-lamp exam: To assess the health of the eye
Fundus examination: To evaluate optic nerve status
Ultrasound biomicroscopy (UBM): To visualize the ciliary body
Visual acuity and field testing (if applicable)
OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography): To assess optic nerve and retina if some
vision remains
Patients with poor visual potential or severe pain are often prioritized for this treatment.
Management and Treatment
How Laser Cyclocryopexy Works
The procedure involves applying a freezing laser (cryotherapy) or thermal laser energy to the
ciliary body to partially destroy the tissue that produces aqueous humor.
Procedure Steps:
1. Performed under local or general anesthesia
2. A cryoprobe or diode laser probe is applied to the sclera (outer eye wall)
3. The surgeon targets specific regions of the ciliary body
4. Freezing or heating causes tissue damage and scarring, which reduces fluid production
5. The procedure takes about 20–30 minutes
Post-Treatment Care:
Topical antibiotics and steroid eye drops
Pain management (mild discomfort for a few days is common)
Follow-up IOP measurements
Regular eye pressure checks to assess effectiveness

Laser Cyclocryopexy Types & Surgical Techniques
Types of Cyclodestructive Procedures:
1. Transscleral Cyclophotocoagulation (TSCPC) – Laser (diode) is applied through the
sclera
2. Endoscopic Cyclophotocoagulation (ECP) – Laser applied from inside the eye during
surgery (less common in cyclocryopexy cases)
3. Cyclocryotherapy – Cryoprobe freezes the ciliary body externally
4. Micropulse Cyclophotocoagulation (MP-TSCPC) – Newer, gentler laser that
minimizes collateral damage

Prevention
While laser cyclocryopexy cannot prevent glaucoma, preventing progression to a stage where
it’s needed is possible through:
Early detection via routine eye exams
Strict compliance with prescribed glaucoma medications
Lifestyle changes: avoiding smoking, managing diabetes/hypertension
Regular IOP monitoring and optic nerve evaluations
Timely glaucoma surgeries before damage becomes irreversible
Outlook / Prognosis
Success Rate
Laser cyclocryopexy effectively reduces IOP in 60–80% of patients, particularly those with:
End-stage glaucoma
No remaining vision but ongoing eye pain
Failed previous treatments

Limitations and Risks:
Not suitable for early or moderate glaucoma
Vision may worsen if some sight remains
Risks include inflammation, hypotony (too-low pressure), or phthisis bulbi (shrunken eye
in rare cases)
Still, for pain relief and IOP control, it offers significant benefit when other options are
exhausted.
Living With Laser Cyclocryopexy
Patients who undergo cyclocryopexy should:
Attend regular follow-up visits to monitor eye pressure
Use prescribed medications post-procedure
Expect gradual pressure lowering, sometimes over weeks
Be aware of changes in vision or eye comfort
Continue monitoring the fellow eye if only one was treated
Most patients report significant relief from pain and stabilized eye pressure, which improves
quality of life.
Additional Common Questions
Is laser cyclocryopexy painful?
No. It is performed under local or general anesthesia. Post-procedure discomfort is usually mild
and temporary.
How long does the procedure take?
About 20–30 minutes per eye, often done on an outpatient basis.
Can the procedure restore vision?
No. It is not intended to improve vision but to relieve pain and reduce pressure in severely
damaged or blind eyes.
Is repeat treatment possible?
Yes. Some patients may require repeat cyclocryopexy if pressure remains high.

<!-- Page 729 -->
What are the risks?
Potential risks include eye inflammation, over-reduction of pressure, or, rarely, atrophy of the
eyeball.
FAQs
Q: Who is a candidate for laser cyclocryopexy?
A: Patients with uncontrolled glaucoma, especially when vision is already lost or very limited
and other treatments have failed.
Q: What is the difference between SLT and cyclocryopexy?
A: SLT is a gentle laser for early to moderate glaucoma. Cyclocryopexy is a more aggressive
treatment for advanced cases.
Q: Will I need medications after cyclocryopexy?
A: Sometimes, yes. While the procedure lowers pressure, some patients may still need topical or
oral medications.
Q: Can both eyes be treated?
A: Yes, but usually not on the same day. Each eye is evaluated individually.
Q: Is the procedure covered by insurance?
A: Yes, typically covered if medically necessary for glaucoma management.

OCT-Macula
OCT-Macula, or Optical Coherence Tomography of the Macula, is a non-invasive imaging
test used to obtain high-resolution, cross-sectional images of the macula, the central portion of
the retina responsible for sharp, detailed vision. This advanced diagnostic tool allows eye doctors
to detect and monitor macular diseases, often before symptoms become noticeable.
OCT-Macula is invaluable in diagnosing and tracking conditions such as age-related macular
degeneration (AMD), diabetic macular edema, macular holes, epiretinal membranes, and
retinal vein occlusions.

<!-- Page 730 -->
Symptoms and Causes
When Is OCT-Macula Recommended?
This test is typically ordered when patients present with symptoms that suggest macular
involvement or are at high risk for retinal disease.
Common Symptoms Suggesting Macular Issues:
Blurry or distorted central vision
Difficulty reading or recognizing faces
Wavy or warped appearance of straight lines (metamorphopsia)
A central blind spot (scotoma)
Sudden vision loss
Color vision changes
Common Conditions That Affect the Macula:
Age-related macular degeneration (AMD)
Diabetic macular edema (DME)
Macular hole
Epiretinal membrane (macular pucker)
Central serous retinopathy
Retinal vein occlusion
Myopic maculopathy
Cystoid macular edema
Diagnosis and Tests
How OCT-Macula Works
OCT uses light waves (rather than X-rays or ultrasound) to capture cross-sectional images of the
retina. The technology provides detailed views of each retinal layer, especially the central
macula, allowing the detection of minute structural changes.
What Happens During the Test?
1. The patient rests their chin on a support device.
2. The machine scans the eye using near-infrared light.
3. The scan takes only a few minutes and is completely painless.
4. Dilation may be required in some cases for better imaging.

<!-- Page 731 -->
What OCT-Macula Measures:
Retinal thickness
Presence of fluid, cysts, or hemorrhages
Macular contour and layers
Signs of atrophy or degeneration
Subretinal or intraretinal fluid
Traction or membranes on the retinal surface
Management and Treatment
How OCT-Macula Aids in Treatment Planning
OCT results guide both diagnosis and treatment plans for various macular conditions. Depending
on the findings, the eye specialist may recommend:
Intravitreal injections (e.g., anti-VEGF drugs for AMD or DME)
Laser therapy for leaking blood vessels
Surgical procedures (e.g., vitrectomy for macular holes or membranes)
Ongoing monitoring for slow-progressing diseases
Medical management of underlying conditions like diabetes or hypertension
OCT-Macula is also used to track the effectiveness of treatment over time, with repeat scans
comparing baseline and post-treatment images.

Surgical Planning with OCT:

Prevention
How OCT-Macula Helps Prevent Vision Loss
While OCT itself doesn’t prevent disease, it plays a critical role in early detection, which leads
to timely intervention and vision preservation.
Prevention Tips for Macular Health:
Regular eye exams, especially after age 50
Managing systemic diseases like diabetes and hypertension
Smoking cessation (linked to AMD progression)
UV eye protection with sunglasses
Healthy diet rich in leafy greens and omega-3s
Prompt reporting of any vision changes to an eye doctor
Outlook / Prognosis
How OCT-Macula Improves Prognosis
Timely OCT scans can significantly improve the outlook for macular conditions:
Early AMD: Detected and monitored before vision loss
Diabetic edema: Treated quickly to preserve vision
Macular holes or traction: Surgically repaired with better outcomes when found early
Central serous retinopathy: Monitored to avoid unnecessary intervention
With regular follow-ups, OCT helps clinicians tailor treatment plans, ensuring patients
maintain the best possible vision.

Living With OCT-Macula Monitoring
Patients undergoing regular OCT-Macula scans are typically living with chronic eye conditions
that require lifelong management.
What to Expect:
Repeat scans every 1–6 months, depending on condition
Eye injections or treatments based on OCT changes
Lifestyle modifications (e.g., diet, glucose control, eye protection)
Occasional dilation for imaging
Patient engagement in visual self-monitoring (e.g., Amsler Grid for AMD)
With ongoing monitoring, many patients retain functional vision for years.
Additional Common Questions
Is OCT-Macula safe?
Yes. It is a completely safe, non-invasive imaging test with no exposure to radiation.
Does it hurt?
No. The test is quick and painless, with no contact to the eyeball.
How long does it take?
The scan usually takes 5–10 minutes per eye.
Can it detect all macular problems?
OCT is extremely effective for structural issues but may be complemented by angiography or
other imaging in complex cases.
Do I need to be dilated for an OCT scan?
In many cases, no. But for better image quality, dilation may be recommended.

FAQs
Q: Is OCT-Macula the same as a retinal exam?
A: No. A retinal exam uses direct visualization. OCT provides a cross-sectional view that
reveals deeper, microscopic structural changes.
Q: Is OCT-Macula used for glaucoma?
A: OCT of the optic nerve head (OCT-ONH) is typically used for glaucoma, though macular
scans can also help assess ganglion cell loss.
Q: Can children get OCT scans?
A: Yes. It is safe for children, especially when congenital or genetic macular diseases are
suspected.
Q: Does insurance cover OCT-Macula?
A: Most insurances cover it when medically necessary, such as in AMD, diabetic retinopathy, or
unexplained vision loss.
Q: How often should OCT-Macula be repeated?
A: Frequency depends on the condition—ranging from monthly (for active treatment) to
annually (for routine monitoring).
