DMEK - Overview
Descemet Membrane Endothelial Keratoplasty (DMEK) is a modern, minimally invasive
corneal transplant procedure used to treat diseases of the corneal endothelium, which is the
innermost layer of the cornea. Unlike traditional corneal transplants, which replace the entire
cornea, DMEK involves the transplant of only the damaged endothelial layer, leaving the rest of
the cornea intact. This technique is often preferred for conditions like Fuchs’ dystrophy or
corneal edema, as it offers faster recovery times, improved visual outcomes, and a lower risk of
complications.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms of conditions requiring DMEK:
Blurred or cloudy vision: Often associated with damage to the endothelial layer of the
cornea.
Light sensitivity (photophobia): The corneal endothelium controls fluid balance, and its
dysfunction may lead to corneal swelling, which causes sensitivity to light.
Eye discomfort: Patients may experience a dull ache or discomfort due to corneal edema
(swelling).
Reduced vision despite corrective lenses: As the endothelial layer fails, fluid
accumulates in the cornea, affecting vision.
Distorted or haloed vision: This may occur in more advanced cases of corneal disease,
where the cornea becomes thickened or deformed.
Causes of endothelial dysfunction treated with DMEK include:
Fuchs’ endothelial dystrophy: A genetic condition where the endothelial cells
deteriorate, leading to corneal swelling.
Corneal edema: Swelling of the cornea due to endothelial cell loss, which disrupts the
cornea's ability to pump out excess fluid.
Previous eye surgery: Surgery like cataract removal can damage the endothelial layer,
leading to corneal problems.
Infections or trauma: Severe infections or injury can damage the endothelium and cause
corneal dysfunction.

Diagnosis and Tests
To diagnose conditions requiring DMEK, an ophthalmologist typically performs several tests:
Slit-lamp examination: A high-powered microscope allows the doctor to examine the
corneal structure, including the endothelial layer, for damage or swelling.
Specular microscopy: This test uses a special microscope to examine the endothelial
cells and assess their density and health. A decrease in endothelial cell count can indicate
conditions like Fuchs' dystrophy.
Pachymetry: A test to measure corneal thickness, which helps to assess the extent of
corneal edema.
Corneal topography: Used to map the cornea and identify irregularities in shape or
thickness.
Optical coherence tomography (OCT): A non-invasive imaging technique that helps
visualize the layers of the cornea, particularly the endothelial layer.
Management and Treatment
Before considering surgery, management for corneal endothelial diseases may include:
Hypertonic saline drops: These help reduce corneal swelling by drawing fluid out of the
cornea.
Medication: Anti-inflammatory or anti-glaucoma medications may be used to address
any underlying conditions or secondary complications.
Contact lenses: Special lenses, such as scleral lenses, may be used to improve vision in
patients with corneal edema or endothelial dysfunction.
Monitoring: Regular eye exams to track the progression of the disease and evaluate if
surgical intervention is necessary.
When conservative management fails to alleviate symptoms, DMEK becomes the treatment of
choice, replacing only the endothelial layer to restore the cornea’s normal function.
DMEK Types & Surgery
There are a few variations of endothelial keratoplasty, with DMEK being the most advanced and
least invasive:
1. DMEK: Involves replacing just the endothelial layer of the cornea. It is considered the
gold standard for treating corneal endothelial dysfunction due to its minimal invasiveness
and high success rate.
2. Descemet’s Stripping Endothelial Keratoplasty (DSEK): An earlier technique where a
thicker layer of tissue, including part of the Descemet’s membrane, is transplanted. It has
a slightly higher risk of rejection and longer recovery time compared to DMEK.
The DMEK procedure involves:

Removal of the damaged endothelial layer: The surgeon removes the diseased
endothelial cells from the patient’s cornea.
Donor graft preparation: The healthy endothelial layer from a donor cornea is carefully
prepared and rolled up into a small cylinder.
Graft insertion: The donor graft is inserted into the patient’s eye using a special injector
and unrolled onto the cornea.
Fluid injection: The graft is positioned, and air is injected into the eye to hold the graft in
place while it adheres to the corneal tissue.
Postoperative care: The patient is monitored for complications, and follow-up visits are
scheduled to ensure proper graft attachment.
Complicated DMEK
While DMEK is a highly effective procedure, complications can occur:
Graft rejection: The immune system may recognize the donor tissue as foreign and
attempt to reject it.
Graft dislocation: In rare cases, the donor graft may become dislodged and require
repositioning or replacement.
Infection: Like any surgical procedure, there is a risk of infection following the
transplant.
Increased intraocular pressure: A rise in pressure inside the eye may occur after
surgery, potentially leading to glaucoma.
Corneal edema: If the graft fails to function properly, the cornea may become swollen
again, affecting vision.
Endothelial cell loss: There can be a loss of endothelial cells from the graft over time,
which may lead to graft failure.
DMEK in Adults
DMEK is primarily used in adults with endothelial diseases such as Fuchs’ dystrophy, post-
cataract surgery endothelial failure, or corneal edema. In these cases, DMEK offers excellent
outcomes with rapid visual recovery and minimal risk of graft rejection or astigmatism. Adult
patients generally benefit from the procedure, which restores normal corneal function and
improves quality of life. Postoperative care, including follow-up visits, is crucial for monitoring
the success of the transplant.
DMEK in Children
Though less common in children, DMEK may be performed in cases where congenital
endothelial dystrophies or other endothelial issues are present. The surgery in children may carry
different risks and requires careful monitoring since younger patients are still growing, and the
cornea may change in shape over time. The success rate is high, but special consideration is
given to the long-term stability of the transplant.

Prevention
Preventing the need for DMEK focuses on protecting corneal health:
Early detection of endothelial diseases such as Fuchs’ dystrophy through regular eye
exams can help manage symptoms before they require surgery.
Protective eyewear: For individuals at risk of eye trauma, using protective eyewear
during activities can prevent damage to the cornea.
Managing systemic conditions: Conditions like diabetes can impact eye health, so
regular monitoring is important for early intervention.
Treating infections promptly: Timely treatment of corneal infections can help prevent
lasting damage to the endothelial layer.
Outlook / Prognosis
The outlook for DMEK patients is generally very positive, with many experiencing a dramatic
improvement in vision. The procedure’s minimally invasive nature leads to a quicker recovery,
often within 3-6 months. The success rate is high, with graft survival rates upwards of 90% in
many studies. However, the long-term success depends on factors like the patient's age, the
underlying disease, and adherence to postoperative care.
Living With DMEK
After DMEK, patients need to:
Follow strict aftercare instructions, which may include the use of eye drops and
avoiding physical strain on the eyes.
Attend regular follow-up visits to monitor the health of the graft and address any
complications early.
Protect the eyes from injury and avoid rubbing the eyes, as this can affect graft healing.
Adhere to activity restrictions: While light activities can resume relatively soon, high-
risk activities should be avoided until full recovery.
Additional Common Questions (FAQs)
Q1: How long does recovery take after DMEK surgery?
Recovery from DMEK surgery typically takes about 3-6 months, with most patients seeing
significant vision improvement within the first 1-2 months. Full healing may take up to a year.
Q2: Is there a risk of the graft being rejected?
Yes, graft rejection is a potential risk after DMEK surgery. However, with proper post-surgical
care, rejection can often be detected early and treated effectively.
Q3: Will I need glasses or contact lenses after DMEK surgery?
While DMEK improves vision, some patients may still need glasses or contact lenses, especially
if there are any residual refractive errors or astigmatism after surgery.

Q4: Is DMEK surgery painful?
DMEK is generally well-tolerated, and pain is usually minimal, with most patients experiencing
discomfort that can be managed with over-the-counter pain medications.
Q5: How effective is DMEK compared to traditional corneal transplants?
DMEK is considered more effective than traditional full-thickness corneal transplants due to
faster recovery, reduced risk of rejection, and improved visual outcomes. It also reduces the risk
of complications like astigmatism.
Q6: Can DMEK be performed on both eyes at the same time?
In some cases, DMEK can be performed on both eyes during the same surgery, but this depends
on the individual’s condition and overall health. Typically, surgeries are done one at a time to
minimize complications.
