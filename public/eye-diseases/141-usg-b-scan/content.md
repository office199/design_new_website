USG - B Scan
USG - B Scan, or B-scan ultrasonography, is a non-invasive diagnostic imaging technique used
primarily in ophthalmology to visualize the interior structures of the eye. It uses high-frequency
sound waves to produce two-dimensional cross-sectional images, allowing detailed examination
of the vitreous, retina, optic nerve, and other intraocular components. B-scan ultrasonography is
invaluable when direct visualization is hindered by opacities like dense cataracts or vitreous
hemorrhage.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms Leading to USG - B Scan:
Sudden vision loss or blurring
Presence of floaters or flashes of light
Eye trauma causing internal damage
Suspected retinal detachment or tear
Unexplained eye pain or redness
Decreased visual acuity when fundus exam is not possible
Causes for Referral for B Scan:
Opaque media (e.g., dense cataract, corneal opacity)
Vitreous hemorrhage obstructing fundus view
Suspected retinal detachment or vitreous detachment
Intraocular tumors or foreign bodies
Evaluation of optic nerve head or orbital pathology
Follow-up after ocular surgeries or trauma
Diagnosis and Tests
USG - B Scan involves placing a small probe on the closed eyelid after applying a coupling gel.
The probe emits sound waves that bounce off ocular tissues and return echoes, which are
converted into real-time images.

This test is often used when:
Direct ophthalmoscopic examination is not possible.
The extent of retinal detachment or vitreous pathology needs assessment.
Monitoring tumors inside the eye or orbit.
Detecting foreign bodies or vitreous opacities.
It is quick, painless, and safe, with no radiation exposure.
Management and Treatment
While the USG - B Scan itself is a diagnostic tool and not a treatment, its results guide
management decisions:
Confirmation of retinal detachment or tears may lead to surgical intervention.
Identification of vitreous hemorrhage may prompt vitrectomy.
Detection of intraocular tumors requires oncologic management.
Monitoring response to treatments or progression of disease.
The scan helps tailor personalized treatment plans and avoid unnecessary invasive procedures.
USG - B Scan Types & Related Procedures
B Scan is part of ocular ultrasonography and can be complemented by:
A-Scan Ultrasonography: Provides one-dimensional data mainly for measuring axial
eye length.
Doppler Ultrasonography: Assesses blood flow in ocular vessels.
Orbital Ultrasound: Examines structures around the eye.
No surgery is involved in B-scan itself, but the findings can determine the need for surgeries like
vitrectomy, retinal repair, or tumor removal.
Prevention
Since B-scan is a diagnostic procedure, prevention relates to avoiding eye conditions that
necessitate imaging:
Regular eye checkups to catch issues early.

Protecting eyes from trauma and injury.
Controlling systemic diseases like diabetes and hypertension.
Prompt treatment of infections or inflammations.
Early detection through routine exams can minimize complications that require invasive
interventions.
Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis depends on the underlying condition diagnosed with the help of the B Scan. Early
and accurate detection of retinal detachment, tumors, or hemorrhages can significantly improve
outcomes through timely treatment. B-scan helps reduce diagnostic uncertainty and guides
effective management strategies.
Living With
Undergoing a B-scan is simple and non-invasive, causing no discomfort or downtime. Patients
should:
Follow up with their eye specialist for results and treatment plans.
Understand that the scan aids in monitoring eye health.
Maintain regular eye evaluations especially if diagnosed with chronic eye conditions.
Additional Common Questions
Q: Is USG - B Scan painful?
A: No, the procedure is painless and safe.
Q: How long does the B scan take?
A: Usually 5-15 minutes.
Q: Are there any risks or side effects?
A: There are no known risks as it uses sound waves, not radiation.
Q: Can B scan detect all eye problems?
A: It is excellent for vitreous and posterior segment issues but may not replace other imaging for
certain anterior segment diseases.

Q: How often should I have a B scan?
A: Only as advised by your ophthalmologist based on symptoms or disease monitoring needs.
FAQ’s
Q1: Can a B scan replace an eye exam?
A1: No, it is complementary and used when direct visualization is difficult.
Q2: Is USG B-scan safe during pregnancy?
A2: Yes, it is safe as it does not use radiation.
Q3: Will the scan affect my vision?
A3: No, it does not affect eyesight.
Q4: Can B scan detect glaucoma?
A4: B scan cannot diagnose glaucoma but may help detect secondary complications like optic
nerve changes.
Q5: Do I need to prepare for a B scan?
A5: No special preparation is required.
