Visual Field Test (Glaucoma)
A visual field test is a non-invasive diagnostic procedure used to map a person’s entire scope of
vision—central and peripheral. For individuals with or at risk of glaucoma, this test plays a
crucial role in detecting vision loss, tracking disease progression, and guiding treatment.
Glaucoma often damages the optic nerve silently, gradually narrowing the field of vision without
noticeable symptoms until the late stages. Early testing and regular monitoring are essential for
preserving sight.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms Suggesting the Need for a Visual Field Test
Glaucoma often presents with few early signs. However, if symptoms appear, they may include:
Gradual loss of side (peripheral) vision
Tunnel vision in advanced stages
Difficulty adjusting to low light
Frequent changes in glasses prescription
Eye pain or pressure (in acute angle-closure glaucoma)
Headaches or halos around lights
Causes of Glaucoma
Elevated intraocular pressure (IOP) damaging the optic nerve
Genetic predisposition
Age (more common in people over 60)
Medical conditions like diabetes, hypertension
Past eye injuries or surgeries
Long-term use of corticosteroids
Diagnosis and Tests
The visual field test is part of a comprehensive glaucoma evaluation and involves:

<!-- Page 697 -->
Perimetry Testing: Patient looks straight ahead and presses a button each time they see a
light flash in their side vision.
Types of Visual Field Tests:
o
Standard Automated Perimetry (SAP): The most common and detailed test for
glaucoma.
o
Frequency Doubling Technology (FDT): A quick screening tool for early vision
loss.
o
Kinetic Perimetry (e.g., Goldmann): Used for individuals who may struggle
with standard tests, such as children or those with physical limitations.
Other tests usually done alongside include:
Tonometry: To measure intraocular pressure.
Ophthalmoscopy: To inspect the optic nerve.
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): To assess optic nerve fiber thickness.
Management and Treatment
While the visual field test itself is diagnostic and not therapeutic, it is essential in managing
glaucoma. Based on results:
Medications: Eye drops to lower eye pressure.
Laser treatments: Such as Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT).
Surgical options: Trabeculectomy or minimally invasive glaucoma surgeries (MIGS).
Lifestyle adjustments: Monitoring blood pressure, controlling diabetes, and protecting
the eyes from injury.
Ongoing visual field tests help determine whether treatment is effective or needs adjustment.
Visual Field Test (Glaucoma) Types & Surgery
Visual Field Test Types for Glaucoma
1. Static Perimetry (e.g., Humphrey Field Analyzer): Measures light sensitivity at fixed
locations.
2. Kinetic Perimetry (e.g., Goldmann): Uses moving targets to map the field.
3. Frequency Doubling Perimetry: Detects early nerve fiber loss.

Surgery for Glaucoma
While the test does not involve surgery, visual field testing helps determine the timing and need
for glaucoma surgery, such as:
Trabeculectomy
Glaucoma drainage implants
MIGS (Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery)
Prevention
Glaucoma cannot always be prevented, but vision loss from it can be minimized with:
Routine eye exams, especially for people over 40 or with a family history.
Regular visual field testing if you have been diagnosed with glaucoma or are at risk.
Managing underlying conditions like high blood pressure and diabetes.
Protecting the eyes from trauma and excessive pressure.
Outlook / Prognosis
With early detection and ongoing monitoring through visual field tests:
Glaucoma can often be managed, and further vision loss prevented.
Visual field loss is typically permanent, making early detection critical.
Patients adhering to treatment plans and undergoing regular testing often retain
functional vision throughout life.
Living With
Living with glaucoma means being proactive:
Take prescribed medications consistently.
Attend all follow-up appointments.
Undergo visual field testing as often as your eye doctor recommends.
Use low-vision aids if vision loss occurs.
Make home modifications for better lighting and safety.

Additional Common Questions
Why do I need repeated visual field tests?
Glaucoma is a chronic condition. Regular testing helps detect subtle changes that might require a
treatment update.
Is the visual field test painful?
No. It’s a non-invasive, painless test.
Can I fail a visual field test?
It’s not about passing or failing—it’s about identifying how well your peripheral vision is
functioning.
How long does the test take?
Each eye typically takes 5–10 minutes. The full test usually lasts under 30 minutes.
What do abnormal results mean?
They may indicate optic nerve damage from glaucoma or other eye/neurological conditions.
FAQs
Q1: How often should I take a visual field test if I have glaucoma?
A: Typically every 6–12 months, or more frequently if your condition is progressing rapidly.
Q2: Can glaucoma be diagnosed with just a visual field test?
A: No. It must be combined with IOP measurement, optic nerve imaging, and other tests.
Q3: Can other conditions affect my visual field?
A: Yes. Neurological disorders, retinal diseases, and even strokes can alter visual fields.
Q4: What if I have trouble concentrating during the test?
A: Inform your doctor. The test can often be paused or rescheduled, and accommodations made.
Q5: Can children undergo visual field testing?
A: Yes, but kinetic perimetry or modified techniques are usually used for pediatric patients.
Conclusion:
The visual field test is a vital tool in diagnosing and managing glaucoma, a silent but potentially
blinding condition. By catching early changes in peripheral vision, this test empowers doctors
and patients to take timely action. With regular monitoring, treatment adjustments, and eye care,
vision can often be preserved for life.
