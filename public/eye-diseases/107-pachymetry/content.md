Pachymetry
Pachymetry is a non-invasive diagnostic test that measures the thickness of the cornea, the
transparent front part of the eye. It is a crucial tool in the diagnosis, monitoring, and
management of various eye conditions, especially glaucoma, corneal diseases, and pre-
operative assessment for refractive surgeries like LASIK.
The thickness of the cornea can influence intraocular pressure (IOP) readings and help detect
conditions like keratoconus, making pachymetry a standard test in comprehensive eye exams.
Symptoms and Causes
Pachymetry itself is not used to detect symptoms, but it helps diagnose conditions that may
present with the following symptoms:
Blurred or distorted vision
Frequent changes in eyeglass prescription
Eye discomfort or irritation
Unexplained visual disturbances
Suspected glaucoma or elevated eye pressure
Causes for Performing Pachymetry:
Screening for glaucoma
Evaluating corneal diseases such as keratoconus, Fuchs’ dystrophy, or corneal edema
Pre-LASIK or PRK assessment
Post-operative evaluation after corneal surgeries or transplants
Monitoring corneal swelling due to trauma, infection, or surgery

Diagnosis and Tests
Pachymetry is quick, painless, and safe. It may be part of a broader corneal evaluation that
includes:
Types of Pachymetry Tests:
Ultrasound Pachymetry: A probe touches the cornea to measure thickness via sound
waves.
Optical Pachymetry: Uses light (usually from a slit lamp or OCT device) to measure
corneal thickness without contact.
Procedure:
In ultrasound pachymetry, numbing drops are applied, and the probe is gently placed
on the center of the cornea.
In optical pachymetry, the patient looks into a device while light scans the cornea.
Measurements are usually completed in under 5 minutes.
Management and Treatment
Pachymetry is not a treatment but a diagnostic tool. However, the results of the test guide
treatment decisions:
A thin cornea may indicate a higher risk for glaucoma and influence the interpretation of
IOP.
A thick cornea might lead to falsely elevated IOP readings, avoiding unnecessary
treatment.
For patients with keratoconus or corneal thinning, pachymetry helps in tracking
progression and planning interventions like corneal cross-linking.
Corneal transplant candidates require pachymetry for surgical planning.

Surgical Relevance:
Refractive Surgery (LASIK/PRK): Pachymetry ensures the cornea is thick enough to
safely perform laser vision correction.
Corneal Transplants: Determines the extent of damage or thinning before planning
surgery.
Glaucoma Surgery: Influences treatment plans, especially when pressure-lowering
surgeries are considered.
Prevention
Pachymetry helps prevent vision loss by identifying risks early. While you can’t prevent the
need for pachymetry, you can:
Attend regular eye exams, especially if you have risk factors like glaucoma or a family
history of eye disease.
Avoid eye trauma and protect your eyes from injury.
Manage conditions like diabetes and hypertension, which can affect eye health.
Seek evaluation before undergoing refractive surgery.
Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis depends on the underlying condition being monitored or diagnosed with
pachymetry. For example:
In glaucoma, knowing corneal thickness leads to more accurate IOP readings and better
treatment decisions.
In keratoconus, pachymetry helps track thinning and progression to guide interventions.
In surgical planning, accurate measurements help prevent complications.
Pachymetry itself is a safe and repeatable test that improves patient outcomes across various
eye conditions.

Additional Common Questions About Pachymetry
Is pachymetry painful?
No. Whether contact or non-contact, the test is completely painless. Numbing drops are used if
the probe touches your eye.
How often should I get pachymetry done?
It depends on your condition. For glaucoma or keratoconus, you may need it annually or semi-
annually.
Can it detect eye diseases?
Not directly, but it provides critical information that helps detect or monitor eye diseases.
Is it safe for children?
Yes, pachymetry is safe for children, especially if congenital glaucoma or corneal abnormalities
are suspected.
Do results vary with time?
Corneal thickness may vary slightly, especially with fluid retention or eye swelling. Repeat tests
may be needed for accuracy.
FAQs for Pachymetry
Q1. What is a normal corneal thickness?
A: Average central corneal thickness is around 540–560 microns, but this can vary by
individual.
Q2. Why is pachymetry important in glaucoma?
A: It helps interpret intraocular pressure accurately. Thin corneas may underestimate pressure,
affecting treatment plans.

Q3. Is pachymetry required before LASIK?
A: Yes. LASIK candidates must have a certain minimum corneal thickness to safely undergo the
procedure.
Q4. Can corneal thickness change over time?
A: Yes. Conditions like keratoconus or corneal edema can cause thinning or swelling over time.
Q5. Will insurance cover pachymetry?
A: Often yes, especially when medically necessary for glaucoma or other diagnostic purposes.
Check with your provider.
