DALK - Overview
Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty (DALK) is a specialized type of corneal transplant
surgery where only the diseased or damaged layers of the cornea are removed, while leaving the
healthy, innermost layer (Descemet's membrane) intact. This procedure is typically used to treat
corneal diseases that affect the outer layers of the cornea, such as keratoconus and corneal
scarring, without the need to replace the entire cornea. DALK has become a preferred method of
corneal transplantation, offering faster recovery times, fewer complications, and reduced risk of
graft rejection compared to traditional full-thickness corneal transplants (Penetrating
Keratoplasty).
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms that may indicate a condition requiring DALK include:
Blurry or distorted vision: As the cornea becomes progressively more irregular due to
conditions like keratoconus, the vision becomes increasingly difficult to correct with
glasses or contact lenses.
Corneal scarring: Trauma, infections, or diseases like keratoconus can lead to scarring,
affecting vision.
Sensitivity to light (photophobia): Corneal irregularities may lead to discomfort in
bright light.
Eye irritation or pain: As the disease progresses, the cornea becomes more vulnerable,
and the patient may experience discomfort.
Frequent changes in prescription: Patients with progressive corneal diseases may need
frequent changes to glasses or contact lenses due to deteriorating vision.

Common Causes of Corneal Conditions Treated by DALK include:
Keratoconus: A condition in which the cornea thins and bulges outwards, causing severe
visual distortion.
Corneal scarring: Results from trauma, infection, or past surgeries, causing opacity and
impairing vision.
Corneal dystrophies: Genetic disorders like granular dystrophy or lattice dystrophy,
which affect the cornea’s clarity.
Post-surgical scarring: After cataract surgery or refractive surgeries, some patients
develop corneal scarring that impairs vision.
Diagnosis and Tests
To determine if DALK is necessary, several diagnostic tests are performed:
Slit-lamp examination: A detailed examination of the cornea to identify irregularities,
scarring, or thinning of the corneal layers.
Corneal topography: Maps the shape and surface of the cornea to assess irregularities
and guide surgical planning, especially in cases of keratoconus.
Specular microscopy: Used to examine the endothelial cells of the cornea to ensure they
are functioning correctly and to rule out endothelial diseases that may not be treatable by
DALK.
Pachymetry: Measures the thickness of the cornea to determine the extent of thinning
and assess suitability for DALK.
Optical coherence tomography (OCT): Provides high-resolution images of the cornea's
layers, helping surgeons assess the depth and extent of disease.
Management and Treatment
In many cases, corneal conditions are managed with non-surgical treatments first, such as:
Contact lenses: Specialized lenses, including rigid gas permeable (RGP) or scleral
lenses, may help patients with keratoconus or corneal irregularities to improve vision.
Eye drops: Anti-inflammatory drops or hypertonic saline solutions may be prescribed to
reduce swelling and inflammation in the cornea.
Corneal crosslinking: For conditions like keratoconus, this treatment strengthens the
cornea to slow or stop disease progression and improve stability.
Medications: If an infection is present, antibiotics or antiviral medications may be used
to treat the underlying issue.
However, when these conservative treatments fail, or the condition is severe, DALK may be
recommended as a surgical option to replace the damaged layers of the cornea.
DALK Types & Surgery
Types of DALK:

<!-- Page 559 -->
1. Selective DALK: The surgeon removes only the anterior layers of the cornea, leaving the
Descemet's membrane and endothelium intact. This technique is commonly used in
treating keratoconus or other anterior corneal diseases.
2. Manual DALK: In this traditional method, the surgeon manually separates the healthy
inner corneal layers from the outer diseased tissue before performing the transplant.
3. Automated DALK: This technique uses specialized tools like a microkeratome or
femtosecond laser to create a precise lamellar dissection, offering a more controlled and
predictable procedure compared to manual methods.
The DALK procedure involves several steps:
Dissection of the cornea: The surgeon removes the anterior layers of the cornea, leaving
Descemet’s membrane intact. This can be done manually or with the help of lasers.
Preparation of the donor tissue: The donor cornea is prepared by isolating the
endothelial layer and keeping it intact, ensuring it fits properly into the recipient’s eye.
Graft placement: The donor tissue is sutured into place, ensuring a perfect fit between
the donor and recipient corneal layers.
Air injection: Air is injected into the eye to press the graft against the recipient’s cornea,
allowing it to adhere properly while the graft heals.
The entire surgery typically takes around 1-2 hours, and the patient is often discharged the same
day, although follow-up visits are required for monitoring.
Complicated DALK
As with any surgery, DALK carries a risk of complications:
Graft rejection: Although rare, the body’s immune system may recognize the
transplanted tissue as foreign and attempt to reject it.
Graft dislocation: In some cases, the graft may shift or become dislodged, requiring
repositioning or even replacement.
Infection: Like any surgical procedure, infection is a risk, which can threaten the success
of the transplant.
Increased intraocular pressure (IOP): Surgery can sometimes lead to a rise in eye
pressure, potentially causing glaucoma.
Incomplete endothelial function: If the Descemet’s membrane is damaged or not
functioning correctly, it could lead to corneal swelling (edema) post-surgery.
Astigmatism: Changes in the cornea's curvature following surgery may cause
astigmatism, requiring additional corrective procedures.
DALK in Adults
DALK is most commonly performed on adults, particularly those with conditions like
keratoconus, post-surgical corneal scarring, or corneal dystrophies. The procedure is highly
effective for treating corneal diseases that affect only the anterior layers. Adults typically

DALK in Children
DALK is less common in children but can be performed in cases where the child has
keratoconus or congenital corneal dystrophies. Since children's corneas are still developing,
special care is taken to monitor their growth and corneal health post-surgery. The success rate is
high, but close follow-up is critical to ensure long-term graft survival and avoid complications.
Prevention
Preventing the need for DALK involves:
Early detection of keratoconus and other corneal diseases through regular eye exams,
particularly for those with a family history of these conditions.
Protective eyewear: Wearing safety goggles during sports or risky activities to prevent
trauma to the eye.
Prompt treatment of corneal infections: Treating any infections early can prevent
scarring that might necessitate a transplant.
Managing underlying conditions: Ensuring conditions like atopy or allergies, which
may exacerbate keratoconus, are well-controlled.
Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis after DALK is generally very good, with many patients experiencing a significant
improvement in vision. The procedure offers advantages over traditional corneal transplants,
such as:
Reduced risk of graft rejection: Since only the outer layers of the cornea are
transplanted, the body’s immune system is less likely to reject the graft.
Faster visual recovery: The healing process is often quicker than with full-thickness
transplants, as fewer layers are disrupted.
Better long-term outcomes: With proper aftercare, the success rate of DALK is high,
with many patients enjoying excellent visual outcomes.
However, recovery times vary, and regular follow-up visits are essential to monitor the healing
process and ensure there are no complications.
Living With DALK
Post-DALK, patients will need to:
Use prescribed medications, including antibiotics, corticosteroid eye drops, and
sometimes anti-rejection drugs, to prevent infection and inflammation.

Attend follow-up appointments regularly to ensure the graft is taking hold and monitor
for complications like rejection or infection.
Protect the eye from injury and avoid rubbing the eyes, which can interfere with the

Gradually resume normal activities: Most patients can return to light activities within a
few weeks, but high-impact sports and activities that could risk eye injury should be
avoided for several months.
Additional Common Questions (FAQs)
Q1: How long does it take to recover from DALK surgery?
Recovery from DALK is typically quicker than full-thickness corneal transplants. Most patients
begin to see improvement within 1-2 months, with full recovery taking around 6-12 months.
Q2: Will I need to wear glasses or contact lenses after DALK surgery?
While DALK restores the corneal structure, patients may still need glasses or contact lenses for
vision correction, especially if there are residual refractive errors.
Q3: What are the risks of DALK surgery?
The main risks include graft rejection, infection, graft dislocation, and increased intraocular
pressure. However, these complications are rare, and the procedure is generally safe.
Q4: Can both eyes be treated with DALK at the same time?
In some cases, DALK can be performed on both eyes during the same surgery, but it is often
done one eye at a time to minimize the risk of complications and to allow for better healing.
Q5: Is DALK suitable for everyone?
DALK is particularly effective for conditions that affect the outer layers of the cornea, but it may
not be appropriate for patients with damage to the innermost layers of the cornea. Your eye
doctor will determine the best treatment for your specific condition.
