YAG Laser Treatment
YAG laser (short for Yttrium-Aluminum-Garnet laser) is a non-invasive laser procedure
commonly used in ophthalmology to treat posterior capsule opacification (PCO)—a common
complication after cataract surgery—as well as certain types of glaucoma and other eye
conditions. The laser delivers focused energy pulses to create precise openings in eye tissues
without incisions or stitches.
It is a quick, outpatient procedure that significantly improves vision clarity and helps manage
elevated intraocular pressure (IOP), depending on its application.
Symptoms and Causes
When YAG Laser Treatment Is Needed:
YAG laser is typically recommended for individuals who experience visual disturbances
following certain eye surgeries or due to fluid flow issues in the eye.

Symptoms That May Require YAG Laser Treatment:
Cloudy or blurry vision after cataract surgery
Increased sensitivity to light or glare
Difficulty reading or seeing fine detail
Halos around lights
Vision that appears foggy or as if looking through a film
Elevated intraocular pressure (glaucoma-related)
Causes:
Posterior Capsule Opacification (PCO) – Also called “secondary cataract,” it occurs
when the membrane behind the artificial lens becomes cloudy after cataract surgery.
Angle-closure glaucoma – Where fluid builds up in the eye due to a blocked drainage
angle.
Iris abnormalities or vitreous floaters, in some cases.
Diagnosis and Tests
Before recommending YAG laser treatment, an eye care provider will conduct a comprehensive
evaluation.
Diagnostic Steps Include:
Slit-lamp examination – to check for cloudiness in the posterior capsule.
Visual acuity test – to measure any vision decline.
Tonometry – to assess intraocular pressure.
Optical coherence tomography (OCT) – to evaluate the back of the eye, including the
retina and optic nerve.
Gonioscopy – to inspect the drainage angle in glaucoma patients.
Accurate diagnosis ensures the YAG laser is used appropriately and effectively.
Management and Treatment
What Happens During YAG Laser Treatment?
Performed in an outpatient setting (no hospitalization).
Takes only 5–10 minutes per eye.
No need for incisions or sutures.
Local anesthetic eye drops are used.

The laser creates a small opening in the cloudy capsule (for PCO) or improves fluid
flow (for glaucoma).
Post-Treatment Care:
Vision typically improves within 24–48 hours.
Temporary floaters may appear.
Anti-inflammatory eye drops may be prescribed for a few days.
Regular follow-up is required to monitor recovery and pressure.
Most patients return to normal activities the same day or next.
YAG Laser Types & Surgery
Types of YAG Laser Procedures:
1. YAG Capsulotomy:
o
Used to treat posterior capsule opacification after cataract surgery.
o
Restores clarity by creating an opening in the cloudy capsule behind the IOL.
2. YAG Peripheral Iridotomy:
o
Used to treat or prevent angle-closure glaucoma.
o
Creates a tiny hole in the peripheral iris to allow fluid to drain, reducing eye
pressure.
3. Laser Vitreolysis (less common):
o
Targets vitreous floaters to improve visual comfort.
Is It a Surgery?
YAG laser is considered minimally invasive and is often categorized as a laser procedure
rather than traditional surgery, as there are no cuts or stitches involved.
Prevention
While YAG laser treats conditions after they occur, preventive strategies can help minimize the
risk of requiring it:
Attend all follow-up visits after cataract surgery to detect early PCO.
Control intraocular pressure with medications if you're at risk for glaucoma.
Protect your eyes from trauma and UV exposure.
Manage systemic health issues, such as diabetes and hypertension, that can impact eye
health.

Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis after YAG laser treatment is excellent for most patients:
Vision often improves within hours to days.
PCO does not usually return after capsulotomy.
Complications are rare but may include temporary eye pressure spikes or inflammation.
Glaucoma patients may need continued monitoring or additional treatments.
Most individuals experience long-term improvement in visual function and quality of life.
Living With YAG Laser Results
What to Expect Post-Procedure:
Clearer vision, especially if treated for secondary cataracts.
Floaters may appear but usually subside within days.
Eye drops may be used briefly to prevent inflammation.
Resume normal activities the next day unless advised otherwise.
Regular eye exams remain important, especially for glaucoma or those with existing retinal
issues.
Additional Common Questions About YAG Laser
Is YAG laser painful?
No. The procedure is painless. Numbing eye drops are used, and patients usually feel only mild
discomfort or light flashes.
How soon will my vision improve?
Many patients notice improvement within 24 hours, although it may take up to a week.
Will I need it again?
Usually not. Once the cloudy capsule is removed, it does not grow back. However, glaucoma
patients may need additional treatments.
Are there side effects?
Side effects are rare but may include floaters, eye pressure spikes, or inflammation. These are
usually temporary and treatable.

Can YAG laser damage my eye?
When performed by an experienced ophthalmologist, the risk of complications is very low.
FAQs for YAG Laser Treatment
Q1. How long does the YAG laser procedure take?
A: About 5–10 minutes per eye in most cases.
Q2. Can both eyes be treated the same day?
A: Sometimes, yes. But often, doctors treat one eye at a time for safety.
Q3. Is the laser covered by insurance?
A: Yes, when deemed medically necessary (e.g., for PCO or glaucoma), it is typically covered.
Q4. Can I drive after the procedure?
A: You should arrange transportation on the day of the procedure. Most people resume driving
within a day, once vision stabilizes.
Q5. Will I still need glasses after YAG?
A: Possibly. YAG laser clears the capsule but doesn't correct refractive errors. You may still
need glasses or contact lenses for optimal vision.
