Ptosis (Drooping Eyelid)
Ptosis, also known as blepharoptosis, is a condition characterized by the abnormal drooping of
the upper eyelid. It can affect one eye (unilateral) or both eyes (bilateral) and may vary from
mild to severe. Ptosis can obstruct vision, especially if the eyelid droops far enough to cover the
pupil. It may be present from birth (congenital) or develop later in life (acquired). Depending on
the underlying cause, ptosis can be temporary or permanent, cosmetic or vision-threatening.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms
Visible drooping of one or both upper eyelids
Impaired or partially blocked vision
Eye fatigue or strain, especially during reading or detailed work
Head tilting backward to see clearly
Brow elevation to compensate for drooping lid
Uneven appearance of the eyes (asymmetry)
Dry or watery eyes
Causes
Congenital Ptosis – Caused by poor development of the levator muscle (muscle that lifts
the eyelid)
Age-related (involutional) Ptosis – Due to weakening or stretching of eyelid muscles
over time
Neurological disorders – Such as Horner’s syndrome, third cranial nerve palsy, or
myasthenia gravis
Trauma – Injury to eyelid or surrounding nerves
Eye surgery complications – Cataract or LASIK surgeries can occasionally affect eyelid
muscles
Tumors or cysts – Masses on the eyelid can weigh it down
Systemic diseases – Diabetes or muscular dystrophy affecting nerve or muscle function
Diagnosis and Tests
Diagnosing ptosis typically involves:
Comprehensive eye exam
Eyelid height measurement – Including margin-reflex distance and levator function

Visual field testing – To assess the impact of drooping on vision
Neurological evaluation – If nerve damage or systemic disease is suspected
Blood tests – In cases where autoimmune diseases (like myasthenia gravis) are suspected
Imaging (MRI/CT scan) – If a tumor or structural cause is suspected
Management and Treatment
Non-Surgical Treatment
Observation – Mild ptosis that doesn’t affect vision may not require intervention
Ptosis crutch – A mechanical device attached to glasses to lift the eyelid (temporary
solution)
Treatment of underlying condition – Addressing issues like nerve damage or
autoimmune disorders
Surgical Treatment
Levator resection or advancement – Tightening or shortening the eyelid muscle
Frontalis sling procedure – Connecting the eyelid to the forehead muscle (used in
severe or congenital cases)
Müller’s muscle-conjunctival resection – A less invasive surgery for mild ptosis
Blepharoplasty (in some cases) – Cosmetic eyelid surgery can be combined with ptosis
correction
Ptosis Types & Surgery
Types
Congenital Ptosis – Present at birth, often due to developmental muscle defect
Acquired Ptosis – Develops later in life due to age, trauma, or disease
Neurogenic Ptosis – Caused by nerve-related conditions (e.g., third nerve palsy)
Myogenic Ptosis – Due to muscle disease or dysfunction
Mechanical Ptosis – Caused by a mass or excess tissue weighing down the eyelid
Traumatic Ptosis – Result of direct injury to the eyelid or its muscles
Surgery
Customized based on cause and severity
Typically performed as an outpatient procedure under local or general anesthesia
Recovery involves mild swelling or bruising for 1–2 weeks
Results are often immediate but refine over a few weeks
Prevention

While congenital ptosis cannot be prevented, you can reduce the risk of acquired ptosis
by:
o
Protecting eyes from trauma
o
Managing chronic conditions such as diabetes or neurological diseases
o
Being cautious during eye surgeries
o
Regular eye exams to detect issues early
Outlook / Prognosis
With proper diagnosis and treatment, most ptosis cases can be corrected effectively
Early treatment, especially in children, is critical to avoid amblyopia ("lazy eye")
Surgical outcomes are typically long-lasting and significantly improve appearance and
function
In some cases, revision surgery may be needed for best results
Living With
Mild cases may only require monitoring or nonsurgical aids
Use sunglasses to protect eyes and improve comfort
Children with ptosis should have regular vision screening
Be attentive to new or worsening symptoms as they may indicate underlying neurological
issues
Post-surgery, follow care instructions carefully to ensure proper healing and function
Additional Common Questions
Is ptosis only cosmetic?
No. While it can affect appearance, ptosis may also impair vision and eye health.
Can ptosis go away on its own?
Congenital and age-related ptosis usually do not resolve without treatment.
Is surgery the only option?
Not always. Mild cases may be managed with ptosis crutches or by treating the
underlying condition.
Can children have ptosis?
Yes. Early diagnosis and treatment are important to prevent vision problems during
development.
Is ptosis surgery risky?
Complications are rare but can include asymmetry, undercorrection, or overcorrection.
FAQs
Q1: How long is recovery after ptosis surgery?
A: Most patients recover in 1–2 weeks, with minor swelling or bruising. Full results may take a
few months.

Q2: Does ptosis affect both eyes?
A: It can affect one or both eyes, depending on the underlying cause.
Q3: At what age can ptosis be treated in children?
A: Surgery is often considered around 3–5 years of age, unless vision is at immediate risk.
Q4: Can ptosis be hereditary?
A: Yes, congenital ptosis can run in families.
Q5: What is the success rate of ptosis surgery?
A: High—most patients experience both functional and cosmetic improvement with long-lasting
results.
Conclusion:
Ptosis is more than a cosmetic issue—it can interfere with vision and overall eye function.
Whether congenital or acquired, early diagnosis and a tailored treatment plan, including surgery
when necessary, can significantly improve quality of life and visual outcomes.
