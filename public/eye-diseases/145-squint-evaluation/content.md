Squint Evaluation
Overview
Squint evaluation is a comprehensive clinical assessment performed to diagnose and understand
the nature and extent of strabismus (commonly known as squint), where the eyes do not align
properly. This evaluation helps determine the cause, severity, and impact of the eye
misalignment on vision and guides appropriate management. Early and accurate evaluation is
essential for effective treatment, preventing complications like amblyopia (lazy eye) and
improving binocular vision.
Symptoms and Causes

<!-- Page 758 -->
Common Symptoms Noticed Before Squint Evaluation:
Visible deviation of one or both eyes inward, outward, upward, or downward
Double vision (diplopia)
Difficulty focusing or maintaining eye contact
Head tilting or turning to compensate for misalignment
Poor depth perception or difficulty judging distances
Eye strain or frequent headaches
Reduced vision in one eye
Causes Leading to Squint:
Congenital factors (present at birth)
Refractive errors such as farsightedness or astigmatism
Muscle imbalance or nerve palsies affecting eye movement
Neurological disorders (e.g., cerebral palsy, stroke)
Trauma or injury to the eye or brain
Systemic diseases like thyroid eye disease
Previous eye surgeries or infections
Diagnosis and Tests
The squint evaluation involves several diagnostic procedures performed by an ophthalmologist
or orthoptist to thoroughly understand the eye misalignment:
Visual Acuity Test: Measures clarity of vision for each eye.
Cover-Uncover Test: Detects the presence and type of squint.
Alternate Cover Test: Measures the total deviation of the eyes.
Prism Cover Test: Quantifies the angle of deviation in prism diopters.
Refraction Test: Identifies underlying refractive errors contributing to squint.
Ocular Motility Testing: Assesses eye muscle function and movement.
Stereopsis Testing: Evaluates depth perception.
Hirschberg and Krimsky Tests: Estimate eye deviation using corneal light reflexes.
Fundus Examination: Checks for retinal or optic nerve abnormalities.
Imaging (if needed): MRI or CT scans for neurological causes.
These tests help define the type, cause, and severity of squint, critical for formulating a treatment
plan.
Management and Treatment

<!-- Page 759 -->
Treatment of squint is highly individualized based on evaluation findings and may include:
Optical Correction: Glasses or contact lenses to correct refractive errors.
Patching Therapy: Covering the stronger eye to improve vision in the weaker eye
(amblyopia treatment).
Orthoptic Exercises: Targeted eye muscle training to improve coordination.
Prism Therapy: Using prism lenses to realign images and reduce double vision.
Pharmacologic Treatment: Botox injections to temporarily weaken overactive muscles.
Surgical Intervention: Realignment of eye muscles if non-surgical methods are
insufficient.
Regular follow-up after treatment initiation is essential to monitor progress and adjust therapy.
Squint Evaluation Types & Surgery
Types of Squint Evaluations:
Infantile Squint Evaluation: Focuses on congenital squints appearing in infancy.
Accommodative Squint Evaluation: Assesses squints caused by focusing effort and
refractive errors.
Paralytic Squint Evaluation: Deals with nerve palsies affecting eye muscles.
Intermittent vs Constant Squint Evaluation: Differentiates between sporadic and
persistent misalignment.
Surgery:
When conservative treatments fail or the squint is severe, strabismus surgery is performed to
adjust the length or position of extraocular muscles. Surgery aims to realign the eyes for better
cosmetic appearance and binocular function. Surgery is often combined with non-surgical
therapy for optimal outcomes.
Prevention
While some causes of squint cannot be prevented, early detection and intervention reduce risks:
Routine vision screening during childhood.
Early correction of refractive errors.
Prompt evaluation for any signs of eye misalignment.
Protecting eyes from trauma.
Managing systemic and neurological conditions proactively.

<!-- Page 760 -->
Regular pediatric eye exams are crucial for prevention of complications like amblyopia.
Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis depends on factors such as age at diagnosis, type of squint, and promptness of
treatment. Early intervention often results in excellent visual outcomes and improved eye
alignment. Delayed treatment may lead to permanent vision loss in the affected eye and
persistent cosmetic concerns. With comprehensive evaluation and appropriate management,
many patients achieve good functional and aesthetic results.
Living With
Individuals with squint can lead normal lives with proper management:
Adhering to treatment plans including glasses, patching, or exercises.
Understanding that some cases require long-term monitoring.
Using adaptive strategies at school or work if needed.
Embracing cosmetic options, including surgery if desired.
Supportive counseling and education help patients and families cope with social or psychological
impacts.
Additional Common Questions
Q: How early can squint be detected?
A: Squint can be detected as early as infancy, and early detection is key to successful treatment.
Q: Is squint always visible?
A: Some squints are intermittent or subtle and may require professional evaluation to detect.
Q: Can squint cause blindness?
A: If untreated, squint can lead to amblyopia, which may cause permanent vision loss.
Q: Does squint affect both eyes?
A: Squint usually involves one eye turning, but both eyes can be affected over time.
Q: How successful is squint surgery?
A: Surgery is generally very successful, especially when combined with non-surgical treatments.

FAQ’s
Q1: What is the difference between squint and lazy eye?
A1: Squint refers to misaligned eyes; lazy eye is decreased vision in one eye due to poor use
during development.
Q2: Can adults undergo squint evaluation and treatment?
A2: Yes, adults can be evaluated and treated, though outcomes may differ from children.
Q3: How long does squint evaluation take?
A3: A comprehensive evaluation typically takes 30-60 minutes.
Q4: Is squint evaluation painful?
A4: No, it is a non-invasive and painless process.
Q5: Will squint evaluation require repeated visits?
A5: Yes, follow-ups help monitor treatment progress and adjust plans.
