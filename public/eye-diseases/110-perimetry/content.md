Perimetry
Perimetry, also known as a visual field test, is a non-invasive diagnostic procedure used to
measure the full horizontal and vertical range of what a person can see, including peripheral
(side) vision. This test helps detect blind spots, vision loss, or abnormalities in the visual
pathways that may indicate eye or neurological conditions.
Perimetry plays a crucial role in diagnosing and managing conditions such as glaucoma, optic
nerve damage, brain tumors, stroke-related vision loss, and retinal diseases. It’s often a
standard component of a comprehensive eye exam when there’s suspicion of visual field defects.

Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms That May Indicate a Need for Perimetry:
Difficulty seeing to the side while driving or walking
Bumping into objects that are not seen in peripheral vision
Sudden or gradual vision loss in part of your field of view
Headaches or neurological symptoms with visual changes
Tunnel vision or patchy vision
Common Causes for Visual Field Loss:
Glaucoma – Gradual optic nerve damage leading to peripheral vision loss
Stroke or brain injury – Can cause hemianopia or quadrantanopia (loss of half or
quarter of visual field)
Optic neuritis – Inflammation of the optic nerve
Pituitary tumors – Can press on the optic chiasm causing visual field defects
Retinal diseases – Like retinitis pigmentosa or diabetic retinopathy
Neurological conditions – Including multiple sclerosis
Diagnosis and Tests
Perimetry is a specialized eye test typically performed in an eye clinic or optometry office. There
are various techniques, but most involve a machine that presents lights or patterns while the
patient signals when they see them.
Common Types of Visual Field Tests:
Automated Static Perimetry (e.g., Humphrey Field Analyzer) – Flashes of light
appear in various areas, and the patient presses a button when seen.
Kinetic Perimetry (e.g., Goldmann Perimetry) – Uses moving light targets to map
visual fields manually.
Confrontation Visual Field Test – A basic in-office screening done by the doctor
without a machine.
Frequency Doubling Technology (FDT) Perimetry – Quick screening test often used
for early glaucoma detection.
What Happens During the Test?
You’ll place your chin on a rest and look straight ahead at a central target.
You’ll be instructed to press a button whenever you detect a light or stimulus in your side
vision.
The process takes about 15 to 30 minutes per eye, depending on the test type.

Management and Treatment
Perimetry is not a treatment, but it guides diagnosis and treatment decisions by mapping how
much vision is affected.
Based on Results:
Glaucoma patients may need medications, laser treatment, or surgery to lower
intraocular pressure.
If vision loss is due to a neurological cause, further imaging (like MRI or CT scan) may
be necessary.
For stroke or tumor-related field loss, referral to a neurologist or neurosurgeon may be
required.
Ongoing monitoring is important—visual field testing is repeated at regular intervals to
assess disease progression.
Perimetry Types & Surgical Relevance
Types of Perimetry Tests:
1. Static Automated Perimetry: Most commonly used in glaucoma detection and
monitoring.
2. Kinetic Perimetry: Effective for evaluating peripheral field loss due to neurological
issues.
3. FDT (Frequency Doubling Technology): Often used in quick screenings.
4. Short-Wavelength Automated Perimetry (SWAP): Sensitive to early glaucomatous
changes.
Surgical Relevance:
Visual field testing is critical before and after neurosurgical procedures, especially
when tumors compress the visual pathway.
It's also used post-surgically in glaucoma to monitor how effective surgery was in
preserving or improving vision.
Prevention
While perimetry doesn’t prevent eye disease, it aids in early detection, which is vital for
preserving vision.

Preventive Actions:
Regular eye exams, especially if you’re over 40 or at risk for glaucoma
Control systemic conditions like high blood pressure, diabetes, and high cholesterol
Avoid smoking—a risk factor for optic nerve damage
Protect your head and eyes from trauma
Recognize warning signs early, such as vision changes or side vision loss
Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis depends on the underlying condition causing the visual field loss.
In glaucoma, early detection through perimetry allows treatment to slow progression.
Neurological vision loss may improve with treatment or rehabilitation, but not always.
Monitoring over time is essential—field tests can detect even small changes in vision,
helping adapt treatment accordingly.
With proper care and monitoring, many patients can maintain functional vision and quality of
life.
Living With Visual Field Loss
If perimetry reveals vision loss:
Work with your eye doctor to adjust your treatment as needed.
Use low vision aids or visual field expansion techniques, such as prism glasses.
Stay cautious in daily activities—especially driving, cooking, or crossing streets.
Consider vision therapy or rehabilitation to adapt to the changes.
Maintain regular perimetry testing to track disease and modify care as needed.
Additional Common Questions About Perimetry
Is perimetry painful?
Not at all. It’s completely non-invasive and painless.
Can perimetry detect all vision problems?
No. It specifically evaluates field of vision, not visual clarity or focus.

How often should I get a visual field test?
That depends on your condition. Glaucoma patients may need it every 3–12 months, while
others may need it less frequently.
Can you fail a perimetry test?
You can't "fail" in the usual sense, but inaccurate responses or distractions can affect results,
which may require retesting.
Does it work for children?
Yes, though younger children may find it challenging to concentrate for the test. Pediatric-
friendly options are available.
FAQs for Perimetry
Q1. What conditions can perimetry help diagnose?
A: Glaucoma, optic nerve damage, brain tumors, stroke, multiple sclerosis, and some retinal
disorders.
Q2. Is perimetry necessary for LASIK surgery?
A: Not typically, unless there are concerns about the optic nerve or preexisting field loss.
Q3. Can perimetry detect macular degeneration?
A: It’s not ideal for macular problems; OCT or other retinal imaging is more useful. However, it
may pick up central scotomas in advanced cases.
Q4. What do the colors and graphs in a visual field report mean?
A: Dark areas usually indicate areas of vision loss. Your doctor will interpret the pattern to
determine cause and severity.
Q5. Is visual field loss reversible?
A: That depends on the cause. Some neurological vision losses may recover, while
glaucomatous damage is typically permanent, though manageable.
