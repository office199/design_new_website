Topography (Corneal Topography)
Corneal topography is a non-invasive imaging technique used to map the curvature and shape
of the cornea, the transparent front surface of the eye. The resulting “topographic map” helps
eye care professionals evaluate the cornea's surface for irregularities, which is essential for
diagnosing and managing a variety of vision and corneal disorders.
Topography is widely used in the assessment of refractive surgery candidates (like LASIK),
detection of corneal diseases, and fitting of specialized contact lenses.
Symptoms and Causes
While corneal topography itself is a diagnostic test rather than a treatment, it is used when
patients present with symptoms such as:
Symptoms That May Lead to a Corneal Topography Test:
Blurry or distorted vision
Sudden changes in prescription

Difficulty wearing contact lenses
Frequent glare or halos around lights
Vision fluctuations during the day
Conditions Requiring Corneal Topography:
Keratoconus – progressive thinning and bulging of the cornea
Post-refractive surgery complications
Astigmatism evaluation
Corneal scarring from infection or trauma
Pellucid marginal degeneration
Irregular corneas due to injury or surgery
Diagnosis and Tests
Corneal topography is a painless, quick, and highly detailed diagnostic tool. It creates a 3D
map of the corneal surface using reflected light or scanning technology.
How It Works:
The patient places their chin and forehead on a support.
A series of concentric light rings (Placido disc) or scanning laser beams reflect off the
cornea.
Specialized software analyzes the data to create a topographical map showing
curvatures, elevations, and irregularities.
Other Diagnostic Tools Used Alongside:
Slit-lamp exam to visually inspect the cornea
Pachymetry to measure corneal thickness
OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography) for deeper cross-sectional imaging
Refraction tests to evaluate the impact on vision
Management and Treatment
Topography is a diagnostic tool, but it plays a crucial role in managing and planning treatment
for various conditions:
In keratoconus, topography tracks the disease’s progression and helps determine the
need for treatments like corneal cross-linking.

For refractive surgery, it ensures the cornea is suitable and safe for procedures like
LASIK or PRK.
Helps create custom contact lenses, especially rigid gas-permeable or scleral lenses for
irregular corneas.
Post-operative topography is used to monitor healing and detect complications early.
Topography Types & Surgical Relevance
Types of Corneal Topography:
1. Placido-Based Systems – Use reflected light rings to assess the anterior corneal surface
curvature.
2. Scheimpflug Imaging (e.g., Pentacam) – Provides 3D cross-sectional imaging of the
entire anterior segment.
3. Scanning Slit Systems (Orbscan) – Combines Placido and slit-scan technology to
measure both anterior and posterior corneal surfaces.
Surgical Applications:
Pre-LASIK/PRK screening to rule out conditions like keratoconus.
Cataract surgery planning, especially for toric intraocular lenses.
Post-corneal transplant monitoring.
Evaluating corneal implants or INTACS for stability and positioning.
Prevention
While corneal topography doesn’t prevent eye diseases, it plays a key role in early detection,
allowing for preventive interventions such as:
Avoiding unsuitable refractive surgeries in patients with abnormal corneal shapes.
Starting corneal cross-linking early in keratoconus to halt progression.
Ensuring safe contact lens wear by identifying early signs of corneal warpage or
thinning.
Outlook / Prognosis
The prognosis depends on the underlying eye condition, but with accurate topographic mapping:
Patients with keratoconus can receive timely treatment before vision worsens.

Surgical candidates are better screened, improving outcomes and reducing
complications.
Post-operative monitoring becomes more precise, ensuring a quicker and safer

Topography contributes significantly to personalized, data-driven eye care, ensuring safer

Living With Corneal Irregularities
If you’re undergoing corneal topography as part of ongoing eye care:
Follow-up regularly if you have a diagnosed corneal condition like keratoconus or had
eye surgery.
If your vision fluctuates or worsens, repeat topography can detect changes early.
Stick to contact lens guidelines, especially for hard or specialty lenses shaped based on
topography.
Modern topography helps tailor treatment plans to individual needs, making living with corneal
issues more manageable.
Additional Common Questions About Corneal Topography
Is corneal topography painful?
No. It is a completely non-contact, painless procedure.
How long does the test take?
Typically, it takes 5–10 minutes.
Is it safe for children?
Yes. It’s safe for all ages and is often used in pediatric patients with suspected corneal disorders.
Will I need to remove contact lenses before the test?
Yes. Soft lenses should be removed at least 24 hours before the test, and rigid lenses several
days prior.
Can corneal topography detect glaucoma?
No, but it can be used alongside other tests to assess optic nerve and corneal thickness, which
are relevant to glaucoma diagnosis.

FAQs for Corneal Topography
Q1. What is the difference between topography and tomography?
A: Topography measures the corneal surface curvature; tomography provides detailed 3D
imaging including the cornea’s inner layers.
Q2. Can I have LASIK if my topography is abnormal?
A: Not usually. Abnormal topography may indicate conditions like keratoconus, making LASIK
unsafe.
Q3. Is corneal topography covered by insurance?
A: It may be covered when deemed medically necessary, such as for keratoconus diagnosis or
surgical planning. Elective uses (e.g., LASIK evaluations) may not be covered.
Q4. How often should corneal topography be done?
A: As recommended by your eye doctor—every 6 to 12 months if monitoring a corneal
condition, or before/after surgery.
Q5. Can it help diagnose dry eye?
A: Not directly, but it can detect corneal irregularities and tear film issues that may contribute to
dry eye symptoms.
