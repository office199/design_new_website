Thyroid Eye Disease (Graves’ Eye Disease) – Complete Guide
Overview
Thyroid Eye Disease (TED), also referred to as Graves’ Eye Disease, is an autoimmune
condition where the body’s immune system mistakenly attacks tissues around the eyes. Most
commonly linked to Graves’ disease, an overactive thyroid disorder (hyperthyroidism), TED
can also occur in people with normal or underactive thyroid function. The inflammation and
tissue buildup behind the eye can cause visible changes in appearance and impair vision, making
early detection and care essential.
Symptoms and Causes
Common Symptoms
Symptoms vary in severity and progression but may include:
Eye bulging (proptosis)
Swelling around the eyes
Redness and inflammation
Excessive tearing or dry eyes
Light sensitivity (photophobia)
Double vision (diplopia)
Eye pain or discomfort
Restricted eye movement

Eyelid retraction or puffiness
Causes
TED is primarily an autoimmune response. Factors that may trigger or worsen it include:
Autoimmune Dysfunction: The immune system targets the tissues behind the eyes.
Thyroid Disorders: Especially Graves’ disease, but it may also occur in hypothyroid or
euthyroid patients.
Genetics: A family history of autoimmune diseases can increase risk.
Smoking: A well-established risk factor; it also worsens symptoms.
Gender and Age: More common in women, especially between 30–60 years old.
Diagnosis and Tests
To diagnose TED, a healthcare provider will review symptoms, perform an eye exam, and may
order several tests:
Thyroid Function Tests: Blood tests for TSH, T3, and T4 hormone levels.
Eye Examination: Assessing eye movement, visual acuity, and bulging.
Orbital Imaging: CT or MRI scans help detect muscle and fat tissue swelling behind the
eyes.
Exophthalmometry: Measures the degree of eye protrusion.
Ultrasound: May be used to assess soft tissue swelling and eye structure.
Management and Treatment
The approach to managing TED depends on the severity of symptoms and the phase of the
disease (active or inactive):
Non-Surgical Treatments
Corticosteroids: Reduce inflammation and swelling during the active phase.
Teprotumumab: A newer, FDA-approved biologic specifically for TED.
Immunosuppressive Therapies: Help control autoimmune responses.
Lubricating Eye Drops: For dryness and irritation.
Prism Glasses: Help manage double vision.
Radiation Therapy: Used in some cases to target orbital inflammation.

Types of Thyroid Eye Disease & Surgery
Types of TED
1. Mild TED: Minor symptoms like dry eyes or slight puffiness.
2. Moderate TED: Involves double vision, noticeable bulging, or eyelid retraction.
3. Severe TED: May include vision loss, corneal exposure, or optic nerve compression.
Surgical Options
Surgery is typically reserved for patients whose symptoms persist after the active phase or
impact vision or appearance severely.
1. Orbital Decompression Surgery: Removes bone or fat to reduce pressure behind the
eyes.
2. Strabismus Surgery: Corrects misaligned eyes to relieve double vision.
3. Eyelid Surgery: Repairs retracted or drooping eyelids to improve appearance and eye
protection.
Prevention
While TED cannot always be prevented, proactive measures can lower the risk or slow its
progression:
Quit Smoking: Smoking greatly increases the risk and severity.
Control Thyroid Function: Regular monitoring and treatment for thyroid disorders.
Protect the Eyes: Use sunglasses and lubricating drops.
Regular Screenings: Especially if you have Graves’ disease or a family history of
thyroid issues.
Outlook / Prognosis

The prognosis for TED varies depending on the severity and how early it is treated:
Mild cases often resolve on their own or with minimal intervention.
Moderate to severe cases require medical or surgical treatment to prevent lasting
damage.
Vision-threatening TED is rare but requires urgent care.
With proper management, most people regain normal or near-normal function and appearance.
Living With Thyroid Eye Disease
Living with TED means adjusting to physical and emotional changes. Some tips for coping
include:
Follow-up Appointments: Consistent monitoring with an endocrinologist and
ophthalmologist.
Emotional Support: Counseling or support groups can help manage anxiety or body
image concerns.
Practical Adjustments: Use humidifiers, wear sunglasses, and practice good eye
hygiene.
Healthy Lifestyle: Balanced diet, stress management, and avoiding triggers.
Additional Common Questions
Can I get TED if I don’t have Graves’ disease?
Yes. TED can occur in euthyroid (normal thyroid) or hypothyroid patients, although it is less
common.
How long does TED last?
TED typically has an active phase lasting 6–24 months, followed by an inactive phase where
inflammation subsides.
Does TED recur?
While TED usually follows a single course, flare-ups can happen, especially if thyroid function
isn’t well-managed.

FAQs
Q: Is Thyroid Eye Disease permanent?
A: Not always. Some symptoms improve with treatment, while others like eye bulging may
require surgery to correct.
Q: Is TED contagious?
A: No, it’s an autoimmune condition and not spread from person to person.
Q: Can TED be treated naturally?
A: While lifestyle changes help, medical treatment is often necessary, especially for moderate to
severe cases.
Q: Who treats TED?
A: Typically, a multidisciplinary team including an endocrinologist, ophthalmologist, and
sometimes an oculoplastic surgeon.
Q: Can TED cause blindness?
A: In rare, severe cases, optic nerve compression can threaten vision. Early treatment minimizes
this risk.
