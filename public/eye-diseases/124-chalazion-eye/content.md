Chalazion Eye
A chalazion is a common, benign lump that forms on the eyelid due to blockage and
inflammation of the Meibomian glands—oil-producing glands located within the eyelids. Unlike
a stye, which is an acute infection, a chalazion is usually a chronic, non-infectious swelling
caused by gland obstruction and lipid buildup. Chalazia can cause discomfort, eyelid swelling,
and sometimes blurred vision if large enough to press on the eye.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms
Firm, painless lump on the eyelid, usually away from the eyelid margin
Mild tenderness or discomfort if inflamed
Swelling and redness of the eyelid
Blurred or distorted vision in severe cases due to pressure on the eyeball
Sensation of heaviness or fullness in the eyelid

<!-- Page 671 -->
Occasional tearing or eye irritation
Causes
Blockage of Meibomian glands leading to oil retention
Chronic inflammation of eyelid margins (blepharitis)
Poor eyelid hygiene
Previous stye or eyelid infection
Skin conditions such as rosacea or seborrheic dermatitis
Hormonal changes or immune system dysfunction
Diagnosis and Tests
Diagnosis of a chalazion is usually clinical and based on:
Physical examination of the eyelid lump
Differentiation from stye or other eyelid cysts and tumors
Slit-lamp examination to assess the extent and location
Rarely, biopsy if the lesion appears atypical or persists despite treatment
Management and Treatment
Conservative Treatment
Warm compresses applied 3-4 times daily for 10-15 minutes to soften the blocked gland
Gentle eyelid massage to encourage drainage
Maintaining good eyelid hygiene, including cleaning eyelid margins
Avoid squeezing or attempting to pop the lump
Use of lubricating eye drops to relieve irritation
Medical Treatment
Topical antibiotic ointments or steroid eye drops if inflammation is significant
Oral antibiotics in cases of secondary infection or persistent inflammation
Surgical Treatment
Incision and curettage under local anesthesia for chalazia that do not resolve after 4-6
weeks
Minor outpatient procedure where the lump is drained through a small incision
Steroid injection into the lesion as a less invasive alternative for selected cases

Types
Internal Chalazion: Located inside the eyelid, arising from blockage of the Meibomian
glands.
External Chalazion: Rare; develops near the eyelid margin from Zeis or Moll gland
blockage.
Surgery
Performed when conservative treatment fails or the chalazion is large and affects vision
Safe and effective with minimal risk when done by an ophthalmologist
Postoperative care includes antibiotic ointment and avoiding eye rubbing
Prevention
Practice regular eyelid hygiene to prevent gland blockage
Remove eye makeup thoroughly before sleeping
Manage underlying skin or eyelid conditions like blepharitis
Avoid rubbing eyes with dirty hands
Use warm compresses regularly if prone to chalazia
Outlook / Prognosis
Most chalazia resolve within weeks to months with conservative care
Surgical intervention has high success rates with minimal complications
Recurrence can occur, especially without proper eyelid care
Chalazion is not cancerous but persistent lumps should be evaluated
Living With
Incorporate daily eyelid hygiene into your routine
Avoid triggers such as eye rubbing and harsh cosmetics
Monitor for new lumps or changes in existing ones
Attend follow-up appointments if treated surgically or with medications
Communicate any vision changes or increasing discomfort to your eye doctor
Additional Common Questions
Is a chalazion contagious?
No, it is caused by gland blockage and not by infection that spreads person-to-person.

<!-- Page 673 -->
Can a chalazion turn into a stye?
A chalazion is generally non-infectious but can become inflamed or infected, mimicking
a stye.
How long does a chalazion last?
Usually several weeks to a few months without treatment; faster with intervention.
Can chalazion cause vision loss?
Vision loss is rare; large chalazia may blur vision temporarily due to pressure.
Are there risks to surgery?
Surgery risks are minimal but can include infection, bleeding, or eyelid scarring.
FAQs
Q1: How do I know if the lump on my eyelid is a chalazion or a stye?
A: Chalazion lumps are usually painless and away from the eyelid edge; styes are painful and
near the margin.
Q2: Can I treat a chalazion at home?
A: Warm compresses and eyelid hygiene often help, but persistent lumps need medical
evaluation.
Q3: Should I use antibiotics for a chalazion?
A: Only if prescribed, typically when inflammation or infection is present.
Q4: Can chalazion recur?
A: Yes, especially if underlying causes are not addressed.
Q5: When should I see a doctor about a chalazion?
A: If the lump persists beyond a few weeks, grows, becomes painful, or affects vision.
Chalazion eye lumps are common and generally benign conditions that respond well to simple
care or minor procedures. Timely treatment and preventive measures ensure comfort and eye
health.
