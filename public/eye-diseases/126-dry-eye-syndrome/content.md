Dry Eye Syndrome
Dry Eye Syndrome, also known as keratoconjunctivitis sicca, is a common condition where the
eyes don’t produce enough tears or the quality of tears is poor. Tears are essential for
maintaining the health of the front surface of the eye and for clear vision. When tear production
is disrupted, it can lead to irritation, inflammation, and discomfort. Dry eye can be temporary or
chronic, and it ranges in severity from mild discomfort to significant vision impairment if left
untreated.
Symptoms and Causes
Symptoms
Stinging, burning, or scratchy sensation in the eyes
Redness and irritation
Blurred vision or eye fatigue
Sensitivity to light (photophobia)
Feeling like something is in the eye (foreign body sensation)
Watery eyes (a paradoxical response to dryness)

<!-- Page 678 -->
Discomfort when wearing contact lenses
Difficulty driving at night or reading for extended periods
Causes
Aging: Tear production decreases naturally with age
Hormonal changes: Particularly during menopause
Medications: Antihistamines, antidepressants, and blood pressure medications
Medical conditions: Diabetes, thyroid disorders, rheumatoid arthritis, lupus
Environmental factors: Wind, smoke, dry climates, or prolonged screen time
Eye surgeries: LASIK and cataract surgeries can disrupt tear production
Contact lens wear: May contribute to tear film instability
Meibomian gland dysfunction: Blocks oil glands, reducing tear quality
Diagnosis and Tests
Diagnosis begins with a detailed eye examination. Key tests include:
Schirmer’s test: Measures tear production using filter paper strips
Tear breakup time (TBUT): Assesses tear film stability
Ocular surface staining: Dyes like fluorescein or lissamine green highlight damage to
the eye surface
Meibography: Imaging of the oil-producing glands in the eyelids
Tear osmolarity test: Evaluates salt concentration in tears
Patient questionnaires: Help gauge the frequency and severity of symptoms
Management and Treatment
Conservative Treatment
Artificial tears: Over-the-counter lubricating drops for mild cases
Warm compresses: To unblock meibomian glands
Lid hygiene: Cleansing eyelids to reduce inflammation and bacterial load
Environmental adjustments: Using humidifiers, avoiding fans, taking screen breaks
Medical Treatment
Prescription eye drops:
o
Cyclosporine (Restasis) or lifitegrast (Xiidra) to reduce inflammation
Punctal plugs: Tiny devices inserted into tear ducts to slow drainage
Steroid eye drops: For short-term inflammation control

Dry Eye Syndrome Types & Surgery
Types
Aqueous-deficient dry eye: Caused by insufficient tear production
Evaporative dry eye: Caused by excessive tear evaporation due to poor tear film
stability
Mixed dry eye: A combination of both types
Surgical Options
Punctal cautery: Permanent closure of tear drainage ducts
Amniotic membrane grafts: For severe corneal damage due to dryness
Tarsorrhaphy: Surgical narrowing of the eyelids in extreme cases
Prevention
Blink regularly, especially when using digital screens
Maintain a clean environment, free of smoke and pollutants
Use protective eyewear in windy or dusty areas
Stay hydrated and eat a diet rich in omega-3 fatty acids
Avoid prolonged use of contact lenses
Choose makeup and skincare products labeled "ophthalmologist-tested" or "non-
irritating"
Outlook / Prognosis
Most cases can be managed effectively with proper treatment
Chronic dry eye may require ongoing care but typically does not lead to vision loss
Severe cases left untreated can lead to corneal ulcers or scarring
Early intervention significantly improves long-term comfort and eye health
Living With
Make eye care part of your daily routine
Carry artificial tears, especially in dry environments
Schedule regular follow-ups with your eye care provider
Monitor and report any worsening of symptoms
Be mindful of screen time and take frequent breaks

Additional Common Questions
Is dry eye permanent?
It can be chronic, but symptoms are manageable with proper care.
Can dry eyes damage vision?
Yes, if severe and untreated, it can lead to corneal damage and blurred vision.
Do dry eyes mean I have an autoimmune disease?
Not necessarily, but dry eye can be a symptom of autoimmune conditions like Sjögren’s
syndrome.
Can allergies cause dry eyes?
Yes, especially if combined with antihistamine use, which can reduce tear production.
Can drinking more water help?
Staying hydrated may help, but it’s not a cure. Comprehensive treatment is typically
needed.
FAQs
Q1. What is the best eye drop for dry eye?
A: It varies. Preservative-free artificial tears are recommended for frequent use. Prescription
drops like Restasis or Xiidra may be needed for chronic cases.
Q2. How often should I use artificial tears?
A: As needed—some people may require them several times a day.
Q3. Are there natural remedies for dry eye?
A: Omega-3 supplements, warm compresses, and proper eyelid hygiene are helpful home
remedies.
Q4. Can I wear contact lenses if I have dry eye?
A: It's possible, but specialty lenses like scleral lenses or daily disposables may be more
comfortable.
Q5. Can dry eye be cured?
A: There's no definitive cure, but it can be effectively controlled with long-term management.
Conclusion:
Dry Eye Syndrome is a manageable condition that can significantly impact quality of life if
ignored. With the right treatment plan—ranging from simple lifestyle changes to advanced
medical therapies—most individuals can enjoy lasting relief and maintain healthy vision.
